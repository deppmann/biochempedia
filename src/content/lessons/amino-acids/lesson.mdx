summary: >-
  Twenty amino acids share one backbone, so what makes them different — and why
  can a single swap among them turn a healthy protein into a disease? The answer
  is the side chain. One idea drives the whole lesson: the R group sets the
  chemistry, and charge, pKa, the zwitterion, and the titration curve all fall out
  of it. It ends on the Glu→Val swap behind sickle cell.

  - "Predict whether a group is protonated or deprotonated from pH and pKa, and explain why an amino acid is a zwitterion at pH 7.4."
  - "Read pKa and pI off a titration curve, and compute pI as the average of the two pKa values bracketing the net-zero form."
  - "Classify a side chain by R-group property and predict the consequence of swapping one amino acid for another (e.g., Glu→Val in sickle cell)."

      his wife to bring him paper and a slide rule, drew a polypeptide chain on an
      ordinary sheet, and folded it into a right-handed coil that came around every
      3.6 residues — the alpha helix. He had cracked it by throwing out the
      assumption everyone else clung to: that the coil had to close after a whole
      number of amino acids. The following year, working with Harvey Itano, he ran
      sickle-cell and healthy hemoglobin through electrophoresis and watched them
      drift at different speeds — the first time anyone pinned a specific human

      disease," and the phrase reframed medicine: a single changed amino acid could
      be the whole catastrophe. Vernon Ingram would later locate the exact lesion —
      one glutamate swapped for a valine at the sixth position of the beta chain —
      but the conceptual leap was Pauling's. He holds two unshared Nobel Prizes,
      Chemistry and Peace, and proposed a triple helix for DNA that was wrong in
      nearly every particular, partly because the government had pulled his passport
      and he never saw the photographs at King's College.

techniques:
  - name: "Titration to find pKa and pI"
    blurb: >-
      Add base in measured steps and track pH. Each plateau is a buffering region
      centered on a pKa; the steep jumps mark the equivalence points. The
      half-equivalence point reads the pKa directly, and pI sits at the pH where net
      charge is zero — the two numbers that fingerprint an amino acid's acid-base
      behavior.
  - name: "Assigning chirality (the CORN rule)"
    blurb: >-
      View the alpha-carbon with the H pointing toward you and trace CO → R → N
      around the carbon. Clockwise reads L, counterclockwise reads D. Commit to one
      camera angle: look from the opposite side and the handedness flips. Proteins
      use only L.
  - name: "Detecting amino acids (ninhydrin and chromatography)"
    blurb: >-
      Ninhydrin reacts with free alpha-amino groups to produce a purple color
      (yellow for proline), so a separated mixture lights up spot by spot. Paper or
      ion-exchange chromatography sorts amino acids by charge and hydrophobicity —
      the same two side-chain properties this lesson is built on.

practiceQuestions:
  - stem: "A protein biochemist is designing a peptide intended to span the hydrophobic interior of a lipid bilayer. Based on the four R-group categories in this chapter, which set of amino acids would be MOST appropriate to enrich in the membrane-spanning segment?"
    choices:
      - "Leucine, Isoleucine, and Valine"
      - "Aspartate, Glutamate, and Lysine"
      - "Serine, Threonine, and Tyrosine"
      - "Arginine, Histidine, and Glutamine"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Leu, Ile, and Val are branched aliphatic, hydrophobic (nonpolar) residues
      whose hydrocarbon side chains are stable buried in the lipid interior of a
      membrane. Asp/Glu/Lys are charged and hydrophilic; Ser/Thr/Tyr are
      polar-neutral (the hydroxyl trio); Arg/His are charged and Gln is a polar
      amide — all of them prefer water, not the bilayer core.
  - stem: "Glycine is the only achiral amino acid. What structural feature accounts for this?"
    choices:
      - "Its R group is a single hydrogen, so the alpha-carbon bears two identical hydrogen substituents"
      - "Its R group forms a covalent ring with the backbone nitrogen, locking the configuration"
      - "Its side chain contains a sulfur atom that prevents mirror-image formation"
      - "Its carboxylate group is replaced by an amide, eliminating one stereocenter"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Chirality requires four different groups on the alpha-carbon. For glycine, R =
      H, so the carbon carries two hydrogens plus the amino and carboxylate groups;
      with two identical substituents the molecule is superimposable on its mirror
      image and therefore achiral. Choice 2 describes proline's cyclic side chain
      (proline is still chiral); the sulfur and amide claims are simply false.
  - stem: "A student titrates glycine and identifies pKa₁ = 2.34 (alpha-carboxyl) and pKa₂ = 9.60 (alpha-amino). Which statement correctly describes the predominant form and how to obtain the isoelectric point (pI)?"
    choices:
      - "The net-zero zwitterion predominates between the two pKa values; pI = (2.34 + 9.60)/2 = 5.97"
      - "The fully protonated +1 form predominates between the two pKa values; pI = 2.34"
      - "The net-zero form predominates only above pKa₂; pI = 9.60"
      - "The pI equals the higher pKa because the amino group deprotonates last; pI = 9.60"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Between pKa₁ and pKa₂ the carboxyl is deprotonated (−COO⁻) and the amino group
      is still protonated (−NH₃⁺), giving the net-zero zwitterion. The pI is the
      average of the two pKa values that bracket this neutral form: (2.34 + 9.60)/2 =
      5.97. The +1 form predominates only below pKa₁; above pKa₂ the molecule is net
      −1; and pI is the average of the flanking pKa values, not simply the higher one.
  - stem: "Histidine has three ionizable groups: alpha-carboxyl (pKa₁ = 1.82), the imidazole side chain (pKa₂ = 6.00), and alpha-amino (pKa₃ = 9.17). At physiological pH (7.4), what is the net charge on free histidine, and why?"
    choices:
      - "0 — the carboxyl is deprotonated (−1), the imidazole is mostly deprotonated/neutral (0), and the alpha-amino is protonated (+1)"
      - "+1 — the imidazole stays protonated and adds a positive charge at pH 7.4"
      - "−1 — both the carboxyl and the imidazole carry negative charges at pH 7.4"
      - "+2 — both the imidazole and the alpha-amino are protonated at pH 7.4"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Apply pH > pKa → deprotonated. At pH 7.4: carboxyl (pKa 1.82) is deprotonated,
      −1; imidazole (pKa 6.00) is below pH 7.4 so mostly neutral, 0; alpha-amino (pKa
      9.17) is above pH 7.4 so still protonated, +1. Sum = 0. The imidazole, more than
      a full unit below the pH, is predominantly neutral — not still positive.
  - stem: "An enzyme uses a single ionizable side chain as a proton-shuttle group, and the designer wants it to buffer most effectively near physiological pH (~7.4) while toggling between charged and neutral states. Which amino acid fits, and why?"
    choices:
      - "Histidine — a group buffers best when the ambient pH is near its pKa, and the imidazole pKa (~6.0) is the closest of all side chains to physiological pH"
      - "Aspartate — its carboxyl pKa (~4) makes it the strongest acid and therefore the best buffer at pH 7.4"
      - "Lysine — its high side-chain pKa (~10.5) keeps it fully protonated and locked in one state at pH 7.4"
      - "Glycine — its small size lets it move protons quickly regardless of pKa"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      A group buffers best when the solution pH is within about ±1 unit of its pKa.
      Histidine's imidazole pKa (~6.0) is the side-chain pKa closest to physiological
      pH, which is why it can both buffer and toggle between charged and neutral — the
      "Swiss Army knife of catalysis." Aspartate buffers near pH 4; lysine stays fully
      protonated and cannot toggle near 7.4; glycine's size has nothing to do with
      buffering, which depends on pKa.

mcatQuestions:
  - stem: "An ionizable group with a pKa of 4.34 sits in a solution buffered at pH 6.34. By the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation, what is the approximate ratio of deprotonated to protonated form, [A⁻]/[HA]?"
    choices:
      - "100 : 1"
      - "1 : 100"
      - "10 : 1"
      - "1 : 1"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]), so log([A⁻]/[HA]) = 6.34 − 4.34 = 2.0 and [A⁻]/[HA] =
      10² = 100:1. Because pH is above pKa, the deprotonated form dominates (ruling out
      1:100). A ratio of 10:1 would need a difference of only 1.0; 1:1 would need pH =
      pKa.
  - stem: "A peptide chemist looks down the H→alpha-carbon bond (hydrogen toward her eye) and traces carboxyl (CO) → side chain (R) → amino (N). The path runs clockwise. By the CORN rule, what is the configuration, and is it the form found in proteins?"
    choices:
      - "L-configuration, and yes — it is the form incorporated into proteins"
      - "D-configuration, and yes — it is the form incorporated into proteins"
      - "L-configuration, but no — only D-amino acids are used in proteins"
      - "D-configuration, and no — it would not be incorporated into proteins"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Viewing from the H side and tracing CO → R → N, a clockwise path is L and
      counterclockwise is D. Proteins use only L-amino acids, so this one is both L
      and protein-appropriate. The distractors either reverse the clockwise = L
      assignment or falsely claim proteins use D.
  - stem: "Sickle-cell disease arises when glutamate at position 6 of beta-globin (E6) is replaced by valine. A hypothetical variant instead substitutes that same surface glutamate with lysine. Which variant best reproduces the sticky-patch aggregation of sickle cell, and on what basis?"
    choices:
      - "The Glu→Val variant — it converts a charged/hydrophilic surface residue into a hydrophobic one, creating a sticky patch that drives aggregation"
      - "The Glu→Lys variant — Lys is larger than Glu, and steric bulk alone causes aggregation"
      - "The Glu→Lys variant — replacing a negative with a positive charge removes all surface charge and exposes the core"
      - "Both equally — any substitution at position 6 produces identical aggregation regardless of the new residue"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      The pathology is a property switch: a charged, hydrophilic surface Glu becomes a
      hydrophobic Val, and the new greasy patch drives deoxyhemoglobin to polymerize.
      Glu→Lys swaps one charged/hydrophilic residue (negative) for another (positive),
      so it stays hydrophilic and would not create the same hydrophobic patch. Size
      does not drive the aggregation, a lysine still carries charge, and the outcome
      depends specifically on the property of the new residue.

**All twenty amino acids are built on one backbone.** Same alpha-carbon, same
carboxyl group, the same amino nitrogen, the same lone hydrogen. The only thing
that differs is one attachment — the side chain, the R group. And yet swapping one
amino acid for another at a single position in one protein is enough to cause
sickle cell disease.

So here is the question this lesson turns on: if the scaffold is identical, how can
one side chain decide so much? The answer runs in a straight line — structure sets
charge, charge sets behavior — and it is one of the most useful chains of reasoning
in biochemistry.

## The idea: the backbone is shared, the side chain is everything

Every amino acid is a central carbon — the **alpha-carbon** — wearing four groups:
an amino group (−NH₂), a carboxyl group (−COOH), a hydrogen, and a distinctive **R
group**. Three of those four are the same across the set, with one twist: proline's
side chain loops back onto its own backbone nitrogen, so its amino group is a
secondary amine. The R group is the variable, and it carries all the personality:
its size, its charge, whether it loves or flees water, whether it can react.

That four-groups-on-one-carbon arrangement has a consequence. When all four are
different, the carbon is **chiral** — it has two non-superimposable mirror images,
L and D, like your two hands. Proteins are built almost exclusively from the **L
form**. The lone exception is **glycine**, whose R group is just another hydrogen;
with two identical groups, its alpha-carbon is no longer chiral. Glycine is the
only achiral amino acid, and that simplicity is exactly why it shows up at the tight
turns where a protein needs to fold back on itself.

Chirality has a body count. Thalidomide was sold in the 1950s as a sedative; one
mirror-image form calmed morning sickness, the other caused severe birth defects in
roughly ten thousand children. Same atoms, opposite hand, opposite outcome.

Sort the R groups by what they do and four families appear: **hydrophobic** (greasy
hydrocarbon and aromatic chains that bury themselves in a protein's core, away from
water), **polar but neutral** (the hydroxyls of Ser, Thr, Tyr; the amides of Asn,
Gln; the reactive thiol of Cys), **positively charged** (Lys, Arg, His), and
**negatively charged** (Asp, Glu). Which family a residue belongs to predicts where
it sits and what it does. Ask where you would put an amino acid in a
membrane-spanning helix, surrounded by lipid, and the answer is forced: hydrophobic.

## Charge depends on pH: the zwitterion and the one rule

Here is where the side chain starts doing chemistry. At the pH inside your body —
about **7.4** — an amino acid is not neutral and not singly charged. Its carboxyl
group has given up a proton to become −COO⁻, and its amino group has grabbed one to
become −NH₃⁺. It carries a positive and a negative charge at the same time. That
double-charged, net-neutral form is a **zwitterion**.

What decides whether any given group holds its proton? One rule, and you will use it
for the rest of biochemistry:

> **pH > pKa → deprotonated. pH < pKa → protonated.**

The pKa is the pH at which a group is half-protonated. Sit above it and the group has
mostly let go of its proton; sit below it and the group mostly holds on. Anchor
everything to physiological pH and you can read any amino acid's charge off its pKa
values.

Seven side chains are ionizable — they can gain or lose a proton depending on the
surrounding pH: **Asp, Glu, His, Cys, Tyr, Lys, and Arg**. These are the residues
that form salt bridges, bind metals, and do acid-base catalysis. The standout is
**histidine**. Its imidazole side chain has a pKa near 6, which sits closer to
physiological pH than any other side chain. That one fact lets histidine flip between
charged and neutral with a tiny shift in its environment — exactly what an enzyme
needs from a residue that has to hand a proton off and take one back. It is the
workhorse of acid-base catalysis, and the reason histidine turns up in active site
after active site.

## Reading the curve: titration, pKa, and pI

<AminoTitrator />

Add base to an amino acid drop by drop, track the pH, and you get a **titration
curve**: a rising staircase with a flat plateau at each pKa and a steep jump between
them. Each plateau is a **buffering region**, where added base barely moves the pH
because a group is busy giving up its proton. The midpoint of a plateau — the
**half-equivalence point** — reads the pKa straight off the y-axis.

Glycine, with two ionizable groups, has two plateaus: pKa₁ = 2.34 for the carboxyl,
pKa₂ = 9.60 for the amino group. Between them lies the **isoelectric point**, pI =
5.97, the pH where glycine carries no net charge. Histidine, with its extra ionizable
imidazole, has three plateaus (pKa₁ = 1.82, pKa₂ = 6.00, pKa₃ = 9.17) and a pI of
7.59. The extra wrinkle in the curve is the side chain earning its keep.

The shortcut for pI is worth committing to memory: find the form on the charge ladder
that is net-zero, then average the two pKa values that flank it. For glycine the
neutral zwitterion is bracketed by 2.34 and 9.60, so pI = (2.34 + 9.60)/2 = 5.97.
Drag the pH cursor across the plotter above and it shows which species dominates and
the net charge at every point. Push histidine's imidazole pKa toward 7.4 and you can
see for yourself why a catalytic residue wants its pKa near physiological pH.

## The payoff: one swap, one property, a disease

Now the opening mystery resolves. In sickle cell disease, position 6 of the
beta-globin chain carries a **valine** where a **glutamate** belongs. Glutamate is
negatively charged and hydrophilic; it sits comfortably on the protein's water-facing
surface. Valine is hydrophobic. The substitution plants a greasy patch on a surface
that should be wet, and under low oxygen those patches stick to each other,
polymerizing hemoglobin into rigid fibers that deform the red cell into a sickle. One
side chain changed from charged to greasy. That is the whole disease. The same logic
— R-group property predicts behavior — runs from the membrane helix to the active
site to the clinic.

## How we measure it in the lab

The behavior in this lesson is read off two kinds of measurement. **Titration** turns
charge into a curve. Add base in steps, plot pH, and the plateaus give you the pKa
values while the net-zero point gives you the pI — the numbers that let you predict an
amino acid's charge at any pH. To **detect** amino acids, chemists reach for
**ninhydrin**: it reacts with the free alpha-amino group and stains each one purple
(proline turns yellow). To **separate** them, they reach for **chromatography**, which
sorts a mixture by the same two side-chain properties this lesson is built on — charge
and hydrophobicity. The chemistry you reason about on paper is the chemistry the assay
reports back.
