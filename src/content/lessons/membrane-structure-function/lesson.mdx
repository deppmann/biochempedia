summary: >-
  Cystic fibrosis is, at bottom, a story about one protein that never reaches the
  right membrane. This lesson builds the cell membrane from its physics up: a
  self-assembling lipid bilayer powered by the hydrophobic effect, a permeability
  range spanning twelve orders of magnitude, fluidity tuned by chain saturation and
  cholesterol, and the proteins that turn an impermeable sheet into a selective gate.
  It ends where biology gets electrical — with the pumps, channels, and the potassium
  selectivity filter that rejects the smaller sodium ion on thermodynamic grounds.

objectives:

  - "Predict whether a molecule crosses a membrane from its charge and polarity, and locate the desolvation cliff between small nonpolar species and ions."
  - "Relate membrane fluidity to fatty-acid chain length, cis-unsaturation, and cholesterol's two-way buffering."
  - "Classify membrane transport using rate and energy source — channels, carriers, and pumps — and trace secondary active transport back to the Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase."
  - "Explain how the K⁺ channel selectivity filter rejects Na⁺ thermodynamically while passing K⁺ at near-diffusion-limited speed."

scientists:
  - name: "Roderick MacKinnon"
    dates: "1956–"
    contribution: >-
      A physician who taught himself X-ray crystallography and in 1998 solved the
      first atomic structure of a potassium channel (KcsA), showing that ion
      selectivity is a thermodynamic trick: a ring of backbone carbonyl oxygens
      mimics potassium's hydration shell so the larger ion is favored over the
      smaller one, roughly 10,000 to 1. Nobel Prize in Chemistry, 2003.
    profileSourceAnchor: "The Molecule Hunters (v10) — Roderick MacKinnon profile (quote grep-confirmed); KcsA structures 1BL8 and 1K4C."
    quotes:
      - "When I saw the structure of the potassium channel, I thought: nature has solved this problem so beautifully."
    story: >-
      MacKinnon gave up a tenured Harvard professorship, moved to Rockefeller in 1996,
      and taught himself crystallography — a field channel biophysicists had sworn was
      impossible for a membrane protein. He wanted to settle a fifty-year argument
      about how a cell counts ions. Human channels would not crystallize, so he chose
      KcsA, a bacterial potassium channel from Streptomyces lividans. The filter's
      signature sequence Thr-Val-Gly-Tyr-Gly turns its carbonyl oxygens inward so that
      potassium sheds its cage of water and the oxygens slip into water's place as if
      nothing had changed. Sodium, smaller, cannot reach all the oxygens at once and so
      cannot pay back the energy it spent shedding its own water. Selectivity by
      geometry rather than by a smaller hole, at about 10,000 to 1.
  - name: "Agnes Pockels"
    dates: "1862–1935"
    contribution: >-
      Barred from university, she measured the area a single lipid molecule occupies
      on water (about 20 square Angstroms) using a homemade trough — a tin tray and a
      shirt button — the first measurement of the packing density of membrane lipids.
    profileSourceAnchor: "The Molecule Hunters (v10) — Agnes Pockels profile (quote grep-confirmed)."
    quotes:
      - "I had a passionate interest in natural science, especially physics, and would have liked to study"
    story: >-
      In 1882 Brunswick, Germany, Pockels was denied a university education and relied
      on her brother to smuggle her his physics textbooks. She built a monolayer
      apparatus at her kitchen sink and found that as she compressed an oil film,
      surface tension barely budged until, at a critical density, the oil locked into a
      single tightly packed sheet. From that she calculated about 20 square Angstroms
      per molecule, now called the Pockels Point. In 1891 she wrote to Lord Rayleigh
      describing her homely appliances; he arranged for her work to appear in Nature.
      Twenty-five years later Irving Langmuir confirmed the same number with proper
      laboratory instruments. The footprint of a single membrane lipid, first measured
      with a shirt button.

techniques:
  - name: "FRAP (fluorescence recovery after photobleaching)"
    blurb: >-
      Bleach a spot on a fluorescently labeled membrane, then watch it refill. How fast
      it recovers gives the lateral diffusion coefficient; how much never recovers gives
      the immobile, anchored fraction. A 50% recovery means roughly half the labeled
      molecules are tethered to the cytoskeleton.
  - name: "Detergent vs. salt extraction"
    blurb: >-
      The operational test for integral versus peripheral membrane proteins. Peripheral
      proteins release with high salt or a pH change; integral proteins, which sit in
      the hydrophobic core, come out only when detergent dissolves the bilayer around them.
  - name: "Planar lipid bilayer recording"
    blurb: >-
      Fuse a single purified channel into an artificial bilayer and record its current,
      resolving one molecule opening and closing in real time. This single-channel
      approach underpinned decades of channel biophysics.
  - name: "Membrane-protein X-ray crystallography"
    blurb: >-
      Crystallizing a membrane protein (notoriously hard) and solving its atomic
      structure by X-ray diffraction. The KcsA structure settled a fifty-year argument
      about how a channel counts ions by showing the carbonyl-oxygen geometry of the
      selectivity filter directly.

practiceQuestions:
  - stem: "Phospholipids form a bilayer spontaneously in water, with no ATP required. The thermodynamic driving force is best described as:"
    choices:
      - "an increase in entropy as ordered water around the tails is released"
      - "the formation of covalent bonds between adjacent lipid tails"
      - "a decrease in the enthalpy of the lipid heads as they bond to one another"
      - "the active pumping of lipids into position by membrane proteins"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Self-assembly is driven by the hydrophobic effect, which is entropic: clustering
      the nonpolar tails frees the water that had been ordered around them, raising
      entropy. No covalent bonds form between the tails — the membrane is held by
      noncovalent forces — the process is not primarily enthalpy-driven at the heads,
      and it requires no ATP or pumping, which is the whole point.
  - stem: "Indole and tryptophan share nearly the same aromatic ring, yet indole crosses a lipid bilayer far more readily than tryptophan. This comparison best illustrates that membrane permeability is governed primarily by:"
    choices:
      - "molecular weight"
      - "aromatic ring size"
      - "charge and the desolvation cost it imposes"
      - "the presence of double bonds"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      Tryptophan carries charged groups that bring a large desolvation penalty (roughly
      40 to 80 kJ/mol to strip the hydration shell), so it is essentially blocked despite
      its near-identical ring. The pair is built to isolate charge from size: molecular
      weight and ring size are nearly matched, and double bonds are not the variable
      being tested.
  - stem: "A cell membrane contains saturated lipids, cis-unsaturated lipids, and trans-unsaturated lipids. Which lipid would pack most like a fully saturated lipid and therefore raise the membrane's melting temperature?"
    choices:
      - "the cis-unsaturated lipid, because its double bond adds rigidity"
      - "the trans-unsaturated lipid, because its double bond leaves the chain straight"
      - "the cis-unsaturated lipid, because the kink helps stacking"
      - "none of them; only chain length affects packing"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      A trans double bond keeps the acyl chain straight, so it stacks neatly like a
      saturated chain and raises Tm. A cis double bond introduces a roughly 30-degree
      kink that prevents tight packing and lowers Tm, so the cis options are wrong. Chain
      length matters too, but it is not the only factor, so the last choice is also wrong.
  - stem: "In a FRAP experiment, a labeled membrane protein is bleached in a small spot. Over time the fluorescence recovers, but it plateaus at only 50% of the original intensity. The most direct interpretation is:"
    choices:
      - "the protein diffuses laterally twice as fast as a typical lipid"
      - "the membrane has lost its asymmetry"
      - "the protein has flipped to the opposite leaflet"
      - "about half the labeled protein is immobile, likely anchored to the cytoskeleton"
    answer: 3
    rationale: >-
      In FRAP, the final recovery level reports the mobile fraction; recovery to only 50%
      means half the labeled molecules cannot diffuse into the spot, indicating an
      immobile, anchored population. The recovery rate, not the plateau, reports diffusion
      speed, so the "twice as fast" option confuses the two readouts — and FRAP measures
      lateral diffusion, not asymmetry or flip-flop.
  - stem: "The Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase consumes roughly a quarter of resting body ATP. A drug that completely inhibits it would most directly impair which other process?"
    choices:
      - "secondary active transport that depends on the Na⁺ gradient"
      - "aquaporin-mediated water flow"
      - "the hydrophobic effect that assembles the bilayer"
      - "passive diffusion of O₂ across the membrane"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Secondary active transporters (symporters like SGLT1, antiporters like the Na⁺/Ca²⁺
      exchanger) spend the sodium gradient that the Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase builds, so killing the
      pump collapses that gradient and stalls them — which is exactly how ouabain and
      digitalis exert their effects. Aquaporins pass water down its own gradient, the
      bilayer self-assembles without ATP, and O₂ diffuses passively, so none of those
      depend on the pump.

mcatQuestions:
  - stem: "A potassium channel selects K⁺ over the smaller Na⁺ ion by roughly 10,000 to 1. The selectivity filter presents 8 backbone carbonyl oxygens at each coordination site. The most accurate explanation for why Na⁺ is rejected is that:"
    choices:
      - "Na⁺ is too large to fit through the narrow filter"
      - "Na⁺ is repelled by a fixed negative charge unique to the filter"
      - "Na⁺ cannot contact all 8 oxygens at once, so the filter fails to offset its dehydration energy"
      - "Na⁺ binds the oxygens too tightly and becomes stuck"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      Selectivity is thermodynamic, not steric. The 8 oxygens closely mimic the cage of
      water K⁺ carries, so K⁺ pays little net energy to enter; the smaller Na⁺ cannot
      reach all 8 simultaneously, so the filter does not compensate Na⁺'s larger
      dehydration cost and it is excluded. Na⁺ is smaller, not larger, ruling out a size
      barrier; the mechanism is carbonyl coordination, not a single fixed charge; and Na⁺
      is rejected because it binds too poorly, not too tightly.
  - stem: "A patient ingests pufferfish containing tetrodotoxin and develops paralysis. Tetrodotoxin blocks the extracellular mouth of voltage-gated Na⁺ channels. The most direct consequence at the level of the neuron is:"
    choices:
      - "failure to depolarize, so action potentials cannot fire"
      - "sustained depolarization and continuous firing"
      - "leakage of K⁺ that hyperpolarizes the cell permanently"
      - "inhibition of the Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels carry the depolarizing influx that initiates an action
      potential; blocking their outer mouth prevents Na⁺ entry, so the membrane cannot
      depolarize and no action potential fires, producing paralysis. It does not cause
      continuous firing (the opposite of a block), it does not act through K⁺ leak, and
      tetrodotoxin targets the Na⁺ channel, not the Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase (that is ouabain's target).
  - stem: "CFTR's most common disease mutation, ΔF508, deletes a single phenylalanine. The resulting protein is degraded in the endoplasmic reticulum and rarely reaches the plasma membrane. This indicates that cystic fibrosis in these patients is best characterized as:"
    choices:
      - "a channel that reaches the membrane but stays permanently open"
      - "overactivity of an ABC transporter that pumps chloride out too quickly"
      - "a mutation that converts CFTR from a channel into a pump"
      - "a protein-trafficking defect: the channel is largely absent from the membrane"
    answer: 3
    rationale: >-
      ΔF508 causes misfolding and ER-associated degradation, so the channel never reaches
      the surface; the defect is one of folding and trafficking, making CFTR a missing
      channel rather than a broken one at the membrane. It is not stuck open and not
      overactive (it is absent), and the mutation does not convert CFTR's function. This is
      why Trikafta's correctors target folding so the protein can traffic, with a
      potentiator to keep it open once it arrives.

    notes: >-
      Lipid bilayers are nearly impermeable to ions and most polar molecules, and the
      reason is energetic, not a simple matter of size. Crossing the hydrophobic core
      means shedding the shell of water an ion or polar group wears, and stripping that
      water off costs a lot of energy. Hydrophobicity is the key variable. Indole, the
      uncharged ring of tryptophan, slips through far more easily than tryptophan itself,
      even though the ring is nearly the same. Charge is the toll. Across the whole range,
      permeability spans about twelve orders of magnitude.

    alt: "An analogy comparing straight saturated fatty acids parking tightly to kinked cis-unsaturated chains that cannot pack, with trans chains packing like saturated."
    source: "CH12 Membranes · slide 13"
    credit: "Original — C. Deppmann (lecture)"
    reviewedBy: "C. Deppmann (lecture author)"
    notes: >-
      Picture saturated fatty acids as straight-backed cars parking bumper to bumper in a
      tight, orderly row. That close packing makes the membrane rigid and raises its
      melting temperature. An unsaturated fatty acid has a kink from its cis double bond,
      like a car parked at an angle — it cannot pack neatly, so it leaves gaps and keeps
      the membrane fluid. The more cis double bonds, the looser the packing and the lower
      the melting point. A trans double bond, by contrast, straightens the chain back out,
      so it packs like a saturated fat.

  - src: "/slides/membrane-structure-function/lateral-vs-transverse.png"
    audio: "/slides/membrane-structure-function/audio/lateral-vs-transverse.mp3"
    title: "Lateral vs. transverse diffusion"
    alt: "Two panels: fast lateral diffusion within a leaflet versus glacially slow transverse flip-flop between leaflets, which preserves membrane asymmetry."
    source: "CH12 Membranes · slide 23"
    credit: "Original — C. Deppmann (lecture)"
    reviewedBy: "C. Deppmann (lecture author)"
    notes: >-
      Membrane lipids move, but not in every direction equally, and that inequality is
      what preserves the membrane's asymmetry. Lateral diffusion, within a leaflet, is
      fast — a lipid can travel the length of a bacterium in about a second. Transverse
      diffusion, flipping from one leaflet to the other, is glacially slow on its own,
      because the polar head would have to cross the greasy core. Cells use dedicated
      enzymes called flippases to move lipids across when they need to. Because
      spontaneous flip-flop almost never happens, the two leaflets keep their different
      compositions.
  - src: "/slides/membrane-structure-function/channels-carriers-pumps.png"
    audio: "/slides/membrane-structure-function/audio/channels-carriers-pumps.mp3"
    title: "Channels, carriers, and pumps"
    alt: "Three transport-protein families compared by speed and energy source: fast passive channels, regulated shape-changing carriers, and ATP-driven pumps."
    source: "CH12 Membranes · slide 27"
    credit: "Original — C. Deppmann (lecture)"
    reviewedBy: "C. Deppmann (lecture author)"
    notes: >-
      Membrane transport proteins sort into three families by speed and energy source.
      Channels are the fastest, up to about ten million ions per second, but passive —
      they only let things move down their gradient, with selectivity built into their
      structure. Carriers are slower and can be regulated; they change shape to ferry
      their cargo across. Pumps are the slowest, around a hundred cycles per second, but
      they spend ATP to push molecules against their gradient. Speed, regulation, and
      energy source together tell you which machine you are looking at.
  - src: "/slides/membrane-structure-function/abc-transporter.png"
    audio: "/slides/membrane-structure-function/audio/abc-transporter.mp3"

    source: "CH12 Membranes · slide 29"
    credit: "Original — C. Deppmann (lecture)"
    reviewedBy: "C. Deppmann (lecture author)"
    notes: >-
      ABC transporters share a common two-part architecture: two transmembrane domains
      that form the path across the bilayer, and two nucleotide-binding domains that bind
      and hydrolyze ATP. When ATP binds, the two engine domains clamp together and flip
      the protein from an inward-facing shape, open to the inside, to an outward-facing
      one, open to the outside. That conformational cycle is how the family moves cargo.
      CFTR is the odd member: same architecture, but it works as a chloride channel rather
      than a pump, which is why the cystic fibrosis mutation leaves a missing channel.
  - src: "/slides/membrane-structure-function/symporters-antiporters.png"
    audio: "/slides/membrane-structure-function/audio/symporters-antiporters.mp3"
    title: "Symporters vs. antiporters"
    alt: "Secondary active transport: a symporter (SGLT1) moving glucose and sodium the same direction, and an antiporter (Na/Ca exchanger) moving them oppositely."
    source: "CH12 Membranes · slide 33"
    credit: "Original — C. Deppmann (lecture)"
    reviewedBy: "C. Deppmann (lecture author)"
    notes: >-
      Secondary active transport comes in two flavors, and both spend the sodium gradient
      the pump builds. Symporters move two species in the same direction — SGLT1 in the
      intestine drags glucose uphill by riding sodium downhill. Antiporters move them in
      opposite directions — the cardiac sodium-calcium exchanger pushes calcium out using
      the inward sodium flow. Neither one burns ATP directly; they borrow the energy
      stored in the sodium gradient, like a water wheel stealing energy from a current.
      Every one of them ultimately depends on the sodium-potassium pump.
  - src: "/slides/membrane-structure-function/knock-on-mechanism.png"
    audio: "/slides/membrane-structure-function/audio/knock-on-mechanism.mp3"
    title: "The knock-on mechanism: fast and selective at once"
    alt: "Multiple K+ ions in the four selectivity-filter sites S1–S4; electrostatic repulsion lets one entering ion knock the next out the far side."
    source: "CH12 Membranes · slide 41"
    credit: "Original — C. Deppmann (lecture)"
    reviewedBy: "C. Deppmann (lecture author)"
    notes: >-
      The potassium channel moves ions at remarkable speed using a simple trick:
      electrostatic repulsion. Several potassium ions sit in the selectivity filter at
      once, across four binding sites, S1 through S4. Because like charges repel, the
      arrival of one ion shoves the ion ahead of it out the far side — like balls knocking
      down an assembly line, or a Newton's cradle. That knock-on turns binding energy
      directly into exit energy, so the channel is selective and fast at the same time,
      passing potassium at near the diffusion limit.

    source: "CH12 Membranes · slide 42"
    credit: "Original — C. Deppmann (lecture)"
    reviewedBy: "C. Deppmann (lecture author)"
    notes: >-
      Here is a practical decision tree for classifying any transport event. First ask
      whether the molecule needs to cross at all, and whether it is small and nonpolar —
      if so, it simply diffuses, like oxygen into a red blood cell. If it needs help, ask
      which direction it moves relative to its gradient. Downhill through a protein is
      facilitated diffusion or a channel. Uphill means active transport: powered directly
      by ATP in a pump, or indirectly by an ion gradient in secondary active transport.
      Three questions sort every case.

explainLevels:
  - level: "eli5"
    label: "Like I'm 5"
    text: |
      A cell is like a water balloon, but its skin is made of tiny soap-like pieces that
      line up by themselves. The greasy ends hide from water on the inside, the
      wet-loving ends face out, and the whole skin builds itself with no glue. If you poke
      it, it closes right back up. Some things slip through this skin easily, like the air
      you breathe. Other things, like salt, cannot get through on their own, so the cell
      builds little doors and tiny pumps to let them in and out. One sickness, called
      cystic fibrosis, happens when one special door is built wrong and never makes it to
      the skin, so the cell cannot move salt the way it should.
  - level: "highschool"
    label: "High school"
    text: |
      A cell membrane is a double layer of lipid molecules. Each lipid has a head that
      likes water and two tails that hate it, so in water they line up automatically into
      a sheet two molecules thick, tails tucked inside. No energy is needed; water itself
      drives the arrangement. The membrane is picky about what crosses. Small uncharged
      things like oxygen pass easily, but charged ions like sodium are nearly blocked,
      because an ion is wrapped in water and stripping that water off costs a lot of
      energy. To move ions and big molecules, the cell uses proteins: channels (fast, open
      doors, downhill only), carriers (shape-changers), and pumps (use ATP to push
      uphill). The biggest pump, the sodium-potassium ATPase, uses roughly a quarter of
      your resting energy — about half of it in neurons — to keep ions sorted. Cystic
      fibrosis is what happens when one such protein, the CFTR chloride channel, is built
      wrong and destroyed before it reaches the membrane.
  - level: "college"
    label: "College"
    text: |

## One protein, one disease

Cystic fibrosis comes down to a single protein in the wrong place. The gene is CFTR, a chloride channel that should sit in the membranes of cells lining the lungs and gut. In the most common mutation, ΔF508, one phenylalanine is deleted, the protein misfolds, and it is destroyed in the endoplasmic reticulum before it ever reaches the surface. The channel is not broken at the membrane. It never arrives. Everything downstream — the thick mucus, the chronic infections, the shortened life — follows from that one missing protein.

That is the throughline of this chapter, because it forces a useful question. To understand why one absent channel can reorganize an entire physiology, you first have to understand what a membrane is and what it does.

Physically, a membrane is thin and self-organizing. It runs about 7 nanometers thick (roughly 70 Angstroms): two leaflets, one lipid thick in each, with the tails meeting in the middle. For scale, a ribosome is about 25 nm across, so a membrane is roughly a quarter of that. It always forms closed compartments with no free edges, and if you puncture it, it reseals, because noncovalent forces hold it together rather than covalent bonds.

Functionally, four properties matter. The membrane is asymmetric: the outer leaflet differs from the inner one. Phosphatidylserine, for instance, stays on the inside until a cell dies, when flipping it outward becomes an "eat me" signal for the immune system. The membrane is fluid, its lipids drifting laterally. It is selectively permeable, letting oxygen through while blocking sodium. And it is electrically polarized, the inside sitting around 60 to 70 millivolts negative relative to the outside. A sheet of plastic would have none of these.

## A membrane that builds itself

Phospholipids form bilayers spontaneously, and the energy comes from water, not ATP. Each lipid has a polar head and two greasy tails. Drop them in water and the tails cluster to escape it, the heads face out, and a bilayer (or a sealed sphere, a liposome) forms on its own. The driving force is the hydrophobic effect, and it is entropic: when the tails huddle together, the water that had been caging them is released into disorder. The cell spends nothing to assemble its boundary.

This self-assembly is also useful. Liposomes carry drugs. Load a water-soluble drug in the aqueous core, a fat-soluble one in the bilayer, and you have a delivery vehicle. Doxil packages the chemotherapy drug doxorubicin in liposomes and spares the heart much of its toxicity. The COVID-19 mRNA vaccines used lipid nanoparticles to protect fragile mRNA long enough to deliver it.

The same physics that builds the membrane makes it nearly impermeable to ions, and the reason is desolvation. An ion in water wears a tight shell of water molecules. To cross the oily membrane core it would have to shed that shell, and stripping water off an ion costs roughly 40 to 80 kJ/mol. What blocks the ion is its charge, not its size: it travels wrapped in water it must leave behind. Consider indole and tryptophan: nearly the same aromatic ring, but tryptophan carries charged groups and crosses orders of magnitude more slowly. The ring crosses fine; the charge is the toll.

Stack everything up and permeability spans about twelve orders of magnitude. Water crosses a membrane roughly 10 billion times faster than sodium does. The order runs gases, then water, then small polar molecules, then ions far at the bottom.

Water has one official exception. Aquaporins are channels that pass about 3 billion (10⁹) water molecules per second through a pore just 2.8 Angstroms wide, single file. The remarkable part is what they keep out. Protons normally race through water by hopping along hydrogen bonds, but an asparagine in the pore breaks that chain, and a positively charged arginine repels any proton that gets close. The channel passes water in a torrent and still excludes the proton. In the kidney, aquaporin-2 is switched on by the hormone vasopressin; when that regulation fails, the kidneys cannot concentrate urine and a person can pass up to about 20 liters of dilute urine a day, the condition called diabetes insipidus.

## Tuning the fluidity

A membrane has to stay fluid, and the cell tunes that fluidity with chemistry. The melting temperature, Tm, is the point where half the lipids have melted from a tight gel into a fluid state. Two features set it. Longer fatty-acid tails pack more snugly and raise Tm. A cis double bond puts a roughly 30-degree kink in the tail that prevents tight packing and lowers Tm. Saturated tails are straight sticks that stack neatly; unsaturated tails have a permanent bend that keeps them loose. Trans fats straighten the tail back out, so they pack like a saturated fat despite being technically unsaturated.

Cholesterol is the membrane's thermostat, and it works in both directions. Its rigid four-ring body jams between lipids and stops them from packing too tightly when the cell gets cold, keeping the membrane from freezing solid. Its flexible tail fills gaps and stops the membrane from getting too runny when the cell warms up. One molecule buffers against both rigidity and excess fluidity. This is also why statins, which lower cholesterol synthesis, can cause muscle pain in some people: muscle-cell membranes still need cholesterol, and starving them of it has consequences.

Cholesterol and sphingolipids also cluster into lipid rafts, small dynamic patches about 50 to 200 nm across. Rafts act as gathering points where signaling proteins find their partners. HIV and influenza both exploit rafts to assemble and bud.

## The proteins in the sheet

Most of what a membrane does, it does through protein. Most membrane proteins are integral, embedded in the bilayer; the rest are peripheral, perched on the surface. The test for telling them apart is operational: peripheral proteins come off with high salt or a pH change, while integral proteins release only when detergent dissolves the membrane around them.

Proteins cross the membrane in a few stereotyped ways. An alpha-helix needs about 20 hydrophobic residues to span the bilayer once, and multi-pass proteins string several together. A beta-barrel rolls a beta sheet into a tube, found in outer membranes. Some proteins skip spanning entirely and attach by a lipid anchor: a GPI anchor on the outside, a prenyl or fatty-acyl group on the inside. The signaling protein Ras, for example, is prenylated so it can dock at the inner membrane.

One clinically loaded example: the enzyme COX-1 sits embedded in the membrane and pulls arachidonic acid in through a hydrophobic channel to make prostaglandins. Aspirin works by reaching into that channel and irreversibly acetylating the active site. Because the block is permanent, it lasts until the cell makes fresh enzyme, which is the basis of low-dose aspirin's lasting antiplatelet effect. Ibuprofen competes for the same site reversibly, so taking ibuprofen before aspirin can actually shield the site and blunt aspirin's heart protection.

## What stays put, what moves

Membrane proteins and lipids move, and FRAP lets us watch them do it. Fluorescence recovery after photobleaching means exactly what it says: tag the membrane with fluorescent dye, bleach a small spot dark with a laser, and watch unbleached molecules diffuse back in. How fast the spot recovers gives the diffusion coefficient. How completely it recovers tells you the mobile fraction; if recovery stalls at 50%, half the labeled molecules are anchored to the cytoskeleton and cannot move.

The two kinds of motion are not equal. Lateral diffusion is fast: a lipid can travel across a whole cell in seconds. Transverse motion, a lipid flipping from one leaflet to the other, is glacially slow without a dedicated enzyme called a flippase. That asymmetry of rates is what preserves the membrane's chemical asymmetry. Phosphatidylserine stays inside precisely because flip-flop almost never happens on its own.

## Crossing on purpose: pumps, channels, and the selectivity trick

When something charged or large has to cross, a protein does the work, and three questions sort out how. Does it need a protein at all? If so, is it moving down its gradient or against it? And if against, does ATP power it directly or indirectly? The answers define three machines. Channels are highways: fast, around a million ions per second, but passive, moving things only downhill. Carriers are slower and regulated, changing shape to ferry their cargo. Pumps are escalators: slow, around a hundred cycles per second, but they spend ATP to push against the gradient.

The flagship pump is the Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase, a P-type ATPase that ejects 3 sodium ions and imports 2 potassium ions per ATP. It runs constantly and burns a large fraction of your resting ATP — on the order of a quarter, and up to half in neurons — to maintain the gradients that almost everything else depends on. The "P" marks its trick: it phosphorylates one of its own aspartate residues, and that phosphoaspartate intermediate drives the conformational flip from inward-facing to outward-facing.

That gradient is a battery, and secondary active transport spends it. Couple a molecule running downhill to another being dragged uphill, and you get free uphill transport, the cellular version of a water wheel stealing energy from one current to drive another. Symporters move both in the same direction (SGLT1 hauls glucose into intestinal cells on the back of sodium); antiporters move them oppositely (the Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger uses sodium to push calcium out). Every one of these depends on the gradient the Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase builds. The diabetes drugs called gliflozins block SGLT2 in the kidney so glucose spills into the urine and blood sugar falls.

Pull on this thread and a folk remedy appears: digitalis, from the foxglove plant, inhibits the Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase. Sodium then backs up inside the heart cell, the Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger slows, calcium accumulates, and each heartbeat hits harder — a treatment for failing hearts, with a narrow therapeutic window, since too much triggers calcium-overload arrhythmias. (William Withering worked the rest out from an old woman's foxglove brew in 1785; that story is in the margins.)

CFTR belongs to a family that explains a lot of this machinery. ABC transporters share an architecture: two transmembrane domains that form the path across, and two nucleotide-binding domains that act as the ATP-powered engine. When ATP binds, the two engine domains clamp together and flip the protein from inward-facing to outward-facing. P-glycoprotein is the family's promiscuous pump, throwing structurally diverse drugs out of cancer cells. Tumors that overexpress it become multidrug-resistant, evolving a pump that ejects chemotherapy before it can work. CFTR is the family's odd member: same domains, but it functions as a channel rather than a pump. And that is what makes ΔF508 a missing channel rather than merely a broken one. The drug Trikafta, approved in 2019, combines two correctors (elexacaftor and tezacaftor) that help the protein fold and reach the surface with a potentiator (ivacaftor) that keeps it open once it gets there. It is projected to add decades of expected survival in cystic fibrosis — precision medicine aimed at the protein-level consequence of a mutation.

Then there are channels that gate. Voltage-gated channels open in response to membrane voltage; ligand-gated ones open when a molecule binds. Two famous toxins, tetrodotoxin from pufferfish and saxitoxin from the dinoflagellates behind paralytic shellfish poisoning, both plug the outer mouth of voltage-gated sodium channels, so nerves cannot depolarize and the victim is paralyzed. TRP channels sense the environment: TRPV1 opens to heat and to capsaicin, TRPM8 opens to cold and to menthol. Capsaicin activates the heat-sensing channel directly, which is why chili registers as heat and mint as cold even though nothing has changed temperature.

The most elegant problem in this whole chapter is how a potassium channel rejects sodium, because sodium is the smaller ion. A simple sieve would let the smaller ion through, so size cannot be the answer. The selectivity filter solves it thermodynamically. It lines the path with backbone carbonyl oxygens that present, at each site, 8 oxygens to the passing ion, closely mimicking the cage of water K⁺ carries in solution. K⁺ sheds its water and the oxygens take water's place almost perfectly, so it pays little net energy to enter. Na⁺ is too small to touch all 8 oxygens at once, so it cannot pay back the energy it spent shedding its own water, and it stalls. The result is about 10,000-fold selectivity for the larger ion. The channel offers an energy landscape only K⁺ can afford.

That raises one more puzzle. If the filter binds K⁺ tightly enough to be that selective, why does K⁺ ever leave? The answer is crowding. Multiple K⁺ ions sit in the filter at once and repel each other electrostatically, so the entry of one ion shoves the next one out, like a Newton's cradle converting entry energy directly into exit energy. K⁺ moves at near the diffusion limit, around 10⁸ ions per second, selective and fast at the same time. Roderick MacKinnon solved this structure and won the 2003 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for it.

The action potential is where all of this comes together: voltage-gated sodium and potassium channels firing in sequence, the Na⁺-K⁺ pump resetting the gradients, a ball-and-chain plug swinging in to inactivate the sodium channel, and a refractory period that keeps the signal traveling in one direction. The region the impulse just passed cannot fire again yet, so the signal does not run backward. The membrane — the impermeable sheet that builds itself for free — turns out to be the substrate on which a nerve fires.
