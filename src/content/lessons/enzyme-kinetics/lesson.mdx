  - name: "The enzyme assay"
    blurb: >-
      Velocity is a slope: product formed per unit time. If the product absorbs
      light, you watch a spectrophotometer's reading climb and read the rate
      straight off the line — how many classic assays work.
  - name: "Measuring initial velocity (V₀)"
    blurb: >-
      Measure the slope in the first instants, before substrate is depleted or
      product builds up and gums the enzyme. V₀ is the clean, reproducible number
      every kinetic parameter is built from.
  - name: "Extracting Km and Vmax"
    blurb: >-
      Measure V₀ at many substrate concentrations and fit the hyperbola (today, by
      non-linear regression). The Lineweaver–Burk double-reciprocal linearizes the
      same data: y-intercept = 1/Vmax, x-intercept = −1/Km, slope = Km/Vmax.

practiceQuestions:
  - stem: "An enzyme obeys Michaelis–Menten kinetics. By definition, the Michaelis constant Km is the substrate concentration at which:"
    choices:
      - "the reaction reaches its maximum velocity, Vmax"
      - "the reaction velocity is half of Vmax"
      - "every active site is permanently and irreversibly occupied"
      - "the enzyme begins to denature"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      Km is defined as the [S] at which v = ½Vmax. Vmax itself is only approached
      as [S] → ∞ (it's an asymptote you never quite reach), which is why Km — a
      point you can actually locate on the curve — is the useful handle.

  - stem: "For a Michaelis–Menten enzyme, what substrate concentration (as a multiple of Km) gives 80% of Vmax?"
    choices:
      - "0.8 × Km"
      - "1.25 × Km"
      - "4 × Km"
      - "8 × Km"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      Set v = 0.8 Vmax in v = Vmax[S]/(Km+[S]): 0.8(Km+[S]) = [S] → 0.8 Km = 0.2[S]
      → [S] = 4 Km. In general, to reach a fraction f of Vmax you need [S] =
      (f / (1−f)) · Km, so 0.8/0.2 = 4.

  - stem: "On a Lineweaver–Burk (double-reciprocal) plot of 1/v versus 1/[S], the y-intercept equals:"
    choices:
      - "Vmax"
      - "1/Vmax"
      - "−1/Km"
      - "Km/Vmax"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      Taking reciprocals of the Michaelis–Menten equation gives 1/v =
      (Km/Vmax)(1/[S]) + 1/Vmax — a straight line. The y-intercept (where 1/[S] = 0)
      is 1/Vmax; the x-intercept is −1/Km; the slope is Km/Vmax. A classic exam trap
      is forgetting the intercept is the reciprocal of Vmax, not Vmax itself.

  - stem: "A competitive inhibitor binds the free enzyme's active site. Compared with the uninhibited reaction, it:"
    choices:
      - "lowers Vmax and raises the apparent Km"
      - "leaves Vmax unchanged but raises the apparent Km"
      - "lowers both Vmax and the apparent Km"
      - "leaves Km unchanged but lowers Vmax"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      Substrate and inhibitor compete for the same site, so flooding the system
      with substrate still out-competes the inhibitor — Vmax is unchanged. But you
      now need more substrate to reach ½Vmax, so the apparent Km rises. On a
      Lineweaver–Burk plot this is a shared y-intercept (1/Vmax) with a steeper slope.

  - stem: "Vmax measured in a test tube depends on how much enzyme you added. To get a property of the enzyme molecule itself you compute kcat = Vmax/[E]ₜ. kcat tells you:"
    choices:
      - "the substrate concentration at half-maximal velocity"
      - "how many substrate molecules one enzyme molecule converts per second when saturated"
      - "the total amount of product the reaction will ever form"
      - "the activation energy of the reaction"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      kcat (the turnover number, units s⁻¹) is the maximum number of reactions one
      enzyme molecule catalyzes per second when fully saturated — carbonic
      anhydrase's is ~600,000 s⁻¹. The half-max [S] is Km; the ratio kcat/Km
      measures catalytic efficiency under sub-saturating (physiological) conditions.

  - stem: "An enzyme has Vmax = 60 µmol·min⁻¹ and Km = 4 mM. At a substrate concentration of 12 mM, the initial velocity is closest to:"
    choices:
      - "15 µmol·min⁻¹"
      - "30 µmol·min⁻¹"
      - "45 µmol·min⁻¹"
      - "60 µmol·min⁻¹"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      v = Vmax[S]/(Km+[S]) = 60 × 12 / (4 + 12) = 720/16 = 45 µmol·min⁻¹. Shortcut:
      [S] = 12 mM is 3 × Km, and at [S] = 3 Km the velocity is ¾ Vmax = 45.

  - stem: "On a Lineweaver–Burk plot, the lines measured with and without an inhibitor intersect on the y-axis (same 1/Vmax), but the inhibited line has a steeper slope. The inhibitor is:"
    choices:
      - "uncompetitive"
      - "competitive"
      - "purely noncompetitive"
      - "irreversible"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      A shared y-intercept means Vmax is unchanged; a steeper slope (Km/Vmax) means
      the apparent Km rose — the competitive signature. Uncompetitive inhibition
      lowers Vmax and Km together, giving parallel lines; pure noncompetitive
      inhibition lowers Vmax with Km unchanged, so those lines share the x-intercept
      (−1/Km). You can reproduce all three with the plotter above.

  - stem: "Enzymes accelerate reactions by binding the transition state more tightly than they bind the substrate. This principle predicts that the most potent reversible inhibitors are molecules that:"
    choices:

      - "closely resemble the high-energy transition state"
      - "closely resemble the final product"
      - "are far larger than the active site can accommodate"

      The active site is complementary to the transition state, not the substrate.

**Carbonic anhydrase processes about 600,000 reactions every second** — faster
than any machine humans have ever built. So here is the question this whole
lesson turns on: if enzymes are that fast, why isn't every enzyme in your body
running at top speed right now? And if you keep doubling the amount of substrate,
does the rate just keep doubling forever?

It doesn't. And the reason why is one of the most useful ideas in all of
biochemistry.

## The idea: why velocity hits a ceiling

Thermodynamics tells you the *destination* — whether a reaction can happen.
Kinetics tells you the *journey* — how fast. An enzyme speeds up a reaction, but
to do anything at all it first has to **grab its substrate and hold it**, forming
a temporary enzyme–substrate (ES) complex, before it can convert it to product:

> E + S ⇌ ES → E + P

Here's the catch: there are only so many enzyme molecules, each with a limited
number of active sites. When substrate is scarce, adding more speeds things up —
every new molecule finds an empty site quickly. But as substrate climbs, the
sites start filling. Eventually **every site is occupied**: the enzyme is
*saturated*, working flat out, and pouring in more substrate changes nothing.

Think of a toll booth. When traffic is light, cars are processed as fast as they
arrive. When traffic is heavy, you hit the booth's maximum capacity — a longer
line doesn't make the attendant faster. That capacity ceiling is **Vmax**, the
maximum velocity.

Plot initial velocity (V₀) against substrate concentration [S] and you get a
**hyperbola**: steep at first, then bending over toward the Vmax asymptote. In
1913 Leonor Michaelis and Maud Menten captured that whole curve in one equation:

> **v = Vmax · [S] / (Km + [S])**

Two numbers describe any such enzyme:

- **Vmax** — the top speed, reached only when the enzyme is saturated.
- **Km** (the Michaelis constant) — the substrate concentration at which the
  enzyme runs at *half* its maximum speed. Think of Km as the enzyme's "substrate
  sweet spot": a **small Km** means the enzyme reaches half-speed at low [S], so
  it grabs substrate efficiently. Km is fixed for a given enzyme–substrate pair.

One subtlety worth keeping straight: Km is *not* the same as binding affinity. It
only approximates the dissociation constant of the ES complex when catalysis is
slow compared with substrate falling back off. More safely, read Km as **"how
much substrate you need to get this enzyme going."**

Finally, Vmax depends on how much enzyme you put in the tube — double the enzyme,
double Vmax. To get a property of the enzyme *molecule* itself, divide it out:
**kcat = Vmax / [E]ₜ**, the *turnover number* — how many reactions one molecule
runs per second when saturated. The ratio **kcat/Km** measures catalytic
efficiency, and the best enzymes push it right up against the diffusion limit.

left and right, and watch where the half-max point lands.

## Reading the curve a second way: Lineweaver–Burk

There's a practical problem with the hyperbola: Vmax is an asymptote you never
actually reach, so reading it off a curve by eye is error-prone. Before computers
could fit hyperbolae, biochemists needed a *straight line* they could analyze
with a ruler. Take the reciprocal of both sides of the Michaelis–Menten equation
and you get one:

> **1/v = (Km/Vmax) · (1/[S]) + 1/Vmax**

That's just `y = mx + b`. Plotting 1/v against 1/[S] — the **double-reciprocal**
or **Lineweaver–Burk** plot — gives a line whose **y-intercept is 1/Vmax**, whose
**x-intercept is −1/Km**, and whose **slope is Km/Vmax**. (Watch the reciprocals:
the intercept is 1/Vmax, *not* Vmax — a reliable source of exam mistakes.) The
right-hand panel of the plotter draws this line live as you change the parameters.

## When something gets in the way: inhibitors

The same framework explains how molecules *slow* an enzyme down. Reversible
inhibitors come in three flavors, and Lineweaver–Burk makes them easy to tell
apart:

- **Competitive** — the inhibitor competes for the active site itself. Flood the
  system with substrate and you win, so **Vmax is unchanged**, but you need more
  substrate to get going (**apparent Km rises**). *Signature: lines pivot about a
  shared y-intercept.*
- **Uncompetitive** — the inhibitor binds only the ES complex, locking the enzyme
  mid-reaction. Both **Vmax and Km fall** together. *Signature: parallel lines.*
- **Noncompetitive** — the inhibitor binds whether or not substrate is present and
  reduces the enzyme's catalytic ability. **Vmax falls, Km unchanged.**
  *Signature: lines share the x-intercept.*

Toggle the **competitive inhibitor** on in the plotter and watch the two
Lineweaver–Burk lines pivot about that shared 1/Vmax intercept — competitive
inhibition's fingerprint.

Why is the active site so picky about what binds it? Because catalysis works by
gripping the fleeting **transition state** more tightly than the substrate
itself. That single idea — the active site is shaped for the transition state,
not the resting molecule — is why **transition-state analogs** make such potent
inhibitors, and it's the thread running through the scientists below: from
Fischer's rigid "lock and key," to Koshland's flexible "induced fit," to the
moment David Phillips finally *saw* an enzyme bend its substrate out of shape.

## How we measure it in the lab

Velocity is a slope — product formed per unit time — so the trick is to watch the
reaction happen. If the product (or substrate) absorbs light, a spectrophotometer
reads the change continuously and the rate falls straight off the line. The one
discipline that matters most: measure the **initial velocity**, in the first
moments before substrate is noticeably depleted, before product accumulates and
interferes, before the enzyme tires. Repeat that clean V₀ measurement across a
range of substrate concentrations, fit the curve, and out come Km and Vmax — the
two numbers that let you compare any enzyme to any other.
