summary: >-
  Can an enzyme make an impossible reaction happen? No — it can only make a
  possible one fast. This chapter separates what a reaction *will* do
  (thermodynamics: ΔG, Keq) from how *fast* it does it (kinetics: the activation
  barrier), and shows that an enzyme touches only the second — by gripping the
  fleeting transition state more tightly than the substrate.

estMinutes: 22
objectives:
  - "Classify an enzyme into its EC class and tell the cofactor terms apart — coenzyme vs. metal ion, prosthetic group, apoenzyme vs. holoenzyme."
  - "Use ΔG to call a reaction exergonic or endergonic, and connect ΔG°′ to the equilibrium constant through ΔG°′ = −RT ln Keq."
  - "Explain why an enzyme changes the rate of a reaction but never its ΔG, ΔG°, or Keq."
  - "Describe how an enzyme lowers the activation barrier by stabilizing the transition state through binding energy and induced fit."

    dates: "1852–1919"
    contribution: >-
      Coined the "lock and key" picture of enzyme specificity in 1894 — the
      metaphor this chapter spends its last section correcting, since the real
      active site flexes shut around what it binds (induced fit) rather than
      sitting rigid like a lock.
    profileSourceAnchor: "The Molecule Hunters (v10) — section intro, \"The Invisible Machines: Enzymes and Catalysis\"; Fischer's 1894 lecture and 1894/1898 papers, quoted inline."
    quotes:
      - "Enzyme and glucoside must fit together like a lock and key."
      - "Only in the case of similar geometrical structure"
      - "the asymmetric structure of the enzyme molecule"
      - "I am far from placing this hypothesis side by side to the established theories of our science, and readily admit that it can only be thoroughly tested when we are able to isolate the enzymes in a pure state and thus investigate their configuration."
      - "the progress of science has deprived the highly respected lifeforce of even this last hiding-place: for we are now in a position to artificially prepare such active substances without any assistance from a living organism."
    photo: "/scientists/emil-fischer.png"
    story: >-
      The most durable metaphor in enzymology escaped almost by accident. Closing
      an 1894 lecture, Fischer reached for an image to explain why an enzyme would
      tear one sugar apart and ignore its near-twin: enzyme and substrate had to
      fit "like lock and key." He distrusted it the moment he said it. By 1898 he
      was publicly walking it back, insisting the specificity lay in "the
      asymmetric structure of the enzyme molecule" and that he had used the
      picture only to make the thought clearer. Then the warning this chapter
      proves him right to give: the idea "can only be thoroughly tested when we
      are able to isolate the enzymes in a pure state and thus investigate their
      configuration." It took most of a century. When crystallographers finally
      held a pure enzyme and looked, the brass lock turned out to flex — closing
      around its substrate, and gripping the fleeting transition state tighter
      still. That correction is what Koshland named induced fit. Fischer reached
      the lock-and-key idea the way he read structure off behavior everywhere
      else: he built sugars from glycerol to prove life held no chemistry beyond
      the ordinary, declaring that science had stripped the "lifeforce" of "this
      last hiding-place." The phenylhydrazine that fingerprinted those sugars
      also, slowly, poisoned him; in 1919, dying of an inoperable cancer, he took
      his own life.

  - name: "Daniel Koshland"
    dates: "1920–2007"
    contribution: >-
      Replaced Fischer's rigid lock with "induced fit" (1958): the enzyme
      reshapes itself around what it binds, reaching its tightest grip on the
      strained transition state rather than the resting substrate.
    profileSourceAnchor: "The Molecule Hunters (v10) — \"The Invisible Machines: Enzymes and Catalysis,\" Koshland profile."
    quotes:
      - "What is new is not true, and what is true is not new. — an anonymous reviewer's dismissal of Koshland's induced-fit paper, as recounted in The Molecule Hunters"
    photo: "/scientists/daniel-koshland.png"
    story: >-
      By the 1950s the lock-and-key picture decorated every textbook, and Daniel
      Koshland knew it was incomplete — because of water. If an active site sat
      permanently open and waiting, water, smaller than any substrate, should slip
      in constantly and trigger chaos. It doesn't. So something must change when
      the real substrate arrives: the site closes around it, and the
      complementarity that matters is reached not at the resting substrate but at
      the strained transition state. Binding induces the fit, and water is too
      small to force it. The idea cut so hard against the dogma that the standard
      journals turned it away; one reviewer's verdict became famous ("What is new
      is not true, and what is true is not new"). Koshland slipped the paper into
      PNAS in 1958 through a loophole, and X-ray crystallography later proved him
      right: enzymes flex, shift, and clamp down. He had moved biochemistry from a
      science of static shapes to one of moving machines.

techniques:
  - name: "Measuring rate enhancement"
    blurb: >-
      Run the reaction with the enzyme and without it, take each rate, and divide.
      A catalyzed rate of 540 s⁻¹ over an uncatalyzed 2.9×10⁻⁶ s⁻¹ gives 1.9×10⁸ —
      the number that says how much work the protein is doing.
  - name: "Reading ΔG°′ from the equilibrium constant"
    blurb: >-
      Let a reaction reach equilibrium, measure [products]/[reactants], and that
      ratio is Keq. Plug it into ΔG°′ = −RT ln Keq (R = 8.314 J/mol·K, T = 298 K)
      to convert a concentration you can measure into a free energy you cannot.
  - name: "Transition-state analog binding"
    blurb: >-
      Build a stable molecule shaped like the transition state, then measure how
      tightly the enzyme holds it versus the real substrate. Proline racemase
      binds its analog 160× tighter — strong evidence the active site is
      complementary to the transition state, not the substrate.

practiceQuestions:
  - stem: "A newly characterized enzyme catalyzes the intramolecular conversion of glucose-6-phosphate to fructose-6-phosphate, rearranging atoms within a single molecule without adding or removing any group. To which EC class does this enzyme belong?"
    choices:
      - "Transferases"
      - "Hydrolases"
      - "Isomerases"
      - "Ligases"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      Isomerases (EC class 5) catalyze intramolecular rearrangements, converting a
      molecule into one of its isomers without changing its molecular formula —
      phosphoglucose isomerase is the classic example. Transferases move a group
      between molecules; hydrolases use water to cleave bonds; ligases join two
      molecules using ATP. None of those describes a simple rearrangement.

  - stem: "A purified enzyme shows no activity in a tube of buffer and substrate, but full activity returns when Zn²⁺ is added. Using this chapter's cofactor terms, which statement is correct?"
    choices:
      - "The enzyme alone is the holoenzyme, and adding Zn²⁺ converts it to the apoenzyme"
      - "The enzyme alone is the apoenzyme, and the Zn²⁺-bound active form is the holoenzyme"
      - "Zn²⁺ is an organic coenzyme derived from a vitamin"
      - "Zn²⁺ is a prosthetic group because it dissociates freely from the protein"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      The inactive protein lacking its required cofactor is the apoenzyme (apo =
      away/absent); the active protein-plus-cofactor complex is the holoenzyme
      (holo = whole). Zn²⁺ is an inorganic metal-ion cofactor, not an organic
      vitamin-derived coenzyme, so C is wrong. A prosthetic group is a tightly
      bound cofactor — "dissociates freely" contradicts that, so D is wrong.

  - stem: "A reaction has ΔG°′ = +13.8 kJ/mol (the chapter's value for glucose-6-phosphate formation). Adding the appropriate enzyme to a mixture at equilibrium will:"
    choices:
      - "Make the reaction spontaneous by lowering ΔG°′ below zero"
      - "Increase the equilibrium constant so more product accumulates"
      - "Have no effect on ΔG°′ or Keq, because enzymes change only the rate"
      - "Reverse the reaction so products become reactants"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      Enzymes lower the activation energy (ΔG‡) and speed the approach to
      equilibrium, but they cannot change ΔG, ΔG°′, or Keq. A positive-ΔG°′
      reaction stays thermodynamically unfavorable in the standard direction
      regardless of enzyme — the catalyst just lets the system reach its
      (unchanged) equilibrium faster. A, B, and D all wrongly move the position of
      equilibrium.

  - stem: "Proline racemase binds the analog pyrrole-2-carboxylate (Kd = 0.022 mM) about 160-fold more tightly than its substrate L-proline (Kd = 3.6 mM). What does this most directly support?"
    choices:
      - "The lock-and-key model, because the enzyme binds substrate with maximum affinity"
      - "Induced fit, because the active site is most complementary to the transition state, not the substrate"
      - "That the enzyme increases the equilibrium constant of the reaction"
      - "That the analog is the true physiological substrate of the enzyme"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      The active site achieves maximal complementarity to the transition state, not
      the ground-state substrate — and tighter binding of a transition-state analog
      than of the substrate is the experimental hallmark of that idea. Lock-and-key
      predicts the opposite (A). Binding affects rate, not Keq (C). A transition-
      state analog is a stable mimic, not the molecule the enzyme turns over (D).

  - stem: "A student claims that because all known catalysts in cells are proteins, RNA cannot perform catalysis. Which counterexample from this chapter best refutes the claim?"
    choices:
      - "DNA ligase, which seals nicks in the DNA backbone"
      - "Carbonic anhydrase, which interconverts CO₂ and bicarbonate"
      - "The ribosome, whose peptidyl transferase activity is carried out by RNA"
      - "Trypsin, which cleaves peptide bonds after Lys and Arg residues"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      Ribozymes are catalytic RNA molecules, and the ribosome's peptide-bond-forming
      center is itself RNA-catalyzed — the ribosome is a ribozyme, which directly
      refutes the "all enzymes are proteins" trap. DNA ligase, carbonic anhydrase,
      and trypsin are all protein enzymes, so none serves as a counterexample.

mcatQuestions:
  - stem: "An enzyme catalyzes its reaction at 540 s⁻¹, whereas the uncatalyzed rate is 2.9 × 10⁻⁶ s⁻¹. Approximately what rate enhancement does the enzyme provide, and what does this tell you about the reaction's equilibrium constant?"
    choices:
      - "~1.9 × 10⁸; the enhancement also increases Keq by the same factor"
      - "~1.9 × 10⁸; Keq is unchanged because catalysis affects only rate"
      - "~5.4 × 10⁻⁹; Keq is decreased proportionally"
      - "~2.9 × 10²; Keq is unchanged because catalysis affects only rate"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      Rate enhancement = catalyzed/uncatalyzed = 540 / (2.9×10⁻⁶) ≈ 1.9×10⁸. A
      catalyst speeds the forward and reverse reactions equally, so it accelerates
      the approach to equilibrium without altering the equilibrium constant — Keq is
      unchanged. A wrongly couples Keq to rate; C and D miscompute the ratio.

  - stem: "Using ΔG°′ = −RT ln Keq with R = 8.314 J/(mol·K) and T = 298 K, a reaction with ΔG°′ = −17 kJ/mol has an equilibrium constant closest to which value, and which direction does it favor?"
    choices:
      - "Keq ≈ 10⁻³; favors reactants"
      - "Keq ≈ 1; equal reactants and products"
      - "Keq ≈ 10³; favors products"
      - "Keq ≈ 10¹⁷; favors products"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      Keq = e^(−ΔG/RT) = e^(17000 / (8.314 × 298)) = e^(6.86) ≈ 950, on the order of
      10³. A negative ΔG°′ gives Keq > 1, so products are favored. A would need a
      positive ΔG°′; B needs ΔG°′ near zero; D (10¹⁷) needs a far more negative ΔG°′.

  - stem: "Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol synthesis, acting as transition-state analogs that bind ~10,000-fold more tightly than the natural substrate. A small subset of patients develops muscle pain with elevated creatine kinase. Which mechanistic chain best explains this off-target effect?"
    choices:
      - "Statins covalently modify actin, degrading muscle filaments independent of the mevalonate pathway"
      - "By blocking the mevalonate pathway, statins reduce CoQ10 and isoprenoid synthesis, impairing mitochondrial electron transport and muscle protein function, leading to myocyte breakdown"
      - "Statins raise the equilibrium constant for cholesterol synthesis, causing toxic cholesterol accumulation in muscle"
      - "Statins increase HMG-CoA reductase activity in muscle, overproducing ATP and rupturing myocytes"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      HMG-CoA reductase sits at the head of the mevalonate pathway, so inhibiting it
      reduces not only cholesterol but also downstream CoQ10 (needed for the
      mitochondrial electron transport chain) and isoprenoids (needed for muscle
      protein function) — leading to mitochondrial dysfunction, ATP depletion, and
      myocyte breakdown with elevated creatine kinase. A invents a covalent actin
      mechanism; C wrongly has an inhibitor raising Keq; D has statins activating,
      not inhibiting, the enzyme.

      This is the single most important diagram in the chapter. Both curves start at
      the same substrate and end at the same product, so ΔG — the overall
      free-energy change — is identical with or without the enzyme. What changes is

**A patient starts a cholesterol drug and within weeks their muscles ache and a
blood marker called CK climbs.** The drug is doing exactly what it was designed to
do — and that is the problem. To understand why, you need to understand what an
enzyme can and cannot do. By the end of this chapter, the muscle pain will make
mechanistic sense.

OMP decarboxylase, an enzyme your cells run constantly, speeds its reaction by a
factor of about 10¹⁷. Without it, that single step has a half-life of 78 million
years — longer than the time since the dinosaurs vanished. With it, the reaction
finishes in milliseconds. Enzymes are not gentle nudges. They are the difference
between a reaction that effectively never happens and one fast enough to keep you
alive.

## What an enzyme is, and how we file it

An enzyme is a reusable protein catalyst — with one important exception: some
enzymes are RNA (**ribozymes**), and the ribosome that builds every protein in
your body is itself one. Its activity depends on temperature, pH, and often on a
helper molecule, and its name usually ends in *-ase*.

There are seven official classes, sorted by the chemistry they do.
**Oxidoreductases** move electrons. **Transferases** move a chemical group from
one molecule to another. **Hydrolases** use water to break a bond. **Lyases** add
or remove groups across a double bond. **Isomerases** rearrange a molecule into
its isomer. **Ligases** join two molecules and spend ATP to do it.
**Translocases**, added to the list in 2018, move ions and molecules across
membranes. Each enzyme gets a four-part EC number: hexokinase is EC 2.7.1.1,
which reads as transferase, phosphate group, alcohol acceptor, first on the list.

Many enzymes cannot work alone. A **cofactor** is a small helper required for
activity, and it comes in two kinds: a **coenzyme** is an organic molecule,
usually derived from a vitamin (niacin becomes NAD⁺; thiamine becomes TPP), and a
**metal ion** is the inorganic kind (Zn²⁺, Mg²⁺). When a cofactor — organic or
metal — binds tightly and permanently rather than coming and going, we call it a
**prosthetic group**; the heme iron buried in catalase is the classic example.
The vocabulary that trips students up is the pair underneath this: an enzyme
missing its cofactor is an **apoenzyme** and it does not work; the assembled,
working complex is the **holoenzyme**. Apo means away; holo means whole.

## What a reaction will do, before how fast

Now separate two questions that students constantly merge. *Will* this reaction
happen? That is thermodynamics. *How fast* will it happen? That is kinetics. An
enzyme answers only the second, and seeing why requires the first.

The currency of thermodynamics is **Gibbs free energy**, G — the energy in a
system available to do work. What matters for a reaction is the change, ΔG, and
its sign tells you the direction. A negative ΔG means the reaction releases free
energy and runs on its own: it is **exergonic**, spontaneous. A positive ΔG means
the reaction needs an input of energy to go: it is **endergonic**. ATP hydrolysis
is the textbook exergonic reaction at ΔG°′ = −30.5 kJ/mol; forming
glucose-6-phosphate is the textbook endergonic one at +13.8 kJ/mol. When ΔG
reaches zero, the reaction sits at equilibrium and goes nowhere net.

One trap worth flagging now: exoTHERMIC and exoGONIC are not the same word.
Exothermic is about heat (ΔH); exergonic is about free energy (ΔG). A reaction can
release free energy without releasing much heat.

The sign of ΔG also tells you where equilibrium sits, through one of the most
useful equations in biochemistry:

> **ΔG°′ = −RT ln Keq**

Keq is just the ratio of products to reactants once the reaction settles. The
more negative ΔG°′, the larger Keq, and the further the reaction runs toward
products before stopping. Work an example: a reaction with ΔG°′ = −17 kJ/mol has a
Keq of about 950 — roughly a thousand-to-one in favor of products. The
relationship is logarithmic, so a modest change in free energy moves the
equilibrium a lot.

## What an enzyme changes — and what it cannot

An enzyme **cannot** change ΔG, ΔG°, or Keq. The destination of a reaction —
whether it favors products, and by how much — is set by thermodynamics, and no
catalyst touches it. What an enzyme changes is the *route, not the destination*:
it speeds the reaction's approach to that same equilibrium.

Think of a mountain between two valleys. The starting valley and the ending
valley sit at fixed elevations; that difference is ΔG. The enzyme does not lower
either valley. It digs a tunnel through the peak. Travelers move between the same
two points far faster, and they still end up exactly where thermodynamics said
they would.

The peak the enzyme tunnels through is the **activation barrier**, and to lower it
the enzyme exploits the highest, strangest point on the whole reaction.

## The transition state

Between substrate and product, every reaction passes through a **transition
state** (written X‡): a fleeting arrangement of atoms, neither starting material
nor product, with bonds half-broken and half-formed. It lives for femtoseconds
and sits at the top of the energy hill. The height of that hill above the
substrate is the **activation energy**, ΔG‡, and it sets the rate. Lower the hill
and the reaction speeds up.

This is the move enzymes make. The active site — a small cleft built from amino
acids pulled together from distant parts of the protein chain — is not shaped to
fit the substrate. It is shaped to fit the *transition state*. When the enzyme
grips that high-energy, distorted form most tightly, the energy released by
binding (the **binding energy**) pays down the activation barrier: ΔG‡(catalyzed)
= ΔG‡(uncatalyzed) − ΔG(binding).

Emil Fischer guessed at this in 1894 with his "lock and key" — enzyme and
substrate fitting like a key in a lock. He was close, and he distrusted his own
metaphor almost immediately, warning it could only be confirmed once someone
could hold a pure enzyme and see its shape. He was right to worry: a rigid lock
would bind the substrate most tightly, and enzymes don't. In 1958 Daniel Koshland
corrected it with **induced fit** — enzyme and substrate both change shape as they
meet, and the complementarity that matters is reached at the transition state.
Product fits the reshaped site worse, which is exactly why it lets go.

## How we measure it

The cleanest evidence that the active site is built for the transition state comes
from a simple comparison. Proline racemase binds its real substrate, L-proline,
with a Kd of 3.6 mM. Then chemists made a stable molecule, pyrrole-2-carboxylate,
shaped like the planar transition state the enzyme passes through. The enzyme
binds *that* with a Kd of 0.022 mM — 160 times tighter than the substrate it
actually works on. The enzyme grips the shape it is built to stabilize.

That principle is also a drug-design strategy, which brings us back to the case we
opened with. Statins are transition-state analogs of HMG-CoA reductase, the
rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol synthesis, and they bind it roughly 10,000
times more tightly than its natural substrate. They shut the pathway down, which
lowers cholesterol as intended. However, that same pathway also makes CoQ10 for
mitochondrial energy and the isoprenoids muscle cells need. Block it hard enough
and muscle mitochondria starve, cells break down, and CK leaks into the blood. The
aching muscles were the predictable cost of an enzyme inhibitor doing its job
exactly as designed.
