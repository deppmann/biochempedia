  - stem: "A researcher incubates purified dietary protein with water and a single enzyme, and finds the peptide bonds are cleaved with an -H added to one product and an -OH added to the other. What general reaction has occurred, and what role did water play?"
    choices:
      - "Hydrolysis, in which water acts as a reactant that is split across the broken bond"
      - "Condensation, in which water is released as a product"
      - "Oxidation, in which water donates electrons to the substrate"
      - "Isomerization, in which water acts only as a solvent and is not consumed"
    answer: 0
    rationale: "Digestion of all three macromolecule classes is hydrolysis: a water molecule is split across the bond, donating -H to one new end and -OH to the other. Water is the reactant, not merely the solvent. Condensation is the reverse (water released); the reaction is not a redox or isomerization event."
  - stem: "A newborn fails to thrive and is described as starving in the presence of food. Workup shows absent enteropeptidase activity at the intestinal brush border. Which combination of digestive functions is most directly impaired?"
    choices:
      - "Carbohydrate digestion only"
      - "Protein digestion only"
      - "Both protein and fat digestion, because trypsin activation is lost"
      - "Both carbohydrate and fat digestion, because amylase activation is lost"
    answer: 2
    rationale: "Enteropeptidase activates trypsinogen to trypsin, and trypsin is the master switch that activates chymotrypsinogen, procarboxypeptidase, proelastase, and prolipase. Without enteropeptidase, trypsin never forms, so both luminal protein digestion and lipase-dependent fat digestion fail. Amylase and disaccharidases are not trypsin-dependent, so carbohydrate digestion is spared."
  - stem: "Pepsinogen differs from the pancreatic zymogens in how it becomes active. What activates pepsinogen in the stomach?"
    choices:
      - "Cleavage by enteropeptidase at the brush border"
      - "Cleavage by trypsin in the small intestine"
      - "Phosphorylation by a gastric kinase"
      - "Autocatalytic activation triggered by low gastric pH"
    answer: 3
    rationale: "Pepsinogen is the exception to the proteolytic-cascade rule. Gastric acid (pH below about 3, generated by the H+/K+-ATPase) triggers autocatalytic activation: the low pH releases the pro-peptide and the first pepsin molecules then cleave more pepsinogen. Enteropeptidase and trypsin act on the pancreatic zymogens in the intestine, not on pepsinogen, and no phosphorylation is involved."
  - stem: "Pancreatic alpha-amylase is added to a sample of amylopectin (branched starch). Which products are expected, and why is free glucose not the main product?"
    choices:
      - "Mostly free glucose, because amylase cleaves every glycosidic bond"
      - "Oligosaccharides and limit dextrins, because amylase cleaves alpha-1,4 but not alpha-1,6 bonds"
      - "Cellobiose, because amylase preferentially cleaves beta-1,4 bonds"
      - "Only disaccharides, because amylase requires the brush border to function"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "Alpha-amylase hydrolyzes alpha-1,4 glycosidic bonds but cannot cleave the alpha-1,6 branch points, so it leaves oligosaccharides and limit dextrins rather than free glucose. Brush-border alpha-glucosidase and alpha-dextrinase finish the job later. Amylase is blind to beta linkages (so no cellobiose) and acts in the lumen, not only at the brush border."
  - stem: "Omeprazole is used to treat acid reflux by inhibiting the gastric H+/K+-ATPase. Which property best explains why omeprazole takes one to two days to reach full effect and to wear off?"
    choices:
      - "It is a reversible competitive inhibitor that washes out quickly"
      - "It blocks secretin release, which has a slow feedback loop"
      - "It forms a covalent, irreversible bond to the pump, so recovery requires synthesis of new pumps"
      - "It must be taken with food to be absorbed, delaying its onset"
    answer: 2
    rationale: "Omeprazole is an acid-activated prodrug that forms a covalent, irreversible (suicide) bond to the proton pump. Because the inhibited pumps cannot be reactivated, acid secretion recovers only as the cell synthesizes new pumps, which accounts for the delayed onset and offset. It is not a reversible competitive inhibitor, and it does not act through secretin or simple food-dependent absorption."

  - level: "highschool"
    label: "High school"
    text: "Digestion breaks the three big food molecules (protein, starch, fat) into small ones your gut can absorb (amino acids, sugars, fatty acids). The one reaction behind it all is hydrolysis: water splits a bond and caps the ends. Three enzyme teams do it: proteases on protein, amylases on starch, lipases on fat. The big safety idea is that your pancreas makes its protein-cutting enzymes in an OFF form called a zymogen, so they can't digest the pancreas itself. An enzyme at the gut wall turns on trypsin, and trypsin then flips on all the others, including the one that digests fat. Sugars get pumped in with sodium, and digested fat gets rebuilt and shipped out through the lymph instead of the blood."
  - level: "college"
    label: "College"
    text: "Digestion is hydrolysis applied to three bond classes: peptide (proteases), glycosidic (amylases/disaccharidases), ester (lipases). The unifying control problem is self-digestion, solved by zymogen storage in granules plus a strictly ordered activation cascade: enteropeptidase on the intestinal brush border converts trypsinogen to trypsin, which then activates chymotrypsinogen, procarboxypeptidase, proelastase, and prolipase. Pepsinogen is the exception, activated autocatalytically below pH ~3 by gastric acid from the H+/K+-ATPase. Protein is cut at three sites (lumen, brush border, cytoplasm); di/tripeptides enter via PepT1. Amylase cleaves only alpha-1,4 (hence starch yes, cellulose no, and limit dextrins as products), with brush-border disaccharidases finishing. Glucose/galactose enter by Na+-coupled SGLT1 (secondary active, ouabain-sensitive), fructose by GLUT5, all exit by GLUT2. Lipid requires bile-salt emulsification, interfacial lipase action (sn-1/sn-3, yielding 2-MAG + 2 FFA), micellar delivery, intracellular TAG re-synthesis, and chylomicron export via lymph. Clinical anchors: omeprazole (suicide inhibitor of the pump), GLP-1 agonists, celiac (autoimmune, proline-resistant peptides), lactase deficiency (osmotic), SGLT2 inhibitors."
  - level: "grad"
    label: "Graduate"
    text: "The architecture of digestion is a spatially and temporally segregated hydrolysis problem with a stringent containment requirement. Pro-segments occlude active sites and confine catalytic competence to the lumen; activation is a thresholded relay in which membrane-anchored enteropeptidase provides the spatial specificity (cleavage only at the absorptive surface) and trypsin provides amplification, autocatalytically reinforcing its own activation while transactivating the downstream pancreatic zymogens, including prolipase. This single point of control is why trypsinogen/enteropeptidase loss simultaneously cripples luminal proteolysis and lipolysis. Gastric pepsinogen escapes the relay via acid-driven autoactivation, a pH-dependent conformational release of the pro-peptide self-amplified once the first pepsin appears. Absorption thermodynamics matter: SGLT1 is a secondary active symporter whose driving force is the inwardly directed Na+ electrochemical gradient set by the basolateral Na+/K+-ATPase (hence ouabain sensitivity and the SGLT1/SGLT2 pharmacology), whereas GLUT5/GLUT2 are equilibrative. Lipid handling is governed by interfacial enzymology: lipase activity is a function of accessible lipid-water interface, so bile-salt emulsification is rate-determining, colipase relieves bile-salt inhibition of lipase, regiospecific sn-1/sn-3 cleavage spares the 2-monoacylglycerol, and the resynthesis/chylomicron/lacteal route reflects the impossibility of moving large hydrophobic aggregates through fenestrated blood capillaries. The historical throughline (Kühne, Payen, Northrop) is that each of these activities was abstracted from the organ into a defined molecule, the precondition for treating digestion as mechanism rather than vital appetite."

**Every meal you eat is a chemistry problem your body has to solve. How do you turn a pizza into you?** The pizza is built from someone else's molecules: wheat protein, starch, cheese fat, none of it yours and none of it small enough to cross into a cell. Before a single one of those atoms can enter glycolysis or any pathway that follows, it has to be cut down to a monomer your gut can absorb. That cutting is digestion, and this chapter is the gateway to all of metabolism, because nothing downstream happens until the meal is taken apart.

The remarkable part is how few ideas you need to understand it. Three macromolecule classes, three bond types, three enzyme families, and underneath all of them one reaction.

## One reaction, run three ways

The single chemistry of digestion is **hydrolysis**: a water molecule splits a covalent bond and caps the two new ends, donating an -H to one side and an -OH to the other. Water here is not the solvent. It is the reactant. Run that reaction on the three bonds that hold our food together and you have the whole chapter. **Proteases** hydrolyze the peptide bonds in protein. **Amylases** hydrolyze the glycosidic bonds in starch. **Lipases** hydrolyze the ester bonds in fat. Same scissors, three different threads.

Mechanical and chemical digestion work as a team, and the division of labor is clean. Your teeth and stomach muscles crush; they break nothing covalent, but by shredding food they multiply its surface area so enzymes have more bonds exposed to attack. The enzymes then dissolve the bonds themselves. Crush, then dissolve.

## The self-digestion problem

Here is the puzzle that organizes the protein section. Your pancreas is built of protein, and its job is to manufacture proteases powerful enough to take other proteins apart. So why doesn't it digest itself?

The answer is one of the most elegant safety designs in biology. Most digestive enzymes are made and stored as inactive precursors called **zymogens** (or proenzymes), each carrying an extra stretch of polypeptide that physically plugs its own active site. The enzyme is built switched off. It travels through the cell that made it, sits in a secretory granule, and stays harmless until it reaches the place where digestion is supposed to happen.

Activation is a deliberate, ordered cascade. Anchored to the lining of the small intestine sits **enteropeptidase**. It makes a single cut in trypsinogen, converting it to **trypsin**, and trypsin is the master switch of the whole system. Trypsin then activates the rest: chymotrypsinogen to chymotrypsin, procarboxypeptidase to carboxypeptidase, proelastase to elastase, and prolipase. Notice what that means. One activating cut, made at the gut wall, turns on protein digestion and fat digestion at the same time. Knock out trypsin and you lose both, which is exactly the lesson the interactive above is built to make you feel.

There is one stubborn exception. **Pepsinogen**, the stomach's lone zymogen, is not switched on by another enzyme. It activates itself, **autocatalytically**, when the surrounding acid drops below about pH 3. The first few pepsin molecules then cut more pepsinogen, and the reaction snowballs. Acid is the trigger, not proteolysis.

## Protein: three places it gets cut

Protein digestion is the story of three sites. The mouth contributes only mechanical work; there is no significant salivary protease. The real chemistry starts in the **stomach**, where the gastric **H+/K+-ATPase**, the proton pump, drives the pH down to 1.5 to 2.5. That acid does two jobs at once: it denatures dietary proteins, unfolding them so their peptide bonds are reachable, and it activates pepsinogen to pepsin.

That proton pump is also a clinical target you have almost certainly encountered. Acid reflux (GERD) is treated with **omeprazole**, a prodrug with a clever trick: it stays inert until it reaches the acid of the stomach, where it is activated and forms a **covalent, irreversible** bond to the pump. It is a suicide inhibitor. Because the bond cannot be undone, the drug takes a day or two to reach full effect and a day or two to wear off, since relief depends on the cell synthesizing brand-new pumps.

The partly digested protein then enters the small intestine, where two hormones coordinate the response. **Secretin** triggers a flood of bicarbonate that neutralizes the stomach acid. **CCK** (cholecystokinin) triggers release of the pancreatic enzymes and squeezes bile from the gallbladder. Now the second site opens: trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase, and carboxypeptidase chop proteins into short oligopeptides in the lumen. The third site is the **brush border** and the cell itself. Brush-border peptidases trim peptides to amino acids plus di- and tripeptides; the small peptides are absorbed intact through the transporter **PepT1** and finished off by peptidases inside the cell, and the freed amino acids exit to the blood through antiporters. Lumen, brush border, cytoplasm: proteolysis happens three times over.

Two clinical asides land here. **GLP-1**, an incretin hormone from intestinal L-cells, slows gastric emptying and signals fullness; the blockbuster drug **semaglutide** (Ozempic, Wegovy) amplifies that signal, and the famous nausea is not a bug, the slowed stomach is the mechanism. And **celiac disease** is an autoimmune reaction, not an allergy, to proline- and glutamine-rich gluten peptides that resist complete digestion. Proline kinks the backbone in a way proteases cannot easily access, so fragments survive intact and provoke the immune system.

## Carbohydrate and lipid: the easy lane and the hard one

Starch digestion begins in the mouth with salivary **alpha-amylase** and continues with pancreatic alpha-amylase, but amylase has a blind spot worth memorizing. It cleaves only the **alpha-1,4** glycosidic bonds, not the alpha-1,6 branch points, so its products are oligosaccharides and stubby **limit dextrins**, not free glucose. This blind spot answers a classic question: why do we digest starch but not cellulose, when both are glucose polymers? Starch uses alpha linkages and cellulose uses beta linkages, and our amylase sees only alpha. Brush-border enzymes finish the job: alpha-glucosidase and alpha-dextrinase, plus sucrase and lactase splitting the disaccharides. (Lactase deficiency causes an **osmotic**, not immune, diarrhea, the mirror image of celiac.) Absorption sorts the monosaccharides by transporter: **SGLT1** is secondary active transport, coupling glucose and galactose uptake to the sodium gradient (poison the Na+/K+-ATPase with ouabain and it stalls); **GLUT5** carries fructose by facilitated diffusion; **GLUT2** is the basolateral door they all leave through. Its kidney cousin SGLT2 is the target of the gliflozin diabetes drugs, which dump glucose into the urine.

Lipid is the hard lane, complicated by one problem: fat will not dissolve in the watery gut. The solution has four steps. First, **bile salts emulsify** the fat the way soap cuts grease, breaking it into tiny droplets with enormous surface area. Second, pancreatic **lipase** works at the droplet's surface, cleaving the sn-1 and sn-3 ester bonds to leave a 2-monoacylglycerol and two free fatty acids. Third, those products are packaged into **micelles** for delivery to the cell. Fourth, and strangely, inside the enterocyte the triacylglycerols are **rebuilt** from their parts, like IKEA furniture reassembled after shipping, then exported as **chylomicrons**. These particles are too big for blood capillaries, so they take the scenic route through the lymphatic lacteals. Only lipids travel by lymph.

You now have the full machine: a meal entering as foreign polymers and leaving as amino acids, monosaccharides, and fatty acids ready for the cell. Those monosaccharides are where the next chapter begins, as glucose steps onto the conveyor belt of **glycolysis**.
