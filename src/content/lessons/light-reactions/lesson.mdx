summary: "The light reactions are the act of a two-act play: they convert photons into two energy currencies — ATP and NADPH — that the Calvin cycle (CH23) will spend on CO2. The single organizing idea is the Z-scheme: two photon-driven uphill boosts (at Photosystem II and Photosystem I) separated by downhill electron flow, wired so that water is split, oxygen is released, a proton gradient is built, and NADPH is made. One molecular innovation, the Mn4CaO5 cluster that pries electrons off water, oxygenated the planet."

For over a decade the field dismissed chemiosmosis as theology. techniques:
  - name: "X-ray crystallography of membrane proteins"
    blurb: "Membrane proteins were long thought too greasy to crystallize, hiding roughly a third of all proteins from structure determination. The same approach later gave us PSII, PSI, cytochrome b6f, and ATP synthase."
  - name: "Absorption and action spectroscopy"
    blurb: "Shine monochromatic light across the visible spectrum and measure either how much a pigment absorbs (absorption spectrum) or how fast photosynthesis runs (action spectrum). This is how chlorophyll a and b were distinguished, how the special-pair reaction centers were named for their absorption maxima (P680, P700), and how accessory carotenoids were shown to fill the blue-green gap chlorophyll misses."
  - name: "The acid-bath / pH-jump experiment"
    blurb: "Jagendorf and Uribe soaked chloroplasts at pH 4, then dropped them into pH 8 buffer in total darkness. The artificial proton gradient alone drove ATP synthesis with no light and no electron transport — the cleanest proof of Mitchell's chemiosmotic theory, showing that a proton gradient across the thylakoid membrane is the energy currency that powers ATP synthase."
  - name: "Isolated-chloroplast photophosphorylation assays"
    blurb: "Arnon's group stripped chloroplasts to naked lamellar membranes so ADP and phosphate could reach the thylakoids, then caught them making ATP from light alone. Running the reaction without oxygen (and erasing the apparent O2 requirement with added flavin mononucleotide) proved the ATP came from light, not from contaminating mitochondria — and separated cyclic from non-cyclic photophosphorylation."

practiceQuestions:
  - stem: "During light harvesting, a photon is absorbed by a chlorophyll in the antenna complex, well away from the reaction center. How does its energy reach the reaction center, and what physically moves?"
    choices:
      - "The excited electron itself travels from the antenna chlorophyll all the way to the reaction center"
      - "The energy is transferred pigment-to-pigment by resonance, while the electron stays on its original pigment"
      - "The photon is re-emitted and physically re-absorbed by the reaction-center chlorophyll"
      - "Protons are pumped from pigment to pigment, carrying the energy as a chemical gradient"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "In the antenna, excitation energy migrates by resonance energy transfer from pigment to pigment toward the reaction center; the electrons do not travel. An electron transfer (charge separation) occurs only once the energy reaches the reaction center. The idea that the same electron travels from antenna to acceptor is the chapter's most common misconception. Re-emission/re-absorption and proton pumping are not how antenna energy migration works."
  - stem: "A botanist treats isolated thylakoids with a drug that completely inactivates the Mn4CaO5 oxygen-evolving complex. Assuming all other components are intact, what is the most direct consequence?"
    choices:
      - "NADPH production rises because electrons no longer have to come from water"
      - "Cyclic photophosphorylation stops but oxygen evolution continues normally"
      - "Photosystem II can no longer obtain replacement electrons, so O2 evolution ceases and linear electron flow stalls"
      - "ATP synthase reverses and begins hydrolyzing ATP to pump protons out of the lumen"
    answer: 2
    rationale: "The Mn4CaO5 cluster (oxygen-evolving complex) is the source of electrons that re-reduce P680+ after charge separation. Inactivate it and PSII cannot replace the electrons it loses, so water is not split, no O2 is released, and linear (non-cyclic) electron flow from water to NADPH stalls. This is the 'break PSII' thought experiment: PSI may still function briefly but the electron supply is cut off. NADPH would fall, not rise."
  - stem: "On a Z-scheme plotted with reduction potential on the vertical axis, where do the two large UPHILL (toward more negative potential) jumps occur?"
    choices:
      - "At cytochrome b6f and at ferredoxin-NADP+ reductase"
      - "At plastocyanin and at plastoquinone"
      - "At the absorption of light by P680 (→P680*) and by P700 (→P700*)"
      - "At the oxygen-evolving complex and at NADPH formation"
    answer: 2
    rationale: "The two upstrokes in the Z-scheme correspond to the two photon-absorption events: P680→P680* in Photosystem II and P700→P700* in Photosystem I. Light energy boosts each electron to a much more negative (higher-energy) potential. Cytochrome b6f, plastocyanin, plastoquinone, and FNR all lie on downhill (more positive, or energy-releasing) segments between or after the upstrokes; the OEC and NADPH formation are endpoints, not the photon-driven upstrokes."
  - stem: "Which statement correctly contrasts chlorophyll a with pheophytin, and why does the difference matter biologically?"
    choices:
      - "Pheophytin is chlorophyll a with its central Mg2+ replaced by two protons, and it serves as the first electron acceptor in Photosystem II"
      - "Pheophytin is chlorophyll a with an extra Mg2+, making it a stronger light absorber than chlorophyll"

    rationale: "Pheophytin is chlorophyll lacking its central Mg2+ (two protons take its place) — the same conversion that dulls overcooked greens. In PSII, pheophytin is the first electron acceptor downstream of the excited special pair P680. It is not an extra-Mg variant, it retains its phytol tail, and it does not contain iron or carry oxygen (that confuses it with heme)."
  - stem: "The thylakoid lumen accumulates protons during the light reactions. Which TWO processes are the major direct sources of those lumenal protons?"
    choices:
      - "Glycolysis in the stroma and the citric acid cycle"
      - "The water-splitting reaction at PSII and the cytochrome b6f Q-cycle"
      - "ATP synthase running forward and FNR reducing NADP+"
      - "Carotenoid quenching (NPQ) and chlorophyll fluorescence"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "Two processes load protons into the lumen: splitting water at PSII releases 4 H+ into the lumen per O2, and the cytochrome b6f Q-cycle pumps additional protons (~4 H+ per 2 electrons) across into the lumen. Together they build the proton-motive force that powers ATP synthase. Glycolysis and the citric acid cycle are not chloroplast light reactions; ATP synthase consumes the gradient and FNR acts on the stromal side; NPQ and fluorescence dissipate energy as heat/light, not protons into the lumen."
mcatQuestions:
  - stem: "A researcher adds DCMU, an herbicide that blocks electron transfer from QA to QB in Photosystem II, to a suspension of actively photosynthesizing chloroplasts in the light. Compared with untreated chloroplasts, which set of changes is expected?"
    choices:
      - "O2 evolution increases and NADPH production increases"
      - "O2 evolution decreases, the lumen proton gradient diminishes, and NADPH production falls"
      - "O2 evolution is unchanged but NADPH production doubles"
      - "O2 evolution decreases while NADPH production increases sharply"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "Blocking QA→QB cuts the linear electron flow downstream of PSII. With no electrons leaving PSII, the water-splitting reaction backs up and slows, so O2 evolution falls; fewer electrons reach cytochrome b6f, so Q-cycle proton pumping and the water-derived lumen protons both drop, shrinking the proton gradient; and electrons no longer reach PSI/FNR, so NADPH production falls. All outputs of linear flow decline together — the options showing any increase are inconsistent with a block early in the chain."
  - stem: "Under conditions where a chloroplast must regenerate the acceptor for carbon fixation but already has plenty of NADPH, it shifts toward cyclic electron flow around Photosystem I. Which outcome best characterizes this mode relative to non-cyclic (linear) flow?"
    choices:
      - "It produces NADPH and O2 but no ATP"
      - "It produces ATP without net production of NADPH or O2"
      - "It produces equal amounts of ATP and NADPH but no O2"
      - "It consumes ATP to drive reverse electron flow and reduce NADP+"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "In cyclic photophosphorylation, electrons leaving PSI (via ferredoxin) return to the cytochrome b6f complex and back to PSI rather than going to NADP+. This still pumps protons (making ATP) but produces no net NADPH and does not involve water-splitting, so no O2 is released. It raises the ATP:NADPH ratio to meet demands such as the Calvin cycle's need for extra ATP. The other options describe linear flow or thermodynamically backward processes."
  - stem: "The Mn4CaO5 cluster of the oxygen-evolving complex must absorb the effects of four sequential photon-driven oxidations (the S0→S4 Kok cycle) before it releases a single O2 molecule. What does this requirement most directly explain about oxygen evolution when dark-adapted chloroplasts are given very brief, evenly spaced flashes of light?"
    choices:
      - "O2 is released in equal amounts after every single flash starting with the first"
      - "Maximum O2 release occurs in a pattern peaking on about every fourth flash, after the first few flashes"
      - "O2 is released only once, after a single saturating flash, then never again"
      - "O2 release decreases steadily with each flash regardless of timing"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "Because one O2 requires four sequential one-electron oxidations (S0 through S4, with S4 releasing O2 and resetting to S0), single-turnover flashes produce O2 in a period-four pattern: peaks recur about every fourth flash. The classic Joliot/Kok flash experiments showed exactly this oscillation (initially peaking on the third flash because clusters start distributed among S-states, then damping toward a steady period-of-four). Equal O2 per flash, a single burst, or monotonic decline would all be inconsistent with a four-step accumulation mechanism."

explainLevels:
  - level: "eli5"
    label: "Like I'm 5"
    text: "Plants eat sunlight. Tiny green dots inside a leaf, called chlorophyll, catch the light like a catcher's mitt. The light gives the leaf energy, and the leaf uses that energy to do something amazing: it pulls water apart. When it pulls water apart, it lets go of the oxygen — and that oxygen is what you breathe! The leaf keeps the leftover energy as two little battery packs (called ATP and NADPH) that it will use later to build sugar for food. So a leaf turns sunshine and water into the air you breathe and the food it eats."
  - level: "highschool"
    label: "High school"
    text: "The light reactions happen in the thylakoid membranes inside a chloroplast. Pigments like chlorophyll absorb light; the energy from a photon excites an electron. Most pigments are antennas that funnel the energy to a special spot called a reaction center, where the electron is actually handed off. Photosystem II goes first: its excited chlorophyll (P680) is such a strong electron-grabber that it splits water, which releases the oxygen you breathe and dumps protons inside the thylakoid. The electron then flows downhill to cytochrome b6f (pumping more protons), then to Photosystem I (P700), where a second photon boosts it high enough to make NADPH. The protons trapped inside power ATP synthase. Picture the path as a 'Z': two upward kicks from light separated by a downhill slide. The products, ATP and NADPH, get spent later in the Calvin cycle to build sugar."
  - level: "college"
    label: "College"
    text: "In the thylakoid membrane, light drives linear (non-cyclic) electron flow described by the Z-scheme. A photon's energy migrates by resonance through ~200–300 antenna pigments to a reaction center, where charge separation occurs — only there does an electron actually transfer. PSII's P680 is excited to P680*, which hands its electron to pheophytin; the P680+ left behind (~+1.2 V) is the strongest biological oxidant, ripping electrons from water at the Mn4CaO5 oxygen-evolving complex via the S-state (Kok) cycle: 2 H2O + 4 photons → O2 + 4 H+ (lumen) + 4 e-. Electrons pass P680 → pheophytin → QA → QB/plastoquinol → cytochrome b6f (Q-cycle pumps ~4 H+ per 2 e-) → plastocyanin → PSI. A second photon excites P700 → P700*, lifting electrons to A0 → A1 → Fe-S clusters → ferredoxin → FNR, which reduces NADP+ to NADPH on the stromal side (2 Fd_red + NADP+ + H+ → 2 Fd_ox + NADPH). The lumen proton gradient (water-splitting + Q-cycle) is the proton-motive force that drives ATP synthase. Cyclic photophosphorylation loops PSI → Fd → b6f to make ATP alone when NADPH demand is low."
  - level: "grad"
    label: "Graduate"
    text: "Oxygenic photosynthesis chains two photosystems in series precisely because no single photon (~1.8 eV at 680 nm) spans the ~1.1 V gap from the H2O/O2 couple (+0.82 V) to the NADP+/NADPH couple (−0.32 V) while also supplying overpotential and a proton-motive force. PSII (D1/D2 core, homologous to the purple-bacterial RC) generates the most oxidizing species in biology: P680•+ (the cation left after charge separation) at ~+1.2 V, whose redox tuning by the protein environment lets the Mn4CaO5 cluster perform four sequential one-electron, proton-coupled oxidations of water across the Kok S-state cycle, storing oxidizing equivalents until S4→S0 releases O2. Charge separation is kinetically stabilized by the protein's spatial cascade of acceptors (pheophytin → QA → QB), outrunning recombination. Cytochrome b6f, the homolog of the cytochrome bc1 complex, runs a Q-cycle that doubles proton translocation per electron pair through the Rieske 2Fe-2S/cytochrome f high-potential chain and the b-hemes/low-potential chain. PSI then re-energizes electrons to reduce ferredoxin (~−0.42 V), and FNR (a plant-type FAD flavoenzyme, the prototype of its own reductase family) couples two sequential one-electron Fd oxidations to a hydride transfer onto NADP+. The thylakoid pmf is overwhelmingly ΔpH (the membrane is permeable to counterions, collapsing ΔΨ), and that ΔpH both powers CF1Fo ATP synthase and triggers photoprotective NPQ via the xanthophyll cycle and PsbS. The branch point between linear and cyclic electron flow (PGR5/PGRL1- and NDH-dependent) tunes the ATP:NADPH output ratio to match Calvin-cycle stoichiometry (~3 ATP : 2 NADPH per CO2), a regulatory problem central to engineering improved photosynthetic efficiency."

**Every oxygen molecule you have ever breathed was once part of a water molecule, split apart at a single protein in a leaf.** That protein is Photosystem II, and the moment it learned to pry electrons off water — roughly two and a half billion years ago — it rewrote the chemistry of the entire planet. The light reactions are how a chloroplast turns sunlight into two things a cell can actually spend: ATP and NADPH. Photosynthesis is a two-act play. Act one, the light reactions, makes the coins. Act two, the Calvin cycle, spends them on carbon dioxide. This chapter is act one.

The whole story comes down to one diagram, the Z-scheme: two uphill jumps powered by light, with a downhill electron slide in between. Get that shape into your head and the rest is bookkeeping.

## Where it happens: a captured bacterium

The chloroplast is a double-membrane organelle, and that second membrane is a fossil. Inside the inner membrane sits the **stroma**, the soup where carbon gets fixed later. Floating in the stroma are flattened sacs called **thylakoid membranes**, stacked like poker chips into **grana**, each sac enclosing an interior space called the **lumen**. The light reactions run in and across the thylakoid membrane, and where the protons end up — the lumen — is the whole point, so keep that geography straight.

Why two membranes? Because about 1.5 billion years ago a eukaryotic cell engulfed a free-living cyanobacterium and never digested it. The evidence is hard to argue with: the double membrane (engulfer plus engulfed), the chloroplast's own DNA, and bacterial-style 70S ribosomes inside it. Mitochondria carry the same scar from a separate engulfment. You are, in a real sense, a colony of domesticated bacteria.

## A photon's three fates

Light is energy in discrete packets, photons, each carrying E = hν. When a pigment absorbs one, an electron jumps to an excited state, and that excitation can go one of three ways.

It can simply fall back down, releasing the energy as fluorescence or heat. It can hand its **energy** to a neighboring pigment without the electron going anywhere — **resonance energy transfer**. Or, at one special spot, it can hand off the **electron itself**.

The second fate is where most students stumble, so slow down here. A light-harvesting complex packs 200 to 300 pigment molecules around a reaction center like a satellite dish. When a photon lands anywhere on that dish, the *energy* hops pigment to pigment toward the center, a bucket brigade in which no bucket actually moves. **Energy travels by resonance; the electron transfers only at the reaction center.** Confuse those two and the rest of the chapter dissolves.

At the reaction center, the third fate fires. An excited donor (D\*) gives its electron to an acceptor (A), creating a separated pair of charges, D⁺ and A⁻. The surrounding protein holds them apart long enough that the electron can't snap back. That **photoinduced charge separation** is the actual conversion of light into chemistry, and Deisenhofer and Michel won a Nobel for being the first to see its machinery, atom by atom.

## The pigment that runs the whole show

The primary pigment is **chlorophyll**, a substituted tetrapyrrole built on the same ring skeleton as heme — but with a magnesium ion at its center instead of iron, plus a long greasy phytol tail that anchors it in the membrane. Chlorophyll a and b differ slightly and absorb slightly different colors, which is why leaves harvest a broad slice of sunlight. The reaction-center chlorophylls come as special pairs named for the wavelength they absorb best: **P680** in Photosystem II, **P700** in Photosystem I.

A small kitchen experiment makes the chemistry tangible. Boil greens too long and they go drab olive: acid swaps the central Mg²⁺ for two protons, converting chlorophyll into **pheophytin**, a sort of zombie chlorophyll. That same Mg-stripped molecule is no accident in the leaf — pheophytin is the very first electron acceptor in PSII.

The accessory pigments earn their keep too. **Carotenoids** absorb blue-green light chlorophyll misses, and they double as the plant's built-in sunscreen: when light is too intense, they perform **nonphotochemical quenching (NPQ)**, dumping dangerous excess energy as harmless heat. Those carotenoids were in the leaf all along, masked by chlorophyll. Autumn just removes the green and lets the reds and golds show.

## The Z-scheme: two upstrokes and a downhill

Now the electron path, in order, because order is everything. Water → **PSII (P680)** → pheophytin → plastoquinone (QA → QB → QH₂) → **cytochrome b6f** → **plastocyanin** (a copper protein) → **PSI (P700)** → A₀ → A₁ (phylloquinone) → iron-sulfur clusters → **ferredoxin** → ferredoxin-NADP⁺ reductase (**FNR**, a FAD flavoprotein) → **NADPH**.

Plot that on an energy axis and it draws a Z. A photon hits P680 and kicks its electron sharply uphill (upstroke one); the electron then slides downhill through the carriers to plastocyanin; a second photon hits P700 and kicks it uphill again (upstroke two); then it runs downhill once more to NADPH. Two photon absorptions, two upstrokes, separated by downhill flow. The interactive above lets you fire each photon and watch the electron climb and fall.

The genius is at the bottom of the first upstroke. The instant excited P680\* hands its electron to pheophytin, it leaves behind **P680⁺ — at about +1.2 V, the strongest biological oxidant known** — electron-hungry enough to rip electrons off water itself. That happens at the **oxygen-evolving complex**, a cluster of four manganese atoms, one calcium, and five oxygens (Mn₄CaO₅). It can't grab all four electrons at once, so it counts: the **S-state (Kok) cycle** ratchets from S0 to S4, accumulating oxidizing power one photon at a time. At S4 it snaps, releasing O₂ and resetting. The net PSII reaction: **2 H₂O + 4 photons → O₂ + 4 H⁺ (into the lumen) + 4 e⁻**.

Those lumen protons are half the energy story. Cytochrome b6f adds the rest: like Complex III in mitochondria, it runs a Q-cycle that pumps roughly 4 more H⁺ into the lumen per pair of electrons. At the top of the second upstroke, P700\* lifts electrons high enough to reduce ferredoxin, biology's strongest soluble reducing agent, and FNR uses it to make NADPH on the stromal side, exactly where the Calvin cycle waits.

A worthwhile pause: what if PSI worked but PSII didn't? Toggle "Break PSII" in the widget and watch. PSI keeps lifting electrons, but nothing refills the supply from water. The chain starves, no O₂ appears, everything stalls. PSII acts first despite its name — the photosystems were simply discovered backwards.

## From gradient to ATP, and the planet it made

We now have a thylakoid lumen stuffed with protons, from water-splitting and from the Q-cycle. That gradient is potential energy, and in the next chapter it drives ATP synthase by chemiosmosis, the same proton-gradient logic Peter Mitchell proposed for mitochondria, run here in a chloroplast. Arnon showed isolated chloroplasts can make ATP two ways: **non-cyclic** photophosphorylation (the full linear path, yielding ATP, NADPH, and O₂) and **cyclic** photophosphorylation (electrons loop PSI → ferredoxin → b6f and back, making ATP only — handy when the cell needs ATP more than reducing power).

Step back and the scale is staggering. That one molecular innovation, the manganese cluster that splits water, flooded the ancient atmosphere with oxygen. The iron dissolved in the oceans rusted out as banded iron formations, an ozone layer formed, aerobic respiration became possible, and complex life followed. The Great Oxidation Event traces back to a protein in a pond. As Szent-Györgyi put it on Chris's slide, "What drives life is a little electrical current, kept up by a little sunshine."

With the coins minted — ATP and NADPH in the stroma — we are ready for act two. In **CH23, the Calvin cycle**, you'll watch the cell spend exactly these currencies to pull carbon out of the air and build sugar.
