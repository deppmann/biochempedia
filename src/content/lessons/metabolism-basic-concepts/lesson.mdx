summary: "Metabolism is an energy economy, and this chapter teaches its grammar before any pathway teaches its vocabulary. A cell earns ATP by burning fuel (catabolism) and spends it to build, move, and pump (anabolism); the two are coupled through shared intermediates, run on a small set of activated carriers, and are throttled by the cell's energy charge. Get these five ideas — convergence on acetyl-CoA, reaction coupling, ATP's middle-of-the-ladder transfer potential, the activated carriers, and energy-charge regulation — and every later chapter becomes bookkeeping."

    story: "A refugee from Nazi Germany, Krebs learned to read the Warburg manometer in Otto Warburg's Berlin laboratory under the creed 'measure, do not speculate.' At Freiburg in 1931 he discovered the urea cycle when a pinch of ornithine kept driving urea output without being consumed — teaching him to look for the molecule that comes out unchanged: a wheel. Dismissed under the 1933 civil-service law, he carried 24 Warburg manometers in 16 crates to England. Shared the 1953 Nobel with Fritz Lipmann, whose coenzyme A delivers the two-carbon fragment into Krebs's wheel."
  - name: "Fritz Lipmann"
    dates: "1899-1986"
    contribution: "Established ATP's terminal phosphate bond as the cell's universal energy currency (introducing the ~P 'squiggle' and the concept of 'group potential' = phosphoryl-transfer potential), and discovered Coenzyme A, the activated acyl-group carrier built around the vitamin pantothenate. Both ideas are core CH15 content (ATP as currency; CoA as activated carrier; phosphoryl-transfer potential)."
    profileSourceAnchor: "The Molecule Hunters (v10) — Fritz Lipmann Editor's Profile (quotes grep-confirmed)."
    quotes:
      - "group potential"
      - "subconsciously mapped out there"
    story: "On a Vermont lakeside vacation in spring 1940, Lipmann turned over a single idea: that energy in living cells flows through one common channel, the phosphate bond at the end of ATP. His 1941 review 'Metabolic Generation and Utilization of Phosphate Bond Energy' coined 'group potential' — what mattered was not bond strength but how much more stable the products were — and marked the special phosphates with a tilde, ~P. Hunting a heat-stable cofactor that let pigeon-liver extracts acetylate sulfonamides, his group ran liters of liver mash through columns and named what they found coenzyme A ('A' for acetylation); its heart turned out to be pantothenic acid, an ordinary B vitamin, and it worked through a single reactive thiol forming energy-rich thioesters. Born in Königsberg, he trained under Meyerhof, left Germany ahead of the Nazis via Copenhagen, and shared the 1953 Nobel with Krebs — the two halves of metabolism's machine clicking together like a buckle."

techniques:
  - name: "Phosphoryl-transfer potential (the ΔG°' of hydrolysis)"
    blurb: "Rank a phosphorylated compound by how much free energy its hydrolysis releases under standard conditions. Measured against a common reference, these values build the ladder that tells you which molecule can phosphorylate which: PEP and creatine phosphate sit above ATP and can charge ADP; glucose-6-phosphate sits below and cannot. The whole logic of substrate-level phosphorylation falls out of this one measured quantity."
  - name: "Energy charge"
    blurb: "A single number, ([ATP] + 0.5[ADP]) / ([ATP] + [ADP] + [AMP]), that reports how 'charged' the cell's adenylate pool is, normally near 0.85–0.90. Because adenylate kinase interconverts the three nucleotides, a small drop in ATP produces a large spike in AMP, so energy charge is a sensitive, dimensionless readout of metabolic state — the experimental basis for understanding AMPK signaling."
  - name: "Isotopic and PET tracing of fuel use"
    blurb: "Label a fuel carbon and follow where it lands, or scan a patient with an 18F-glucose analog and watch which tissues take up sugar fastest. Tracing is how convergence on acetyl-CoA was established and how the Warburg effect is read clinically — tumors light up on a PET scan because they run glycolysis hard even when oxygen is plentiful."

practiceQuestions:
  - stem: "A reaction A → B has ΔG°' = +14 kJ/mol and will not proceed on its own. In the cell it is coupled to ATP hydrolysis (ΔG°' = −30.5 kJ/mol) through a shared intermediate. What is the net ΔG°' of the coupled process, and will it now proceed?"
    choices:
      - "+44.5 kJ/mol; it still will not proceed"
      - "−16.5 kJ/mol; it will now proceed"
      - "−30.5 kJ/mol; it proceeds because ATP overrides A → B"
      - "+14 kJ/mol; coupling does not change ΔG°'"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "Coupling works because ΔG°' values are additive when reactions share a common intermediate: +14 + (−30.5) = −16.5 kJ/mol, which is negative, so the coupled process is now spontaneous. The +44.5 value comes from subtracting instead of adding and is wrong. ATP does not 'override' the other reaction; the two free energies simply sum. And coupling absolutely does change the net ΔG°' — that is the entire mechanism."
  - stem: "Which statement correctly explains why ATP hydrolysis is exergonic (ΔG°' ≈ −30.5 kJ/mol)?"
    choices:
      - "The terminal phosphate bond physically stores energy that is released when it breaks"
      - "The products (ADP + Pi) are more stable than ATP due to charge relief, resonance, entropy, and hydration"
      - "ATP is the most reactive phosphate in the cell and hydrolyzes the fastest"
      - "The bond between adenine and ribose is a high-energy bond"
      - "Hydrolysis consumes water, which is thermodynamically favorable on its own"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "The energy is not stored in a special bond. Hydrolysis is favorable because the products ADP and Pi are more stable than ATP — relief of electrostatic repulsion, better resonance stabilization of released phosphate, increased entropy, and improved hydration. ATP is actually kinetically stable (slow to hydrolyze without an enzyme), so it is not the fastest-reacting phosphate. The adenine–ribose bond is irrelevant to the energetics of phosphoryl transfer, and water consumption alone does not account for the free-energy change."
  - stem: "On the phosphoryl-transfer ladder, which compound can directly phosphorylate ADP to form ATP?"
    choices:
      - "Glucose-6-phosphate"
      - "AMP"
      - "Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)"
      - "ATP itself"
    answer: 2
    rationale: "A phosphoryl transfer is spontaneous only when it runs downhill — from a compound of higher transfer potential to one of lower. PEP sits above ATP on the ladder (more negative hydrolysis ΔG°'), so it can hand a phosphate down to ADP and make ATP. Glucose-6-phosphate and AMP both sit below ATP and cannot charge ADP. ATP phosphorylating ADP to make ATP is meaningless (no net change in transfer potential)."
  - stem: "A cell's adenylate pool is measured as [ATP] = 8, [ADP] = 1, [AMP] = 1 (relative units). What is its energy charge, and what response would you expect?"
    choices:
      - "≈ 0.85; the cell favors building (anabolism) and AMPK is largely off"
      - "≈ 0.50; the cell is energy-starved and triggers strong AMPK catabolism"
      - "≈ 0.85; AMPK is fully active and shuts down all biosynthesis"
      - "≈ 0.95; the cell halts catabolism entirely"
    answer: 0
    rationale: "Energy charge = ([ATP] + 0.5[ADP]) / ([ATP] + [ADP] + [AMP]) = (8 + 0.5) / 10 = 0.85. That is squarely in the normal resting range (~0.85–0.90), where the cell is well-charged, AMPK is largely inactive, and anabolic (building) pathways are favored. The 0.50 value would require far more AMP/ADP. A charge of 0.85 does not represent starvation, and 0.95 with a total catabolic halt does not match these concentrations."
  - stem: "Which activated carrier is matched correctly to the kind of group it delivers and its vitamin precursor?"
    choices:
      - "NADP+/NADPH — delivers acyl groups — derived from niacin"
      - "Coenzyme A — delivers acyl groups via a thioester — derived from pantothenate"
      - "FAD/FADH2 — delivers phosphoryl groups — derived from thiamine"
      - "ATP — delivers electrons for biosynthesis — derived from riboflavin"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "Coenzyme A carries acyl groups through a high-transfer-potential thioester linkage and is built around pantothenate (vitamin B5) — the pairing tested by PKAN, a pantothenate kinase deficiency. NADP+/NADPH carries electrons for reductive biosynthesis (not acyl groups). FAD/FADH2 carries electrons (riboflavin-derived), not phosphoryl groups. ATP carries phosphoryl groups, not biosynthetic electrons, and is not riboflavin-derived."
mcatQuestions:
  - stem: "An enzyme couples the synthesis of compound X (X-formation alone: ΔG°' = +22 kJ/mol) to the hydrolysis of ATP to ADP + Pi (ΔG°' = −30.5 kJ/mol). A researcher engineers a mutant enzyme that still catalyzes X-formation and still hydrolyzes ATP, but at two physically separate active sites with no shared intermediate. Compared with the wild-type enzyme, the mutant will most likely:"
    choices:
      - "Synthesize X faster, because ATP hydrolysis now proceeds unhindered"
      - "Fail to drive X-formation, because coupling requires a shared intermediate"
      - "Synthesize X at the same rate, since the summed ΔG°' is unchanged at −8.5 kJ/mol"
      - "Reverse the reaction and consume X to make ATP"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "Free-energy additivity only delivers a favorable net ΔG when the two reactions are mechanistically linked through a common (usually phosphorylated) intermediate. If ATP hydrolysis and X-formation happen at separate sites with no shared intermediate, the −30.5 kJ/mol of ATP hydrolysis is simply dissipated as heat and cannot pay for the +22 kJ/mol X-formation, which remains endergonic and will not proceed. The arithmetic sum (−8.5) is irrelevant without the coupling mechanism, so the 'same rate' choice is wrong. ATP hydrolysis being 'unhindered' does not help X-formation, and there is no basis for net reversal to make ATP."
  - stem: "A muscle biopsy from a patient with a creatine kinase deficiency is compared with normal muscle during the first 60 seconds of intense exercise. Relative to normal muscle, the patient's muscle would be expected to show:"
    choices:
      - "A faster fall in ATP during the first minute, because the creatine phosphate buffer cannot recharge ADP"
      - "A slower fall in ATP, because creatine phosphate is bypassed and glycolysis starts immediately"
      - "No change in ATP kinetics, since creatine phosphate is below ATP on the transfer ladder"
      - "Higher serum creatine kinase, reflecting compensatory enzyme release"
    answer: 0
    rationale: "Creatine phosphate sits above ATP on the phosphoryl-transfer ladder and, through creatine kinase, rapidly recharges ADP to ATP during the first 30–60 seconds of intense effort — bridging the gap between stored ATP (seconds) and glycolysis. Without functional creatine kinase, that buffer is unavailable, so ATP falls faster early in exercise. Creatine phosphate is above ATP on the ladder (not below), which is precisely why it can donate phosphate. A deficiency would not raise serum CK; elevated serum CK reflects leakage of the enzyme from damaged muscle, the opposite scenario."
  - stem: "A tumor biopsy is examined on a PET scan using an 18F-labeled glucose analog and shows intense uptake even though the tissue is well-oxygenated. This finding is best explained by which principle from metabolic design?"
    choices:
      - "Mitochondrial electron transport failure forcing anaerobic ATP production"
      - "The Warburg effect: cells run high glycolytic flux for biosynthetic precursors and redox supply, not for maximal ATP yield per glucose"
      - "Loss of all activated carriers, eliminating oxidative metabolism"
      - "An increase in energy charge above 0.95 that shuts down catabolism"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "The Warburg effect describes proliferating (often cancer) cells running aerobic glycolysis — high glucose throughput even with oxygen present — because the purpose of the flux is to supply biosynthetic intermediates and reducing power for rapid growth, not to maximize ATP per glucose. That high glucose appetite is what lights tumors up on a PET scan. It is not driven by ETC failure (that would be mitochondrial disease with lactic acidosis from a different cause), cells retain their activated carriers, and a high energy charge would suppress, not drive, glucose uptake."

    label: "Like I'm 5"
    text: "Your body is like a piggy bank for energy. When you eat food, you put energy coins into the bank. When you run, think, or grow, you take coins out and spend them. The special coin your body uses is called ATP. The clever trick is that hard jobs that need a lot of energy get done by tying them to easy jobs that give a lot of energy, like pulling a little wagon up a hill by tying it to a big wagon rolling down.   - level: "highschool"
    label: "High school"
    text: "Metabolism is how cells manage energy. Catabolism breaks fuel down and makes ATP (the cell's energy money); anabolism uses ATP to build things and move stuff around. A key idea is reaction coupling: a reaction that wouldn't happen on its own (it needs energy) gets paired with one that releases a lot of energy, like ATP breaking down, so together they go forward. ATP isn't special because of a 'high-energy bond' — it's because its breakdown products are more stable, so energy is released. Different foods all funnel into one molecule, acetyl-CoA, before being burned. The cell uses a few reusable helper molecules (NADH, FADH2, NADPH, coenzyme A), many made from vitamins, and it senses how much energy it has using something called energy charge, which decides whether to burn fuel or build."
  - level: "college"
    label: "College"
    text: "This is the framework chapter. Catabolism is convergent: macronutrients are digested, funneled into key intermediates (chiefly acetyl-CoA), and oxidized to CO2 with electrons banked on carriers. Endergonic reactions are driven by coupling to exergonic ones through a shared intermediate, exploiting the additivity of ΔG°' (e.g., a +12 kJ/mol step coupled to ATP hydrolysis at −30.5 kJ/mol nets −18.5). ATP's favorable hydrolysis (ΔG°' ≈ −30.5 kJ/mol) reflects product stabilization — charge separation relief, Pi resonance, entropy, hydration — not a literal high-energy bond, and ΔG°' = −RT ln K'eq means coupling shifts K'eq by ~10^5. ATP's intermediate phosphoryl-transfer potential (below PEP, 1,3-BPG, creatine phosphate; above glucose-6-P) is what makes it good currency. The activated carriers are vitamin-derived. ATP carries phosphoryl groups; NADH (~2.5 ATP) and FADH2 (~1.5 ATP, entering at Complex II) carry electrons; NADPH supplies reductive biosynthesis; and CoA-SH carries acyl groups through a thioester. Regulation operates via enzyme amount (transcription), activity (allostery + covalent modification), and accessibility (compartmentation), all integrated through energy charge, with adenylate kinase amplifying AMP as a sensitive alarm for AMPK."
  - level: "grad"
    label: "Graduate"
    text: "The chapter's value is its set of unifying thermodynamic and regulatory principles. Free-energy additivity under a common-intermediate mechanism is the formal basis of metabolic coupling; the cell's apparent ability to perform endergonic transformations reduces to enzyme-enforced sharing of a phosphorylated or acyl intermediate whose summed ΔG is negative. The 'high-energy bond' framing is replaced by product stabilization arguments (electrostatic relief, increased resonance and solvation of orthophosphate, entropic gain). The phosphoryl-transfer potential series is itself the design rationale for adenylate as the central currency: occupying an intermediate rank lets ATP be both regenerated by super-equilibrium donors (PEP, 1,3-BPG, phosphocreatine) and serve as a kinetically competent donor to sub-ATP acceptors, while kinetic stability (high activation barrier to spontaneous hydrolysis) makes covalent phosphorylation a clean, enzyme-gated switch. Carrier specialization — NADH/FADH2 partitioned to catabolic electron delivery at different ETC entry points and redox potentials, NADPH reserved for anabolic reductive demand despite near-identical chemistry — implements thermodynamic compartmentalization of redox pools. Regulation is hierarchical in timescale (transcriptional abundance, post-translational allostery/covalent modification, spatial compartmentation) and integrated by energy charge, whose sensitivity is engineered by the adenylate kinase equilibrium that converts modest ATP depletion into large fractional AMP increases, the signal AMPK reads. The Warburg phenotype is the clean counterexample to naive yield-maximization: flux purpose (biosynthetic precursor and redox supply for proliferation) rather than ATP-per-glucose can dictate pathway selection."

**You will not memorize a metabolic map in this chapter. You will learn the grammar that every later map obeys.** Glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, fatty-acid oxidation — each is a long sentence, and this chapter teaches the parts of speech. Get the grammar and the rest is reading. Skip it and you will be memorizing arrows for the rest of the semester.

Here is the organizing idea, and it is worth holding onto for the next ten chapters: **metabolism is an energy economy.** A cell earns energy by burning fuel and spends it to build molecules, move itself, and pump ions against gradients. Earning is catabolism. Spending is anabolism. Almost everything else in this chapter is a detail about how the books are kept.

## Why all roads lead to acetyl-CoA

Cells need energy for three jobs: mechanical work (a muscle contracting, a cell crawling), active transport (the Na⁺/K⁺ pump fighting a gradient), and biosynthesis (building proteins, lipids, nucleic acids). Plants and other phototrophs capture sunlight. We are chemotrophs: we oxidize carbon fuels. Energy is never created or destroyed here, only transformed — from the bonds of food into the currency the cell can spend.

Food becomes usable energy in three stages. First, digestion breaks large molecules into small ones: proteins to amino acids, starch to sugars, fats to fatty acids. Second, those small molecules funnel into a handful of key intermediates, above all **acetyl-CoA**. Third, acetyl-CoA is oxidized completely, and that oxidation drives ATP synthesis.

The striking thing is the convergence. Whether you eat a steak, a potato, or a spoonful of olive oil, your mitochondria see the same molecule: acetyl-CoA. Different highways, all leading to the same city center. That single fact is why you can learn one central pathway (the citric acid cycle, Chapter 17) and have it apply to every fuel you will ever eat.

## Coupling: how the cell pays for unfavorable reactions

Building a molecule is usually uphill — endergonic, with a positive ΔG. So how does it happen? Not by magic and not by reversing some enzyme. The cell pays for it.

A thermodynamically unfavorable reaction proceeds when it is coupled to a favorable one through a **shared intermediate**. The trick is that ΔG values are additive. If A → B has ΔG°' = +12 kJ/mol and ATP hydrolysis has ΔG°' = −30.5 kJ/mol, then running them together through a shared intermediate gives a net −18.5 kJ/mol, and the reaction goes. Picture pulling a light wagon uphill by hitching it to a heavier wagon rolling down. The two are not happening at separate times; they are linked, and the combined system rolls in the favorable direction.

This is why catabolism and anabolism are coupled rather than independent. Catabolism is your paycheck; anabolism is your spending. Some pathways, like the citric acid cycle, are amphibolic — they run in both directions depending on need. But direction is never set by reversing a single enzyme. It is set by using distinct, separately regulated, irreversible enzymes for each direction. Income and spending use the same bank account, but different passwords.

## ATP, and the myth of the high-energy bond

ATP is the universal energy currency. Its structure is adenine plus ribose plus three phosphates, named α, β, and γ from the ribose outward; the γ-phosphate is the one transferred. Hydrolysis of that terminal phosphate is exergonic, with ΔG°' ≈ −30.5 kJ/mol.

Now correct a common misconception. There is no "high-energy bond" storing the energy. Energy is not in a bond waiting to spring out. Hydrolysis is favorable because the **products are more stable than the reactants**: ADP and Pi enjoy more charge separation relief, better resonance stabilization of the released phosphate, more entropy, and better hydration than ATP did. The free energy comes from the difference in stability, not from a special bond.

That ΔG°' is what lets ATP drive other reactions. Recall ΔG°' = −RT ln K'eq: a ΔG°' near −30.5 kJ/mol corresponds to coupling that shifts an unfavorable reaction's effective K'eq by roughly a factor of 10⁵. Couple to ATP and a reaction that essentially would not happen now runs to near completion.

The deeper point is where ATP sits on the **phosphoryl-transfer ladder** — a ranking of how eagerly each phosphorylated compound gives up its phosphate. Phosphoenolpyruvate, 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate, and creatine phosphate all rank above ATP; glucose-6-phosphate ranks below. ATP sits in the middle, and that is exactly what makes it good currency. A compound at the top can recharge ADP into ATP; ATP in turn can phosphorylate things lower down, like glucose. A bill of intermediate denomination is the one you can both receive and spend. Drag the phosphoryl token in the ladder module and you will discover the rule yourself: transfers only run downhill.

One more property earns ATP its job. Phosphate esters are thermodynamically unstable but **kinetically stable** — they want to hydrolyze, but they will not do it on their own at any useful rate without an enzyme. That mismatch makes them ideal on/off switches: a kinase adds a phosphate to flip a protein on, a phosphatase removes it to flip it off, and nothing happens in between.

You can feel ATP's timescale in your own muscles. Stored ATP lasts seconds. Then creatine phosphate, sitting above ATP on the ladder, recharges ADP for another 30 to 60 seconds via creatine kinase. (When muscle is injured, creatine kinase leaks into the blood, which is why elevated serum CK is a clinical marker of muscle damage.) After that, glycolysis takes over, and finally aerobic respiration for the long haul.

## Activated carriers and recurring motifs

Beneath the thousands of named reactions, metabolism uses only about six reaction types — redox, ligation (ATP-driven joining), isomerization, group transfer, hydrolysis, and lyase reactions. Learn the verbs and the sentences get short.

It also runs on a small set of **activated carriers** — think delivery trucks. They are kinetically stable, few in number, and most are built from vitamins, which is why a vitamin deficiency hits metabolism hard. ATP carries phosphoryl groups. **NADH** carries electrons from carbon oxidations (from niacin, vitamin B3) and feeds them to the electron transport chain, worth roughly 2.5 ATP each. **FADH₂** also carries electrons (from riboflavin, B2; the working part is the isoalloxazine ring) but enters lower in the chain at Complex II, worth about 1.5 ATP. **NADPH** is the carrier for reductive biosynthesis — same electron-carrying chemistry, dedicated to building rather than burning. And **coenzyme A** carries acyl groups through a thioester linkage; that bond has high transfer potential because sulfur stabilizes the product poorly by resonance, leaving the energy available. CoA's core is pantothenate, vitamin B5.

The clinical tie-ins write themselves. Pantothenate kinase deficiency causes PKAN, with brain iron accumulation and neurodegeneration. Niacin deficiency is pellagra; thiamine deficiency (the carrier TPP) is beriberi; riboflavin deficiency is ariboflavinosis. Each disease is a missing truck.

## Regulation: amount, activity, access

A cell controls flux three ways, on three timescales. It changes the **amount** of an enzyme by transcription (hours to days). It changes the **activity** of existing enzymes by allostery and reversible covalent modification (seconds to minutes), which is how hormones like insulin coordinate the whole body fast. And it changes **substrate accessibility** by compartmentation — keeping fatty-acid synthesis in the cytosol and fatty-acid oxidation in the mitochondrion so the two never run at once in a futile cycle.

The master gauge is **energy charge**: ([ATP] + 0.5[ADP]) / ([ATP] + [ADP] + [AMP]), normally held near 0.85–0.90. It is the cell's battery meter. Adenylate kinase (2 ADP ⇌ ATP + AMP) makes AMP a hypersensitive alarm: a small dip in ATP produces a large spike in AMP, which activates AMPK to switch on catabolism and shut down anabolism. Read the battery in the third module and watch the needle redline as ATP falls.

When this regulation fails, you get disease — and this is the frame to carry through the whole course: pathophysiology is biochemistry in a clinical context. Diabetes is regulation failure at the level of insulin signaling. Cancer cells run aerobic glycolysis (the Warburg effect), burning glucose for biosynthetic building blocks rather than maximal ATP, which is why tumors light up on a PET scan. Mitochondrial disease is electron-transport failure, often presenting as lactic acidosis.
