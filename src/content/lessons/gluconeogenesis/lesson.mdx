summary: >-
  Gluconeogenesis is how the body builds glucose from scratch when food runs out, and
  its single organizing idea is that it is not glycolysis run backward. It shares seven
  near-equilibrium enzymes with glycolysis but routes around the three irreversible
  glycolytic steps with four different enzymes, working across three compartments and
  paying real ATP to climb a thermodynamic hill. The same fructose-2,6-bisphosphate
  switch that turns glycolysis on turns gluconeogenesis off, so a healthy cell almost
  never runs both at once.

techniques:
  - name: "Isotopic carbon tracing of precursors"
    blurb: >-
      Feed a precursor labeled at a known carbon — ¹⁴C-lactate, ¹³C-alanine, or
      ¹³C-glycerol — and track where the atoms land in newly made glucose. The carbon
      skeleton is what becomes glucose (alanine's nitrogen is stripped by transamination
      first), so the label has to sit on carbon. This is how the field proved the fact at
      the heart of the chapter: carbons from even-chain fatty acids never reach glucose,
      because the two that enter the cycle as acetyl-CoA leave again as CO₂.
  - name: "Stable-isotope tracing of glucose production"
    blurb: >-
      To measure how much glucose the liver is making, infuse a labeled glucose tracer in
      an overnight-fasted (basal) subject and read the dilution: total glucose appearance
      minus any infused glucose is endogenous glucose production, mostly gluconeogenesis.
      A hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp answers a different question — it raises insulin
      to see how well it SUPPRESSES that output, so failure to suppress is the read-out of
      hepatic insulin resistance, not a measure of fasting gluconeogenesis itself.
  - name: "Allosteric enzyme assay with single effectors"
    blurb: >-
      To map a control point you purify the enzyme and change one effector at a time while
      watching velocity. Adding acetyl-CoA to pyruvate carboxylase, AMP or
      fructose-2,6-bisphosphate to FBPase-1, or running the bifunctional PFK-2/FBPase-2
      enzyme with and without PKA phosphorylation is how the reciprocal regulators of this
      pathway were assigned to their enzymes, one molecule at a time.

practiceQuestions:
  - stem: "During a prolonged fast, which tissue is the principal site of gluconeogenesis, and why can skeletal muscle not contribute free glucose to the blood?"
    choices:
      - "The pancreas, because it secretes insulin that drives glucose synthesis"
      - "The liver (and kidney cortex in long fasts); muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase, so it cannot release free glucose"
      - "Adipose tissue, because it stores the glycerol used to build glucose"
      - "The brain, because it consumes the most glucose and therefore makes its own"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      The liver is the primary gluconeogenic organ, with the kidney cortex contributing in
      prolonged fasting. Muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase, the final bypass enzyme that
      strips the phosphate off glucose-6-phosphate, so it cannot release free glucose and
      instead exports lactate. The pancreas secretes hormones but does not synthesize
      glucose; adipose supplies glycerol but is not a gluconeogenic site; and the brain is a
      glucose consumer, not a producer.
  - stem: "A student claims that gluconeogenesis is simply glycolysis run in reverse. What is the strongest reason this is incorrect?"
    choices:
      - "Gluconeogenesis uses four different enzymes to bypass the three irreversible glycolytic steps, in different compartments and at net energetic cost"
      - "Gluconeogenesis and glycolysis use entirely different sets of enzymes for all of their steps"
      - "Glycolysis occurs in mitochondria while gluconeogenesis occurs only in the cytosol"
      - "Gluconeogenesis produces ATP, whereas glycolysis consumes it"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Gluconeogenesis shares its seven near-equilibrium enzymes with glycolysis but cannot
      reverse the three irreversible steps; it routes around them with pyruvate carboxylase
      plus PEPCK, FBPase-1, and glucose-6-phosphatase, spanning mitochondrion, cytosol, and
      ER and spending energy. It does not use entirely different enzymes for all steps,
      glycolysis is cytosolic rather than mitochondrial, and it is gluconeogenesis that
      consumes energy while glycolysis nets ATP.
  - stem: "Why can even-chain fatty acids not serve as a source of net glucose synthesis?"
    choices:
      - "Fatty acids cannot cross the mitochondrial membrane to reach gluconeogenic enzymes"
      - "Acetyl-CoA from β-oxidation cannot enter the citric acid cycle at all"
      - "Acetyl-CoA enters the TCA cycle but two carbons are lost as CO₂ per turn, yielding no net oxaloacetate gain"
      - "Fatty acid carbons are converted directly to lactate, which cannot make glucose"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      Each acetyl-CoA condenses with one oxaloacetate to enter the cycle, and over one turn
      two carbons leave as CO₂, so the OAA consumed is exactly regenerated with no net gain.
      Without net OAA there is no net glucose. Fatty acids do reach the matrix and acetyl-CoA
      does enter the cycle, so those options are wrong, and fatty acid carbons are not routed
      to lactate.
  - stem: "In the pyruvate-to-PEP bypass, what is the purpose of converting oxaloacetate to malate before exporting it from the mitochondrion?"
    choices:
      - "To generate ATP needed for the cytosolic steps"
      - "To activate pyruvate carboxylase allosterically"
      - "To detoxify oxaloacetate, which is toxic to the cytosol"
      - "Oxaloacetate lacks an inner-membrane transporter, so it is shuttled as malate, which also delivers cytosolic NADH"
    answer: 3
    rationale: >-
      Oxaloacetate cannot cross the inner mitochondrial membrane, so it is reduced to
      malate, exported on the malate–α-ketoglutarate carrier, and re-oxidized to OAA in the
      cytosol — a step that, with pyruvate or alanine precursors, also supplies the NADH
      that GAPDH later needs. The shuttle does not generate ATP, acetyl-CoA (not malate
      conversion) activates pyruvate carboxylase, and OAA is not a cytosolic toxin.
  - stem: "Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate is best described as which of the following?"
    choices:
      - "A glycolytic pathway intermediate converted to glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate"
      - "The high-energy phosphate donor used by PEPCK"
      - "A purely regulatory molecule that activates PFK-1 and inhibits FBPase-1"
      - "A precursor that enters gluconeogenesis at the level of DHAP"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate is a regulator only: it activates PFK-1 (favoring glycolysis)
      and inhibits FBPase-1 (suppressing gluconeogenesis). It is not a pathway intermediate —
      that is fructose-1,6-bisphosphate. PEPCK uses GTP as its phosphate donor, and the
      gluconeogenic precursor entering at DHAP is glycerol.

explainLevels:
  - level: "eli5"
    label: "Like I'm 5"
    text: |
      When you don't eat for a long time, your body runs low on sugar, and your brain
      really, really needs sugar to work. So your liver becomes a little sugar factory. It
      takes leftover bits — like the stuff your muscles make when you run hard — and glues
      them back together into brand-new sugar. Building sugar costs energy, the way sliding
      down a hill is easy but climbing back up is hard work. That is why your body only
      builds sugar when it has to, and never tries to build it and tear it down at the same
      time.
  - level: "highschool"
    label: "High school"
    text: |
      Gluconeogenesis is making new glucose from things that aren't carbohydrates: lactate,
      certain amino acids like alanine, and glycerol from fat. It happens mostly in the liver
      during fasting, because the brain and red blood cells depend on glucose. It looks like
      glycolysis running backward, but it isn't. Glycolysis has three one-way steps that
      can't be reversed, so gluconeogenesis goes around them with four different enzymes, and
      it has to spend energy to do it: about 6 high-energy phosphates and 2 NADH per glucose,
      versus the 2 ATP glycolysis makes. One key fact: you can't turn ordinary fat into
      sugar, because the carbons leave as CO₂ before they can become glucose. A master switch
      molecule called fructose-2,6-bisphosphate makes sure the cell runs either glycolysis or
      gluconeogenesis, never both at once.
  - level: "college"
    label: "College"
    text: |
      Gluconeogenesis synthesizes glucose from pyruvate-level precursors (lactate via LDH,
      glucogenic amino acids, glycerol entering as DHAP). It shares the seven near-equilibrium
      enzymes of glycolysis and bypasses the three irreversible steps with four enzymes.
      Bypass 1: mitochondrial pyruvate carboxylase (biotin-dependent, acetyl-CoA-activated)
      makes oxaloacetate, which exits as malate via the malate shuttle (also delivering
      cytosolic NADH for GAPDH from pyruvate/alanine precursors); cytosolic PEPCK then
      converts OAA to PEP using GTP, with the PC-added CO₂ removed as a thermodynamic lever.
      Bypass 2: FBPase-1 hydrolyzes fructose-1,6-bisphosphate. Bypass 3: glucose-6-phosphatase,
      in the ER lumen, releases free glucose (liver and kidney cortex only; muscle lacks it).
      Cost is 4 ATP + 2 GTP + 2 NADH per glucose. Reciprocal regulation at the three pairs is
      governed by fructose-2,6-bisphosphate, set by the bifunctional PFK-2/FBPase-2 enzyme
      under insulin/glucagon (PKA) control, which prevents futile cycling.
  - level: "grad"
    label: "Graduate"
    text: |
      The architecture of gluconeogenesis is a study in how irreversibility is bypassed
      without sacrificing thermodynamic control. The PK bypass is split spatially: pyruvate
      carboxylase performs an ATP- and biotin-dependent carboxylation (an anaplerotic
      reaction allosterically gated by acetyl-CoA, signaling adequate acetyl-CoA flux and a
      need to replenish OAA rather than oxidize it), and PEPCK performs a GTP-dependent
      β-decarboxylation. The carboxylation/decarboxylation cycle is not wasteful: the
      transient CO₂ fixation raises the group-transfer potential needed to generate PEP, and
      the obligatory malate shuttle co-transports reducing equivalents matched to cytosolic
      GAPDH demand, so redox and carbon are balanced by design. Compartmentalizing G6Pase to
      the ER lumen physically separates antagonistic activities (G6Pase vs hexokinase), the
      same substrate-cycle-avoidance logic that fructose-2,6-bisphosphate enforces
      allosterically at the F6P/FBP node. F-2,6-BP is the integrator: made and degraded by a
      single bifunctional polypeptide whose kinase/phosphatase balance is set by PKA
      phosphorylation, coupling hormonal state to the simultaneous, opposite control of PFK-1
      and FBPase-1. The systems point is that flux direction at a substrate cycle is governed
      not by enzyme presence but by the ratio of opposing activities — which is why
      unrestrained PEPCK/G6Pase expression in insulin resistance presents as a regulatory
      failure rather than an enzymatic absence.

  - title: "It's free glucose!"
    source: "CH17 lecture notes — the Cori cycle and the debranching-enzyme discovery."
    text: >-
      Gerty Cori spent years on a fraction other chemists threw away — the supernatant left
      after recrystallizing phosphorylase. In it she found the glycogen debranching enzyme,
      and when its product ran on chromatography she read the spot at a glance and bolted down
      the hall shouting, "It's free glucose, it's free glucose!" That is the exact molecule
      this chapter ends on: free glucose, the thing only liver and kidney can release because
      only they carry glucose-6-phosphatase. The Cori cycle she and Carl mapped — muscle
      lactate to liver glucose and back — is the interorgan loop that makes the whole pathway
      matter beyond a single cell.
  - title: "Why you really can't turn fat into sugar"
    source: "CH17 lecture notes — \"you consume one OAA to bring acetyl-CoA in, get one back; net glucose = zero\""
    text: >-
      It feels like fat should be convertible to glucose; it's an energy store, after all.
      But even-chain fatty acids cannot make net glucose, and the reason is an accounting
      trick worth memorizing. Acetyl-CoA enters the citric acid cycle by combining with one
      oxaloacetate, and over a turn of the cycle two carbons leave as CO₂. You spend one OAA
      to bring acetyl-CoA in and you get exactly one OAA back, so net OAA gain is zero and net
      glucose is zero. Only odd-chain fatty acids escape, by sending their final propionyl-CoA
      to succinyl-CoA and on to OAA.
  - title: "CO₂ as a thermodynamic lever"
    source: "CH17 lecture notes — \"the CO₂ added by PC is removed by PEPCK; CO₂ is a thermodynamic lever\""
    text: >-
      It looks pointless: pyruvate carboxylase bolts a CO₂ onto pyruvate, and a few steps
      later PEPCK pries the same CO₂ right back off. Why add a carbon just to remove it?
      Because the round trip is a lever. Putting the carboxyl on and then taking it off is
      what makes forming high-energy phosphoenolpyruvate thermodynamically favorable. CO₂ here
      is a lever, not cargo — the carboxylation and decarboxylation are one coordinated
      maneuver, not two unrelated steps.
  - title: "One missing enzyme, a glycogen-stuffed liver"
    source: "CH17 lecture notes — Von Gierke disease (glucose-6-phosphatase deficiency)."
    text: >-
      Von Gierke disease is the clinic's proof that the last bypass enzyme is non-negotiable.
      Lose glucose-6-phosphatase (or the transporter that feeds it) and the liver can build
      glucose-6-phosphate but can never release free glucose, so an infant crashes into severe
      fasting hypoglycemia and lactic acidosis, with a liver swollen by the glycogen it cannot
      cash out. It is the same enzyme that lets muscle keep its glucose to itself, seen from
      the other side: when even the liver loses it, the whole body loses access to stored fuel.

**Skip breakfast and lunch, and somewhere in the early afternoon your liver starts building glucose out of spare parts.** It has to. Your brain runs almost entirely on glucose, and your red blood cells run on nothing else, because they have no mitochondria. Stored glycogen covers maybe twelve hours. After that, blood sugar stays in its narrow lane (about 70–100 mg/dL fasting) only because the liver, and in a long fast the kidney cortex, is manufacturing fresh glucose. That manufacturing pathway is gluconeogenesis.

Fasting itself is old and human. The Teton Sioux fasted to invite a spiritual awakening, and cultures across the world still fast for clarity or devotion. Your brain does not care about enlightenment; it cares about glucose, and it will get it. Fasting is not the same as running out of energy. Your body is switching fuel sources, and gluconeogenesis is the switch that keeps the most glucose-dependent tissues supplied while everything else learns to burn fat.

## Three ways to build a sugar

Gluconeogenesis assembles glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors, and there are three to know. **Lactate** comes back from hard-working muscle and red cells; lactate dehydrogenase converts it to pyruvate. **Glucogenic amino acids**, alanine chief among them, enter at pyruvate or at oxaloacetate after losing their nitrogen. **Glycerol**, released when fat is mobilized, enters as DHAP by way of glycerol kinase and glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase.

Notice what is missing from that list: fat itself. This trips up almost everyone, so hold onto it. Even-chain fatty acids cannot become glucose. Their carbons enter the citric acid cycle as acetyl-CoA, but for every acetyl-CoA that comes in, two carbons leave as CO₂. You spend one oxaloacetate to bring acetyl-CoA into the cycle and you get exactly one back. Net oxaloacetate gain is zero, so net glucose from fat is zero. The one exception is odd-chain fatty acids, whose final three-carbon propionyl-CoA can be routed through succinyl-CoA to oxaloacetate. That is the whole reason "you can't turn fat into sugar" is true for the fat you actually carry.

## The trap: is this just glycolysis in reverse?

No. This is the single most important idea in the chapter. Gluconeogenesis shares seven of its enzymes with glycolysis, the near-equilibrium ones that run happily in either direction. But glycolysis has three irreversible, energy-dumping steps, and you cannot simply push those backward. So gluconeogenesis routes around all three with four different enzymes, in different compartments, at real energetic cost.

The three walls and their bypasses: pyruvate kinase is bypassed by **pyruvate carboxylase plus PEPCK**; phosphofructokinase-1 is bypassed by **fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase**; and hexokinase is bypassed by **glucose-6-phosphatase**. Different enzymes, different doors, same map run as opposite traffic.

## Bypass 1: the shipping problem

The first wall is the most elaborate, and it spans two compartments. Inside the mitochondrion, **pyruvate carboxylase** adds CO₂ to pyruvate to make oxaloacetate, spending one ATP. It uses a biotin cofactor mounted on a roughly 14-Å lysine arm that swings the carboxyl group into place, and it is allosterically switched on by acetyl-CoA, the cell's way of saying *plenty of fuel here, make glucose rather than burn more pyruvate.*

Then comes the shipping problem. PEPCK, the enzyme that takes the next step, lives in the cytosol, and oxaloacetate has no transporter across the inner mitochondrial membrane. The fix is the **malate shuttle**: oxaloacetate is reduced to malate, malate rides out on the malate–α-ketoglutarate carrier, and in the cytosol it is oxidized back to oxaloacetate. The trip does double duty, exporting the carbon skeleton and (for pyruvate or alanine precursors) carrying the NADH that GAPDH will need later. Then **PEPCK** spends a GTP and removes the very CO₂ that pyruvate carboxylase added, producing phosphoenolpyruvate. That carboxylate-then-decarboxylate move is a thermodynamic lever: adding CO₂ and pulling it back off is what makes forming high-energy PEP favorable.

## Bypasses 2 and 3, and why muscle can't share

From PEP, the shared enzymes run in reverse up to fructose-1,6-bisphosphate. There **FBPase-1** simply hydrolyzes off a phosphate, with no ATP made or spent, a clean snip past the PFK-1 wall. It is the main regulated step of the pathway, inhibited by AMP and by fructose-2,6-bisphosphate.

The last wall, **glucose-6-phosphatase**, hides in a revealing place: the lumen of the endoplasmic reticulum. Glucose-6-phosphate has to be carried in by the T1 transporter to be dephosphorylated. Why tuck it away there? Because hexokinase sits in the cytosol running the opposite reaction, and keeping the two enzymes in separate rooms prevents a futile cycle. Only liver and kidney cortex express G6Pase. Muscle does not, which means muscle can never release free glucose into the blood. It hoards what it makes and ships out lactate instead, and that single missing enzyme is the reason the Cori cycle has to exist.

## The bill, and the switch that prevents waste

Walk the cost meter up the island and it lands on **4 ATP + 2 GTP + 2 NADH per glucose**. Glycolysis nets only 2 ATP. Building is far more expensive than breaking, exactly as you would expect. Glycolysis runs downhill the way gravity pulls water; gluconeogenesis is the pump that pushes it back up, and pumps cost energy because they fight thermodynamics.

That expense is why the cell must never run both pathways at once, because doing so would just spin ATP into heat. The guard against this is **reciprocal regulation** at the three enzyme pairs, and its master switch is **fructose-2,6-bisphosphate**. Lock in the distinction: fructose-**1,6**-bisphosphate is a pathway intermediate, while fructose-**2,6**-bisphosphate is a regulator only. *1,6 is the pathway; 2,6 is the regulator.* Both are made by the **bifunctional PFK-2/FBPase-2 enzyme**, a single protein carrying a kinase domain and a phosphatase domain. When you are fed, insulin keeps it dephosphorylated, the kinase wins, F-2,6-BP rises, and glycolysis runs. When you fast, glucagon raises cAMP, PKA phosphorylates the enzyme, the phosphatase wins, F-2,6-BP falls, and gluconeogenesis takes over. PKA also shuts off liver pyruvate kinase, closing the door behind you.

The clinic makes all of this concrete. Pyruvate carboxylase deficiency produces hypoglycemia and lactic acidosis together, one missing enzyme and two findings, because pyruvate can neither start gluconeogenesis nor stop piling up as lactate. In type 2 diabetes, insulin resistance lifts the brake on PEPCK, hepatic gluconeogenesis runs unchecked, and fasting blood sugar climbs; metformin works largely by activating AMPK to rein it back in. And the interorgan loops tie the body together. The **Cori cycle** sends muscle lactate to the liver to be rebuilt into glucose, with the liver paying the ATP bill, while the **alanine cycle** carries muscle nitrogen to the liver safely packaged on alanine, because muscle lacks the urea-cycle machinery to dispose of ammonia on its own. A rising serum ALT, the very enzyme that runs the alanine cycle, is a clinic's flag for liver damage.
