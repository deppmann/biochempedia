summary: >-
  Why does a cell spend ATP to make ATP? Because the first three steps of
  glycolysis are a deliberate investment. This lesson tracks one glucose through
  all ten steps — two ATP spent up front, four earned at the end, two NADH banked —
  and shows why the three irreversible steps are exactly where the cell installs its
  throttle.

estMinutes: 22
objectives:
  - "Trace one glucose through all ten steps and explain the two-phase logic: why the cell spends 2 ATP (steps 1, 3) before it earns 4 ATP (steps 7, 10) and 2 NADH (step 6)."
  - "Account for the net yield — +2 ATP, +2 NADH per glucose — and explain why every product from step 6 onward is doubled, beginning at the aldolase cleavage."
  - "Identify the three irreversible, regulated steps (hexokinase, PFK-1, pyruvate kinase) by their large negative ΔG, and explain why irreversibility is what makes a step controllable."
  - "Explain why pyruvate's fate, not glucose, is the real bottleneck — how fermentation regenerates NAD+ so the pathway can keep running, and why that matters for a red blood cell or a sprinting muscle."

      Büchner was not trying to overturn anything. He was grinding yeast to make a
      medical extract for his brother Hans and added sugar as a preservative — and
      the dead juice began to bubble. Fermentation, everyone knew, required living
      cells and some vital force inside them. Here was sugar breaking down with no
      cell left alive. The vitalists fought back, arguing fragments of protoplasm had
      survived the grinding. Büchner answered with controls: boiled extract did
      nothing, so the agent was a destructible substance, not a mystical property;
      filtered extract still fermented, so no cells were needed. He named the agent
      zymase, from the Greek for leaven, and published on January 9, 1897. He had
      moved fermentation out of the realm of life force and into chemistry — the move
      that lets you ask, of every step in glycolysis, which enzyme does it. He died
      at fifty-seven from shrapnel on the Romanian front in 1917, never seeing how
      far the question would run.

      oxygen. His heart failed in 1933 as the Nazis dismantled his institute and

    story: >-
      Meyerhof reached biochemistry by an unlikely road. Bedridden for months by a
      kidney ailment in his twenties, he read poetry and wrote a doctoral thesis on
      the psychology of mental illness before deciding that life itself could be
      written in the language of physical law. His 1913 habilitation, "The Energetics
      of Living Cells," argued that a body is a thermodynamic system holding itself
      against entropy — and he proved it with frog legs. Stimulating detached muscle
      to exhaustion without oxygen, he watched lactic acid pile up; when he readmitted
      air, only about a quarter of it burned for energy and the rest was rebuilt into
      glycogen. That recovery loop, the muscle repaying its "oxygen debt," won him a
      share of the 1922 Nobel Prize. The theory was elegant, and he did not cling to
      it: when his assistant Karl Lohmann isolated ATP in 1929 and muscle was soon
      shown to contract with no lactic acid at all, Meyerhof retooled his whole
      laboratory around the new molecule. Fleeing France in 1940, found in a Marseille
      hotel lobby and asked what he needed, he asked not for food but for his notes
      and his library — and carried both to Pennsylvania.

techniques:
  - name: "ATP and NADH accounting (the ledger)"
    blurb: >-
      The net yield isn't asserted — it's counted. Tally ATP spent (steps 1, 3)
      against ATP made (steps 7, 10) and NADH made (step 6), doubling everything after
      the aldolase cleavage. The books balance at +2 ATP and +2 NADH per glucose, and
      the running ledger is the single most exam-tested skill in the chapter.
  - name: "Isotopic carbon tracing"
    blurb: >-
      Feed cells glucose labeled at a specific carbon — say ¹³C or ¹⁴C on C1 — and
      follow where that atom lands. Tracing is how the field proved the symmetric
      six-carbon sugar splits into two three-carbon halves, and why both halves run
      the payoff phase. The same trick, scaled to a PET scanner, reads a tumor's
      glucose appetite off an ¹⁸F-labeled analog.
  - name: "Enzyme assays for the regulated steps"
    blurb: >-
      To find a throttle you measure the enzyme's velocity while changing one effector
      at a time. Adding ATP, citrate, AMP, or fructose-2,6-bisphosphate to purified
      PFK-1 and watching the rate rise or fall is how the regulators of the committed
      step were mapped — the same initial-velocity logic from enzyme kinetics, applied
      one molecule at a time.

practiceQuestions:
  - stem: "Per molecule of glucose completely converted to two pyruvate by glycolysis, what is the net yield of ATP and NADH?"
    choices:
      - "Net +2 ATP and +2 NADH"
      - "Net +4 ATP and +2 NADH"
      - "Net +2 ATP and +4 NADH"
      - "Net +6 ATP and +2 NADH"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Glycolysis spends 2 ATP in the investment phase (hexokinase, step 1; PFK-1, step
      3) and produces 4 ATP gross in the payoff phase (phosphoglycerate kinase, step 7,
      and pyruvate kinase, step 10, each occurring twice per glucose). Net ATP = 4 − 2 =
      +2. NADH is produced only at GAPDH (step 6), once per triose, so 2 per glucose.
      The +4 gross ATP and +2 NADH figures are each correct in isolation — which is why
      they appear as distractors — but the question asks for the NET ATP, which is +2.
  - stem: "Which set correctly lists the three irreversible, regulated steps of glycolysis, and which is the committed step?"
    choices:
      - "Hexokinase, phosphofructokinase-1, and pyruvate kinase; the committed step is phosphofructokinase-1"
      - "Hexokinase, aldolase, and pyruvate kinase; the committed step is hexokinase"
      - "Phosphoglucose isomerase, PFK-1, and enolase; the committed step is enolase"
      - "Hexokinase, PFK-1, and GAPDH; the committed step is GAPDH"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      The three steps with large negative ΔG that are effectively irreversible and serve
      as control points are hexokinase (step 1), PFK-1 (step 3), and pyruvate kinase
      (step 10). PFK-1 is the committed step because its product, fructose-1,6-bisphosphate,
      is dedicated to glycolysis, and PFK-1 is the primary regulatory enzyme (inhibited by
      ATP and citrate, activated by AMP and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate). Aldolase, isomerase,
      enolase, and GAPDH are not among the irreversible regulatory steps.
  - stem: "At what point does each downstream reaction begin to occur twice per molecule of glucose, requiring the '×2' counting of products?"
    choices:
      - "After aldolase (step 4) cleaves fructose-1,6-bisphosphate into two three-carbon molecules"
      - "After hexokinase (step 1) phosphorylates glucose"
      - "After GAPDH (step 6) produces NADH"
      - "After pyruvate kinase (step 10) produces pyruvate"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Aldolase (step 4) splits one six-carbon fructose-1,6-bisphosphate into two
      three-carbon molecules (DHAP and G3P), and triose phosphate isomerase converts DHAP
      to a second G3P. From step 6 onward both three-carbon units traverse the payoff
      phase, so every product (2 NADH, 4 ATP) is doubled per glucose. The ×2 counting
      begins after the aldolase cleavage, not at the first phosphorylation or at a later
      payoff step.
  - stem: "GAPDH catalyzes the only oxidation step of glycolysis (step 6), converting glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate to 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate. What is the source of the phosphate group added to the substrate?"
    choices:
      - "Inorganic phosphate (Pi), not ATP"
      - "A phosphate transferred from ATP"
      - "A phosphate transferred from GTP"
      - "A phosphate released from NADH"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      In the GAPDH reaction, oxidation of the aldehyde to a carboxylic acid is coupled to
      the attachment of inorganic phosphate (Pi), creating the high-energy acyl-phosphate
      1,3-bisphosphoglycerate via a thioester intermediate on an active-site cysteine; NAD+
      is reduced to NADH in the same step. No ATP or GTP is consumed here — the energy for
      the high phosphoryl-transfer potential comes from the substrate's oxidation, which is
      why the next step (PGK) can make ATP.
  - stem: "Hexokinase undergoes induced fit, closing its two lobes around glucose once substrate binds. What is the primary functional reason for this conformational change?"
    choices:
      - "It excludes water from the active site, preventing wasteful ATP hydrolysis during phosphoryl transfer"
      - "It allosterically activates the downstream enzyme PFK-1"
      - "It lets the enzyme bind two glucose molecules at once to double output"
      - "It converts the aldose glucose into the ketose fructose"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Induced fit closes the active site around glucose so that water is excluded. If water
      remained, ATP's γ-phosphate could be transferred to water (futile hydrolysis) instead
      of to glucose's C6 hydroxyl. By closing only when the correct substrate is bound, the
      enzyme ensures the phosphoryl group goes to glucose, not water. The aldose-to-ketose
      conversion is done later by phosphoglucose isomerase (step 2), not by hexokinase.

mcatQuestions:
  - stem: "A muscle cell metabolizes a glucose unit released from glycogen. Glycogen breakdown yields glucose-1-phosphate, converted to glucose-6-phosphate, which enters glycolysis at step 2 — bypassing the hexokinase reaction (step 1). Assuming complete conversion to two pyruvate, what is the net ATP yield per glucose unit entering this way?"
    choices:
      - "+3 ATP"
      - "+2 ATP"
      - "+4 ATP"
      - "+1 ATP"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Normally the investment phase spends 2 ATP (hexokinase + PFK-1). Entering as
      glucose-6-phosphate bypasses hexokinase, so only PFK-1 spends 1 ATP. The payoff phase
      is unchanged at +4 ATP gross (PGK and PK, each ×2). Net = 4 − 1 = +3 ATP. The +2
      distractor is the standard yield from free glucose; +4 would require bypassing both
      investment steps.
  - stem: "A cell is supplied with fructose-1,6-bisphosphate as its starting substrate, entering glycolysis directly at the aldolase step (step 4). Because this molecule already carries both phosphates, the entire investment phase is bypassed. What are the net ATP and NADH yields per fructose-1,6-bisphosphate fully converted to two pyruvate?"
    choices:
      - "+4 ATP and +2 NADH"
      - "+2 ATP and +2 NADH"
      - "+6 ATP and +2 NADH"
      - "+4 ATP and +4 NADH"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate enters at aldolase (step 4), skipping the investment phase
      (steps 1–3), so 0 ATP is invested. The payoff phase is unchanged: PGK (step 7) and PK
      (step 10) each make 2 ATP per glucose-equivalent, giving +4 ATP gross with nothing
      subtracted; GAPDH (step 6) makes 2 NADH. Net = +4 ATP and +2 NADH. The +2 ATP
      distractor reflects starting from glucose; the NADH count is unaffected by where you
      enter the investment phase.
  - stem: "A red blood cell, which lacks mitochondria, relies exclusively on glycolysis for ATP, and its supply of NAD+ is a small, fixed pool. If lactate dehydrogenase were pharmacologically inhibited (with no functional electron transport chain available), why would glycolytic ATP production rapidly halt?"
    choices:
      - "NAD+ would not be regenerated from NADH, and without NAD+ the GAPDH step (step 6) cannot proceed"
      - "ATP would accumulate and allosterically activate PFK-1, accelerating consumption"
      - "Pyruvate would be unable to enter the citric acid cycle, stopping substrate-level phosphorylation"
      - "Lactate buildup would directly phosphorylate ADP, depleting the inorganic phosphate pool"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      In a cell with no working electron transport chain, lactate dehydrogenase is the route
      that reoxidizes NADH back to NAD+ (pyruvate + NADH → lactate + NAD+). NAD+ is a
      catalytic cofactor present in limited amount and required by GAPDH (step 6). Block LDH
      and NADH accumulates, NAD+ is depleted, GAPDH stalls, the payoff phase cannot run, and
      ATP production stops. The red blood cell cannot use the mitochondrial route (it has
      none); choices B and D are biochemically incorrect.

explainLevels:
  - level: "eli5"
    label: "Like I'm 5"
    text: |
      To get treasure out of a treasure chest, you first have to buy the key.
      Glycolysis is like that. Your cell takes a piece of sugar and, before it can get
      any energy out, it has to spend a little energy first — like paying for the key.
      Then it breaks the sugar into two pieces and gets more energy back than it paid,
      so it ends up ahead. Your muscles do this super fast when you run, which is how
      you can sprint even before you catch your breath.
  - level: "highschool"
    label: "High school"
    text: |
      Glycolysis breaks one glucose into two pyruvate in ten steps in the cytoplasm —
      no oxygen needed. The trick is that the cell has to spend ATP before it can earn
      ATP. In the first three steps it uses up 2 ATP to prime the sugar. Then the
      six-carbon sugar gets cut in half into two three-carbon pieces, and from that
      point everything happens twice. The second half of the pathway makes 4 ATP and 2
      NADH. Subtract the 2 you spent, and the cell nets 2 ATP and 2 NADH per glucose.
      It's not a lot of energy, but it's fast and works without oxygen — which is
      exactly why your muscles rely on it during a hard sprint.
  - level: "college"
    label: "College"
    text: |
      Glycolysis converts one glucose (6C) to two pyruvate (3C) through ten cytoplasmic
      steps, net +2 ATP and +2 NADH. It splits into an investment phase and a payoff
      phase. Investment (steps 1–3): hexokinase and PFK-1 each spend one ATP, so by step
      5 the running total is −2 ATP. Aldolase (step 4) cleaves fructose-1,6-bisphosphate
      into DHAP and GAP; TPI funnels DHAP into GAP, so from step 6 on every yield is
      doubled. Payoff (steps 6–10): GAPDH (step 6) is the lone oxidation, reducing NAD+
      to NADH and generating a high-energy acyl-phosphate; phosphoglycerate kinase (step
      7) and pyruvate kinase (step 10) then make ATP by substrate-level phosphorylation.
      Each fires twice, for +4 ATP and +2 NADH. Three steps are effectively irreversible
      (large −ΔG) and therefore the regulatory sites: hexokinase, PFK-1 (the committed
      step), and pyruvate kinase. PFK-1 is allosterically inhibited by ATP and citrate
      and activated by AMP and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate, so the throttle responds to
      energy charge rather than substrate supply.
  - level: "grad"
    label: "Graduate"
    text: |
      The two-phase architecture of glycolysis is a thermodynamic design, not an
      accident. The preparatory phase invests 2 ATP to trap and destabilize glucose
      (hexokinase's phosphorylation also exploits induced fit to exclude water and
      prevent futile ATP hydrolysis), positioning fructose-1,6-bisphosphate for
      symmetric aldol cleavage. GAPDH (step 6) is the mechanistic crux: it couples an
      exergonic aldehyde oxidation to an endergonic acyl-phosphate formation through a
      covalent thioester intermediate on an active-site cysteine, conserving the
      oxidation free energy as 1,3-BPG, a compound with high phosphoryl-transfer
      potential. That stored potential is cashed at PGK and at PK, where enolase first
      traps PEP as a high-energy enol tautomer. Flux control resides at the three
      reactions held far from equilibrium (HK, PFK-1, PK); the displacement from
      equilibrium is precisely what confers controllability, since near-equilibrium
      reactions cannot be regulated unidirectionally. PFK-1 integrates energy charge
      (ATP/AMP), biosynthetic status (citrate), and hormonal state via
      fructose-2,6-bisphosphate, itself set by the bifunctional PFK2/FBPase2 under PKA
      control. The deeper systems-level constraint is conservation of the NAD+/NADH
      couple: with a fixed cofactor pool, glycolytic flux is hostage to NAD+
      regeneration, which is why pyruvate disposal (fermentation, the malate–aspartate
      or glycerol-phosphate shuttles, or full oxidation) sets the ceiling on anaerobic
      throughput. The Warburg phenotype — aerobic glycolysis sustained for biosynthetic
      intermediate supply rather than ATP yield — is the canonical illustration that
      flux purpose, not bioenergetic necessity, can govern pathway use.

**When Usain Bolt runs 100 meters in under ten seconds, his leg muscles burn
glucose faster than his blood can deliver oxygen.** So his cells do the next-best
thing: they break glucose down without it. That pathway is glycolysis, and it is
running in nearly every cell on Earth right now — in your neurons, in brewer's
yeast, in bacteria that have never met oxygen.

The puzzle that makes glycolysis worth studying is hiding in plain sight. The
cell's whole point is to *make* ATP. Yet the first thing glycolysis does is *spend*
it. Two ATP go in before a single one comes out. Why would a starving cell pay a
toll to enter a pathway whose job is to earn?

## The idea: invest first, then collect

Think of glycolysis as a small business that has to spend money to make money. The
ten steps fall into two phases.

The **investment phase** (steps 1–3) primes the glucose molecule. Hexokinase spends
one ATP to phosphorylate glucose, and phosphofructokinase-1 spends a second. By the
end of step 5 the ledger reads **−2 ATP, nothing earned**. The cell has put down a
deposit and seen no return.

Then the molecule is cut in half. Aldolase splits the six-carbon sugar into two
three-carbon fragments, and triose phosphate isomerase converts one of them into a
second copy of the other — so two identical three-carbon molecules now head down the
rest of the path. From here, everything happens twice. That doubling is the single
fact students most often miss, so hold onto it: every yield from step 6 onward
counts double.

The **payoff phase** (steps 6–10) collects. One oxidation step (GAPDH) banks the
energy as NADH and primes a high-energy phosphate. Two later steps — phosphoglycerate
kinase and pyruvate kinase — hand that phosphate directly to ADP, making ATP by what's
called *substrate-level phosphorylation*: no mitochondrion, no oxygen, no membrane
gradient, just one enzyme moving a phosphate from substrate to ADP. Each of those two
steps fires twice, so the payoff phase earns **+4 ATP and +2 NADH**.

Subtract the deposit from the return and the books balance:

> **Net per glucose: +2 ATP, +2 NADH.**

It is a thin margin. Two ATP is a rounding error next to the roughly thirty that full
respiration extracts later. But glycolysis is fast, it needs no oxygen, and for some
cells it is the *only* option. A red blood cell has no mitochondria at all; glycolysis
is its entire energy economy. Walk through all ten steps below and watch the ledger
build.

## The three locks: where the cell installs its throttle

Most of the ten steps are reversible — they sit near equilibrium and run in whichever
direction the cell needs. Three do not. Hexokinase (step 1), PFK-1 (step 3), and
pyruvate kinase (step 10) each release a large amount of free energy (ΔG roughly −33,
−22, and −17 kJ/mol), which makes them effectively one-way doors.

That irreversibility is not a flaw. It is the whole point. **A reaction near
equilibrium can't be controlled — nudge it and it slides right back. A reaction far
from equilibrium can be throttled, because the cell can hold the door shut.** So the
three irreversible steps are exactly the three the cell regulates.

The committed step is PFK-1, and it carries a beautiful paradox. ATP is its substrate
— and also its inhibitor. When ATP is high, the cell doesn't need more, so ATP binds a
second site and shuts PFK-1 down. When ATP runs low, its breakdown product AMP rises
and switches the enzyme back on. The enzyme isn't asking "is there glucose?" It's
asking "does this cell need energy right now?" That is feedback control written in
protein.

The same enzyme is wired differently in different tissues, which is the deeper lesson.
Muscle PFK-1 listens to the muscle's own energy charge — local, immediate, "I need ATP
now." Liver PFK-1 listens to hormones from the pancreas instead: insulin says store
glucose, glucagon says release it. Same protein, opposite job, because the tissue's
purpose differs. Enzyme identity does not determine regulation; context does.

## Why pyruvate, not glucose, is the real bottleneck

Here is the part that surprises people. The thing that can stall glycolysis isn't
running out of glucose. It's running out of **NAD+**.

Step 6 consumes NAD+ and produces NADH. But the cell holds only a small, fixed pool of
NAD+, and it must be recycled or the pathway grinds to a halt within seconds. So
pyruvate's fate is really about regenerating that NAD+. A sprinting muscle does it by
handing the electrons to pyruvate itself, making lactate and recovering NAD+ in one
step. Yeast does the same thing by making ethanol — which is convenient for brewers but
incidental to the yeast, who care only about their NAD+.

This is also why lactate gets a bad reputation it doesn't deserve. It is not a fatigue
toxin. It regenerates NAD+ so you can keep running, it travels to the liver to be
rebuilt into glucose (the Cori cycle), and it even acts as a signal. The burn in your
legs is not the lactate. The lactate is the rescue.

## How we measure it

The net yield was *counted*, not assumed — by labeling one carbon of glucose with a
heavy or radioactive isotope and tracing where it lands. That tracing proved the
six-carbon sugar splits into two three-carbon halves and that both halves run the
payoff phase. The same trick, scaled up to a PET scanner, reads glucose uptake in a
living tumor: cancer cells run glycolysis hard even when oxygen is plentiful, and an
¹⁸F-labeled glucose analog lights them up. To find the throttles, you assay the
regulated enzymes directly — add ATP, AMP, or citrate to purified PFK-1 one at a time
and watch the velocity climb or fall. That is how the committed step gave up its
secrets: the same initial-velocity discipline from enzyme kinetics, turned on one
enzyme at a time.
