summary: "Fatty acid synthesis turns a fed cell's spare acetyl CoA into long-chain fat, and it is deliberately not beta-oxidation run backward. Three differences make that possible: the work happens in the cytoplasm on a dedicated carrier (ACP), it spends NADPH instead of banking NADH, and a single committed enzyme (acetyl CoA carboxylase) decides whether any fat gets made at all. Trace the carbon from citrate to palmitate and the whole pathway resolves into one stoichiometric ledger: 8 acetyl CoA, 7 ATP, and 14 NADPH per C16 chain."

    dates: "1911-1979"
    contribution: "Determined the thioester structure of acetyl-CoA (acetate clamped to a sulfur atom) and showed fatty acids are built on a dedicated cytoplasmic assembly, fatty acid synthase, welding activated acetyl units onto a growing chain two carbons at a time rather than running beta-oxidation in reverse. Shared the 1964 Nobel with Bloch."
    profileSourceAnchor: "The Molecule Hunters (v10) — Feodor Lynen profile, Part 10 (quotes grep-confirmed)."
    quotes:
      - "Euch macht ihr's leicht, mir macht ihr's schwer"
    story: "Working in patched-together rooms in bombed-flat postwar Munich in 1951, Lynen took a boiled yeast extract and worked out how acetate rides on coenzyme A: not on the pantothenic acid as others guessed, but clamped to a sulfur atom as a high-energy thioester, giving acetyl-CoA, the busiest small molecule in metabolism, a structure you could draw, in a barely-one-page paper to Angewandte Chemie. He then showed the cell builds fat on its own route (FAS), not by reversing degradation, and traced the same scraps up to cholesterol, finding the committed step at HMG-CoA reductase, later the statin target. He earned his doctorate under Heinrich Wieland on death-cap mushroom toxins and married Wieland's daughter Eva, becoming Germany's first Professor of Biochemistry in 1953. At the 1964 Stockholm banquet he borrowed Hans Sachs from Wagner's Die Meistersinger, \"you make it easy for yourselves, you make it hard for me,\" when given, as he put it, a poor man too much honor."
  - name: "Konrad Emil Bloch"
    dates: "1912-2000"
    contribution: "Established that animal cells build cholesterol from two-carbon acetate, the same acetyl building blocks used in fatty acid synthesis, and mapped intermediates such as squalene along the more-than-thirty-step route. Shared the 1964 Nobel with Lynen."
    profileSourceAnchor: "The Molecule Hunters (v10) — Konrad Emil Bloch profile, Part 10 (quotes grep-confirmed)."
    quotes:
      - "When I arrived"
      - "it turned out that the station had a single dogfish in the aquarium"
    story: "With David Rittenberg, Bloch fed animals deuterium-labeled acetate and found the heavy label inside their cholesterol, proof that animals synthesize the sterol from a two-carbon fragment. Chasing squalene as an intermediate, he traveled to the Bermuda Biological Station to inject labeled acetate into dogfish (sharks pool squalene in their livers), but the single available dogfish died in the tank and he returned empty-handed. The irony: had it lived, the experiment would still have failed, because the shark's enormous squalene background would have drowned the labeled signal. He kept the idea, dropped the shark, and in 1952 fed radioactive acetate to rats with cold squalene as carrier, recovering radioactive squalene and nailing it as a true intermediate on the path from acetate to cholesterol."

    dates: "1927-2004"
    contribution: "Showed that aspirin works by irreversibly inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX), the enzyme that converts arachidonate into prostaglandins and thromboxanes, the biochemical basis of the eicosanoid and NSAID material. Also discovered prostacyclin. Shared the 1982 Nobel with Bergstrom and Samuelsson."
    profileSourceAnchor: "The Molecule Hunters (v10) — John Vane profile, Part 8 (quotes grep-confirmed verbatim)."

    story: "In May 1971, drafting a review, Vane realized prostaglandins released during lung trauma were synthesized on the spot, not stored. He prepared a cell-free soup of guinea-pig lung, added arachidonic acid, and watched aspirin shut down prostaglandin output, pinning the action on cyclooxygenase and explaining in one mechanism aspirin's effects on pain, inflammation, fever, and platelet clumping. The three-page Nature New Biology paper gave seventy years of empirical medicine a molecular basis. Every aspirin tablet acetylates a serine in the COX active site, the irreversible handcuff Vane characterized."

    dates: "1898-1941"
    contribution: "Pioneered stable-isotope tracing (deuterium, nitrogen-15) with mass spectrometry, proving body constituents including fatty acids and cholesterol are continuously torn down and rebuilt, the dynamic state of body constituents, which is the conceptual and methodological foundation for studying lipid turnover and synthesis."
    profileSourceAnchor: "The Molecule Hunters (v10) — Rudolf Schoenheimer profile, Part 2 (quote grep-confirmed)."
    quotes:

    story: "Across the hall from Harold Urey, deuterium's discoverer, at Columbia, Schoenheimer recruited physical chemist David Rittenberg to thread non-radioactive heavy isotopes into biomolecules and track them by mass spectrometry. Feeding rats nitrogen-15-tagged amino acids, he found that within hours the label scattered across the entire protein pool, overturning the wear-and-tear model and showing tissue is under perpetual demolition and rebuilding. He pushed the same deuterium method onto fatty acids and cholesterol, finding every major biomolecule class in constant flux. Konrad Bloch finished his training in Schoenheimer's lab. techniques:
  - name: "Radioisotope and stable-isotope tracing"
    blurb: "Feed an animal acetate labeled with deuterium or carbon-14 and find the label inside its fat and cholesterol. This is how Schoenheimer showed body lipids are in constant turnover and how Bloch proved animals build sterols from two-carbon acetate. The same logic, run with a labeled bicarbonate, demonstrates that the CO2 added by ACC is the one released during condensation, never incorporated into the chain."
  - name: "Avidin-biotin affinity capture"
    blurb: "Egg-white avidin binds biotin with a dissociation constant near 10 to the minus 15 molar, one of the tightest non-covalent interactions known. Biochemists exploit it to pull any biotin-tagged molecule cleanly out of a mixture. Historically the same binding starved rats fed raw egg white of their biotin, the 1927 Wisconsin observation that revealed biotin as a vitamin and, later, as ACC's CO2 carrier."
  - name: "Enzyme assay of a regulated polymer"
    blurb: "ACC is active only as a long filament and inactive as a dissociated dimer. Adding citrate to the purified enzyme drives polymerization and switches it on; adding palmitoyl CoA depolymerizes it and switches it off. Watching velocity rise and fall as you titrate one effector at a time is how the allosteric controls on the committed step were mapped."

  - stem: "Fatty acid synthesis is often contrasted with beta-oxidation. Which set of three features correctly describes synthesis (not degradation)?"
    choices:
      - "Cytoplasm, CoA carrier, FADH2 produced"
      - "Mitochondrion, ACP carrier, NADPH consumed"
      - "Cytoplasm, ACP carrier, NADPH consumed"
      - "Mitochondrion, CoA carrier, NADH produced"
    answer: 2
    rationale: "Synthesis occurs in the cytoplasm, carries intermediates on ACP via a phosphopantetheine arm, and consumes NADPH as its reducing equivalent. The option naming the mitochondrion with CoA and NADH describes beta-oxidation. The other two mix features from both pathways, which is exactly the confusion the chapter is built to prevent."
  - stem: "Which reaction is the committed step of fatty acid synthesis, and what cofactor does the enzyme require?"
    choices:
      - "Acetyl CoA carboxylase making malonyl CoA; requires biotin"
      - "Ketoacyl synthase condensing acetyl and malonyl; requires biotin"
      - "ATP-citrate lyase cleaving citrate; requires NADPH"
      - "Thioesterase releasing palmitate; requires CoA"
    answer: 0
    rationale: "Acetyl CoA carboxylase (ACC) carboxylates acetyl CoA to malonyl CoA using a biotin cofactor and one ATP; once malonyl CoA is made, the carbon is committed to fat, making this the committed step. ATP-citrate lyase is the upstream export step, thioesterase is the release step, and ketoacyl synthase performs the condensation but is not the committed regulatory point."
  - stem: "A student claims the CO2 added by ACC ends up incorporated into the carbon skeleton of palmitate. Why is this incorrect?"
    choices:
      - "CO2 is incorporated, but only into the terminal carboxyl group"
      - "The CO2 is released again during the condensation step; its decarboxylation drives the otherwise uphill reaction forward"
      - "CO2 is never added by ACC; ACC adds a hydroxyl group"
      - "The CO2 is reduced to a methyl group by NADPH"
    answer: 1
    rationale: "The CO2 attached to make malonyl CoA is released during condensation, and that decarboxylation is the thermodynamic driving force that makes joining two units favorable. CO2 is a leaving group, not a building block (the vending-machine quarter). It is not incorporated, not reduced to methyl, and ACC does add CO2 rather than a hydroxyl."
  - stem: "Per molecule of palmitate (C16) synthesized from acetyl CoA, how many NADPH and ATP are consumed?"
    choices:
      - "16 NADPH and 8 ATP"
      - "8 NADPH and 7 ATP"
      - "7 NADPH and 14 ATP"
      - "14 NADPH and 7 ATP"
    answer: 3
    rationale: "The full stoichiometry is 8 acetyl CoA + 7 ATP + 14 NADPH + 7 HCO3⁻ yielding palmitate + 7 CO2 + 8 CoA + 6 H2O. Fourteen NADPH come from the two reduction steps across seven CRDR cycles (two per cycle), and seven ATP are spent making the seven malonyl CoA units at ACC. The other options swap or mis-scale the two currencies."
  - stem: "Malonyl CoA does more than feed chain elongation. What is its key regulatory action that enforces reciprocal control between fat synthesis and fat breakdown?"
    choices:
      - "It inhibits CPT-I, blocking fatty acid entry into the mitochondrion for oxidation"
      - "It depolymerizes ACC, switching synthesis off"
      - "It activates cytochrome b5 on the ER"
      - "It activates AMPK, slowing synthesis"
    answer: 0
    rationale: "Malonyl CoA inhibits carnitine palmitoyltransferase I (CPT-I), the gate for importing fatty acids into mitochondria for beta-oxidation. So the moment the cell commits to synthesis it blocks oxidation, the reciprocal see-saw. Palmitoyl CoA (not malonyl CoA) depolymerizes ACC; AMPK is activated by low energy charge, not by malonyl CoA; and cytochrome b5 serves desaturases, unrelated to this control."

    choices:

      - "AMPK is activated, ACC is phosphorylated and inhibited, so fatty acid synthesis falls and oxidation rises"
      - "PP2A is activated, ACC is dephosphorylated and activated, so fatty acid synthesis rises"

    answer: 1
    rationale: "A higher AMP:ATP ratio activates AMPK, which phosphorylates and inactivates ACC.   - stem: "A genetic defect abolishes cytoplasmic malic enzyme activity in hepatocytes, while the pentose phosphate pathway is intact. The cell still imports citrate normally. What is the most direct metabolic consequence for fatty acid synthesis?"
    choices:
      - "Acetyl CoA can no longer be exported from the mitochondrion"
      - "The committed step (ACC) can no longer carboxylate acetyl CoA"

explainLevels:
  - level: "eli5"
    label: "Like I'm 5"
    text: "When you eat more food than your body needs right now, your body doesn't throw the extra away. It saves it as fat, like packing snacks for later. It builds the fat by snapping together tiny two-piece blocks, over and over, until it has a long chain. It even has a little helper arm that swings the growing chain from one tool to the next, like a robot on an assembly line. And your body is careful: it never builds fat and burns fat at the same time, because that would be like filling a bathtub while it's draining."
  - level: "highschool"
    label: "High school"
    text: "Fatty acid synthesis is how a well-fed cell turns extra food energy into fat. It happens in the cytoplasm and is not just the breakdown pathway run backward. First the cell ships acetyl CoA out of the mitochondria disguised as citrate. Then a key enzyme, ACC, adds CO2 to make malonyl CoA, which is the committed step: once you make it, you're making fat. A big multi-tool enzyme called fatty acid synthase then runs a four-step cycle (condense, reduce, dehydrate, reduce) seven times, adding two carbons each round, until it builds a sixteen-carbon fatty acid called palmitate. It uses NADPH as the power source, not the NADH the breakdown pathway makes."
  - level: "college"
    label: "College"
    text: "Fatty acid synthesis builds palmitate from acetyl CoA in the cytoplasm and differs from beta-oxidation in three ways: location (cytoplasm vs mitochondrion), carrier (ACP-phosphopantetheine vs CoA), and reducing equivalent (NADPH consumed vs NADH/FADH2 produced), which permits independent regulation. Stage 1: the citrate shuttle exports acetyl CoA (ATP-citrate lyase) and, via cytoplasmic MDH plus malic enzyme, generates 8 of the 14 required NADPH; the pentose phosphate pathway supplies the rest. Stage 2: acetyl CoA carboxylase, a biotin enzyme, carboxylates acetyl CoA to malonyl CoA at the cost of one ATP, the committed step. Stage 3: fatty acid synthase runs the CRDR cycle (condensation driven by decarboxylation of the malonyl unit, NADPH reduction, dehydration, NADPH reduction) seven times to reach C16. Net: 8 acetyl CoA + 7 ATP + 14 NADPH + 7 HCO3⁻ yield palmitate + 7 CO2 + 8 CoA + 6 H2O. ACC is regulated by phosphorylation (AMPK off, PP2A on), allostery (citrate on, palmitoyl CoA off), and hormones (insulin on, glucagon off), and its product malonyl CoA inhibits CPT-I to block simultaneous oxidation."
  - level: "grad"
    label: "Graduate"
    text: "The architecture of fatty acid synthesis is a study in compartmentalized metabolic control. The cytoplasm/mitochondrion split, the ACP/CoA carrier distinction, and the NADPH/NADH currency separation together insulate anabolic flux from catabolic flux, eliminating a thermodynamically wasteful futile cycle and permitting opposed regulation. The committed reaction, ACC, is the integration node: its biotin-dependent carboxylation is reversibly inactivated by AMPK phosphorylation and reactivated by PP2A, allosterically polymerized into active filaments by citrate and depolymerized by long-chain acyl-CoA feedback, and hormonally set by the insulin/glucagon axis through these same effectors. The energetic logic of chain elongation is the malonyl decarboxylation: direct Claisen condensation of two acetyl-CoA is endergonic, so the cell invests ATP in a carboxylation whose CO2 is subsequently lost during condensation, coupling an exergonic decarboxylation to the C-C bond formation, with ACP's phosphopantetheine tether enforcing substrate channeling across the FAS dimer. The reciprocal CPT-I inhibition by malonyl CoA (notably via ACC2) hardwires the synthesis/oxidation switch, and the AMPK node explains metformin's pleiotropic effect: Complex I inhibition raises AMP:ATP, activating AMPK to suppress lipogenesis and gluconeogenesis while derepressing fatty acid oxidation. The stereochemical inversion (D-3-hydroxyacyl in synthesis vs L in oxidation) and the NADPH dependence are mechanistic fingerprints confirming the two pathways are non-superimposable, not mirror images."

**One cookie. One slice of pizza. If you don't burn it, your body turns it into fat using exactly this pathway.** Not a vague metaphor for "fat" but the literal chemistry, running in your liver and fat cells whenever you eat more than you spend. The carbon in that cookie becomes acetyl CoA, and acetyl CoA, in the fed state, gets stitched into long fatty acid chains two carbons at a time.

Here is the trap to avoid from the first sentence. You have already learned beta-oxidation, the pathway that *takes fat apart*, and it is tempting to assume synthesis just runs it backward. It does not. Synthesis is a separate machine in a separate place, and the differences are the whole point.

## Why it is not beta-oxidation in reverse

Three differences set the two pathways apart, and each one earns its keep.

**Location.** Degradation happens in the mitochondrion. Synthesis happens in the cytoplasm. The cell physically separates the factory from the demolition crew.

**Carrier.** Beta-oxidation carries its intermediates on coenzyme A. Synthesis carries them on a dedicated acyl carrier protein, ACP, using a long phosphopantetheine arm that swings the growing chain from one active site to the next without ever letting it float free.

**Reducing power.** Degradation *produces* the high-energy carriers NADH and FADH2. Synthesis *consumes* NADPH. That single letter, the P on NADPH, is the cell's accounting trick: it keeps a separate currency for building and for burning so the two never get confused.

Why bother with all this separation? Because it lets the cell regulate the two directions independently and avoid a futile cycle, the wasteful spin of building and tearing down the same molecule at the same time. Keep that idea close. It is the spine of the whole chapter.

## Stage 1: getting the carbon out, with reducing power for free

Acetyl CoA is made in the mitochondrion, but synthesis happens in the cytoplasm, and acetyl CoA cannot cross the mitochondrial membrane. So the cell sends it across in disguise.

When fuel is plentiful, citrate from the TCA cycle is exported to the cytoplasm. There, **ATP-citrate lyase** cleaves it back into acetyl CoA and oxaloacetate. Think of citrate as an Uber for acetyl groups: it carries the passenger across a membrane the passenger could not cross alone.

The leftover oxaloacetate then does something clever. It is reduced to malate, and malic enzyme decarboxylates malate to pyruvate, generating NADPH on the way out. So the citrate shuttle is a two-for-one: it delivers the carbon *and* it makes reducing power. Eight of the fourteen NADPH a palmitate needs come from this shuttle; the pentose phosphate pathway supplies the other six.

## Stage 2: the committed step

Once the acetyl CoA is in the cytoplasm, **acetyl CoA carboxylase (ACC)** makes the decision that matters. This is the committed step, the one Chris flags as the exam goldmine, and it does one thing: it attaches CO2 to acetyl CoA to make **malonyl CoA**, spending one ATP.

ACC carries a biotin cofactor, a vitamin tethered to a lysine that acts as a CO2 taxi, picking up carbon dioxide at one active site and dropping it at another. It is the same mechanism pyruvate carboxylase uses, so you have seen it before.

Biotin's grip on this story has a vivid history. In 1927, researchers in Wisconsin fed rats raw egg white and watched them sicken. The culprit was avidin, an egg-white protein that binds biotin so tightly (a dissociation constant near 10 to the minus 15 molar) that it sequesters the vitamin and starves the enzymes that need it. That accident is how biotin was discovered.

Once you make malonyl CoA, you are committed to making fat. That single sentence is worth memorizing.

## Stage 3: the assembly line, two carbons at a time

The actual chain is built by **fatty acid synthase (FAS)**, in mammals a single giant polypeptide folded into a homodimer that carries every catalytic activity it needs: a condensing enzyme, a couple of reductases, a dehydratase, a transferase, and the swinging ACP arm. It is a one-stop assembly line.

The chain grows by a four-step cycle, and the mnemonic is **CRDR**:

- **Condensation** joins an acetyl group and a malonyl group into a four-carbon beta-ketoacyl chain, releasing CO2.
- **Reduction** uses NADPH to turn the keto group into a hydroxyl.
- **Dehydration** removes water, leaving a double bond.
- **Reduction** uses a second NADPH to saturate that double bond.

After cycle one you have butyryl-ACP, four carbons. The cycle then repeats, adding two carbons each turn, seven turns in all, until the chain reaches sixteen carbons and a thioesterase clips it free as **palmitate**.

The cleverest piece is the condensation. Two acetyl groups cannot simply join, because that reaction is uphill and will not go on its own. So the cell first spends an ATP to bolt CO2 onto acetyl CoA, making malonyl CoA, and then releases that same CO2 during condensation. The decarboxylation pulls the reaction forward. **CO2 is a leaving group here, not a building block.** It is the quarter you drop into a vending machine: you do not get the quarter back, but it makes the snack come out. (One more tell that synthesis and degradation are different machines: synthesis makes D-hydroxyacyl intermediates, while beta-oxidation runs through the L-isomers.)

Count it all up and the ledger is clean:

> **8 acetyl CoA + 7 ATP + 14 NADPH + 7 HCO3⁻ → palmitate + 7 CO2 + 8 CoA + 6 H2O.**

## After palmitate, and why some fats are essential

FAS stops at sixteen carbons. Longer chains and any double bonds are added afterward on the endoplasmic reticulum: elongases extend the chain, and desaturases (using O2, NADH, and cytochrome b5) install double bonds. Mammals can make a double bond at carbon 9 but lack the enzymes to place one past it, at the omega-6 and omega-3 positions. That gap is why **linoleate and linolenate are essential fatty acids** you must eat. Burr and Burr proved it in 1929 by raising rats on fat-free diets and watching them fail. Downstream, arachidonate becomes the eicosanoids: cyclooxygenase makes prostaglandins and thromboxanes, and aspirin works by permanently acetylating that enzyme, which is why a single dose thins your blood for a week.

## Regulation: the see-saw between building and burning

ACC is controlled three ways, and they all converge on one idea: do not build fat and burn fat at the same time.

**Phosphorylation.** AMPK, the cell's low-fuel sensor, phosphorylates ACC and shuts it off. PP2A removes the phosphate and turns it on. **Allostery.** Citrate (abundant fuel) makes ACC polymerize into active filaments; palmitoyl CoA (the end product) depolymerizes them, a clean feedback brake. **Hormones.** Insulin activates ACC; glucagon and epinephrine inhibit it.

The reciprocal piece is the elegant part. Malonyl CoA, the product of ACC, also blocks CPT-I, the gate that lets fat *into* the mitochondrion for burning. So the moment the cell commits to synthesis, it slams the door on oxidation. This is why metformin works in diabetes: it inhibits Complex I, the AMP-to-ATP ratio climbs, AMPK switches on, synthesis falls, and oxidation rises. (Metformin descends from the French lilac, a folk remedy for high blood sugar.)
