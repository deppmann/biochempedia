summary: >-
  How do you pull one protein out of a soup of ten thousand others — and then
  prove you got it? You need a way to find it (an assay), a way to enrich it
  (centrifugation and chromatography), and a way to read its mass, charge, and
  sequence. This lesson follows a single enzyme through five purification steps,
  watches its specific activity climb three-thousandfold, and ends with the
  methods — SDS-PAGE, immunoassays, Edman degradation, mass spectrometry — that
  turn a purified band into an identity.

estMinutes: 24
objectives:
  - "Explain why every purification needs an assay, and use specific activity (units/mg) to judge whether a step worked."
  - "Match the four chromatographic principles — solubility, size, charge, and binding affinity — to what you know about a target protein."
  - "Read a purification table: why total protein and total activity both fall while specific activity and fold-purification climb."
  - "Trace a protein from a gel band to a confirmed identity using SDS-PAGE, immunoassays, Edman degradation, and MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry."

techniques:
  - name: "The enzyme assay"
    blurb: >-
      You cannot purify what you cannot find. An assay reports how much of your
      target is present at each step — for lactate dehydrogenase, the NADH coenzyme
      it consumes absorbs at 340 nm, so a spectrophotometer reads activity straight
      off the falling line.
  - name: "Specific activity and the purification table"
    blurb: >-
      Specific activity = enzyme units divided by total protein (units/mg). It
      rises every time a step removes contaminant without destroying your target,
      so the column of specific-activity numbers — not the raw activity — tells you
      whether the purification is actually working.
  - name: "Peptide mass fingerprinting"
    blurb: >-
      Cut a protein with trypsin into a predictable set of peptides, measure their
      masses by MALDI-TOF, and match that list against the in-silico digest of every
      protein in a database. The pattern of peptide masses identifies the protein
      the way a fingerprint identifies a person.

practiceQuestions:
  - stem: "During a four-step purification, the specific activity (units/mg) at each step is: crude lysate = 12, ammonium sulfate cut = 40, ion exchange = 220, affinity column = 4,800. What is the overall fold-purification?"
    choices:
      - "120-fold"
      - "400-fold"
      - "40-fold"
      - "4,800-fold"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      Fold-purification = final specific activity ÷ initial specific activity =
      4,800 ÷ 12 = 400-fold. Choice 2 (40-fold) divides the final value by an
      intermediate step rather than the starting one; choice 3 (4,800-fold) mistakes
      the final specific activity itself for the fold; choice 0 (120-fold) is
      unrelated. Specific activity is the success metric because it reports the
      proportion of target in total protein, independent of how much sample remains.

  - stem: "A protein mixture is loaded onto a gel-filtration (size-exclusion) column. Which protein elutes FIRST?"
    choices:
      - "The smallest protein, because it migrates fastest"
      - "The protein with the lowest pI, because it is most negatively charged"
      - "The largest protein, because it is excluded from the bead pores"
      - "The protein that binds the bead ligand most tightly"
    answer: 2
    rationale: >-
      Gel filtration separates by size in a counterintuitive way: large proteins are
      too big to enter the porous beads, so they travel only through the spaces
      between beads — a shorter, faster path — and elute first. Small proteins enter
      the pores, take a longer winding route, and elute last. Charge (choice 1) is
      irrelevant to size exclusion, and ligand binding (choice 3) describes affinity
      chromatography.

  - stem: "An enzyme has a pI of 5.0. You want it to bind an ion-exchange column at a running buffer pH of 8.0. Which resin, and why?"
    choices:
      - "A cation exchanger, because the protein is positively charged at pH 8.0"
      - "An anion exchanger, because the protein is negatively charged at pH 8.0"
      - "A cation exchanger, because pH 8.0 is above the pI"
      - "Either resin works, because pI does not affect binding"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      When the buffer pH (8.0) is above the protein's pI (5.0), the protein carries a
      net negative charge, and negative proteins bind a positively charged
      anion-exchange resin. The rule: pH > pI → protein negative → anion exchanger;
      pH < pI → protein positive → cation exchanger. Choices 0 and 2 reverse the
      charge logic, and choice 3 ignores the pH/pI relationship that governs binding.

  - stem: "In SDS-PAGE, why do proteins separate according to mass alone rather than by their intrinsic charge or shape?"
    choices:
      - "SDS adds a uniform negative charge roughly proportional to chain length and denatures the protein, giving a constant charge-to-mass ratio"
      - "SDS removes all charged side chains so only the backbone mass remains"
      - "The polyacrylamide gel binds proteins by their pI"
      - "Heating in SDS converts all proteins to the same molecular weight"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      SDS is an anionic detergent that denatures proteins into linear chains and
      coats them at roughly one SDS per two residues, adding negative charge in
      proportion to length. This swamps intrinsic charge and gives nearly the same
      charge-to-mass ratio for every protein, so the only variable affecting
      migration through the sieving gel is size — smaller proteins move faster.
      Choice 1 misstates the mechanism (side chains are not removed), choice 2
      describes isoelectric focusing, and choice 3 is biochemically false.

  - stem: "A 2D gel is run on a cell lysate before and after drug treatment. Afterward, one spot shifts to a more acidic position (lower pI) but stays at the same molecular weight. The most likely explanation is:"
    choices:
      - "The protein was cleaved into a smaller fragment"
      - "The protein underwent a post-translational modification such as phosphorylation"
      - "The protein was completely degraded"
      - "The gel was run at the wrong amperage"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      In 2D gels the first dimension separates by pI (isoelectric focusing) and the
      second by mass (SDS-PAGE). A spot that moves to a lower pI with no change in
      apparent mass signals the addition of negatively charged groups — classically
      phosphorylation, which adds phosphate. Cleavage or degradation (choices 0 and
      2) would change the apparent mass, and a gel artifact (choice 3) would not
      produce a specific, reproducible pI shift for one protein.

mcatQuestions:
  - stem: "A researcher purifying lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) tracks activity by monitoring pyruvate + NADH → lactate + NAD⁺. Which spectrophotometric measurement gives the cleanest readout of LDH activity?"
    choices:
      - "Increase in absorbance at 340 nm as NAD⁺ is reduced to NADH"
      - "Decrease in absorbance at 340 nm as NADH is oxidized to NAD⁺"
      - "Increase in absorbance at 280 nm from aromatic residues"
      - "Decrease in absorbance at 260 nm from nucleotide loss"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      NADH absorbs strongly at 340 nm whereas NAD⁺ does not. In the forward reaction
      (pyruvate + NADH → lactate + NAD⁺), NADH is consumed, so absorbance at 340 nm
      decreases over time — that drop is the direct kinetic readout of LDH activity.
      Choice 0 describes the reverse direction. Absorbance at 280 nm (choice 2)
      reports total protein, not turnover, and 260 nm (choice 3) is not a specific
      NADH/NAD⁺ discriminator.

  - stem: "A clinical lab screens serum for HIV with an ELISA, then runs a western blot on positives. What makes the western blot a useful CONFIRMATORY test after the ELISA screen?"
    choices:
      - "It quantifies antibody concentration more precisely than ELISA"
      - "It separates viral antigens by size before antibody detection, so reactivity can be checked against specific proteins of expected molecular weight"
      - "It uses no antibodies and therefore avoids cross-reactivity"
      - "It detects nucleic acid rather than protein, eliminating false positives"
    answer: 1
    rationale: >-
      The western blot runs antigens on SDS-PAGE (separation by mass), transfers them
      to a membrane, and probes with the patient's antibodies plus an
      enzyme-conjugated secondary antibody. Because reactive bands appear at the
      expected molecular weights of specific viral proteins, a true positive must
      light up bands at defined sizes — the size-resolved specificity that makes it
      confirmatory after a sensitive but less specific screen. Choice 0 reverses the
      strengths (ELISA is the quantitative workhorse), choice 2 is false (western
      blots rely on antibodies), and choice 3 describes a nucleic-acid test.

  - stem: "Edman degradation removes and identifies one residue per cycle from the N-terminus at about 98% efficiency per cycle. Roughly what fraction of correctly phased peptide remains after 50 cycles, and what limitation does this illustrate?"
    choices:
      - "About 36%, illustrating why Edman degradation is practically limited to roughly 50 residues before signal degrades"
      - "About 98%, illustrating that efficiency stays nearly constant regardless of length"
      - "About 90%, illustrating that the method works well for proteins of any size"
      - "About 2%, illustrating that no peptide longer than 10 residues can be sequenced"
    answer: 0
    rationale: >-
      Per-cycle yield compounds multiplicatively: 0.98⁵⁰ ≈ 0.36, about 36%. As signal
      from correctly phased chains falls and out-of-phase background accumulates,
      residue calls become ambiguous — which is why Edman degradation is capped near
      50 residues and longer proteins must be cleaved into peptides (e.g., by trypsin
      and chymotrypsin) and read in overlapping fragments. Choice 1 ignores
      compounding, choice 2 overestimates retained signal, and choice 3 (about 2%, the
      value near cycle 200) overstates the limitation.

      Ion exchange separates by charge, and the rule is just opposites attract. A
      cation exchanger carries negative beads and binds positive proteins — use it
      when the buffer pH is below your protein's pI. An anion exchanger carries
      positive beads and binds negative proteins — use it when pH is above the pI.
      Once bound, raise the salt or shift the pH and your protein lets go.

**Many of the clinical tests you will order as a physician run on the techniques
in this chapter.** The pregnancy test detects a hormone with an antibody. The
troponin test that confirms a heart attack does the same. The classic
confirmatory HIV test was a western blot. Strip away the brand names and the
plastic cartridges, and what remains is a small set of methods for finding one
protein in a crowd, pulling it out clean, and proving its identity. It starts
with a problem of numbers.

A single human cell holds billions of individual protein molecules, but they are
drawn from only about ten thousand distinct protein species. Across the whole
body, alternative splicing and post-translational modification expand the roughly
20,000 genes into a proteome of more than a million distinct protein forms. The
genome is the blueprint. The proteome is the building, lived in and constantly
remodeled. To study one protein, you have to get it out of that building alone.

## You cannot purify what you cannot find

Before the first spin, before the first column, you need an **assay** — a way to
detect your protein in a tube where you cannot see it. For an enzyme, the assay is
usually its own reaction. Lactate dehydrogenase converts pyruvate to lactate using
the coenzyme NADH, which is consumed as the reaction runs; NADH absorbs light at
340 nm and lactate does not, so the absorbance falls, and the slope of that fall
is the activity. The number that matters is **specific activity** — units of
activity divided by milligrams of total protein. Picture a jar of mixed marbles:
the count of red marbles is the activity, but the *fraction* that are red is the
specific activity. A good step throws away other-colored marbles without losing
red ones, so the fraction climbs. That single number guides every step that
follows.

## Four ways to separate a protein from its neighbors

First you have to get the proteins out of the cell. Break the cells open into a
homogenate, then spin. **Differential centrifugation** uses force as a sieve. A
gentle spin drops the heavy nuclei; crank it harder and mitochondria pellet;
harder still and the smallest vesicles go down, leaving cytosol in the final
supernatant. Each spin trades one mixture for two cleaner ones.

What centrifugation cannot finish, **chromatography** does, by exploiting four
different properties of your protein, one at a time.

The first is **solubility**. Add ammonium sulfate and proteins begin to drop out
of solution, but not all at once. Different proteins precipitate at different salt
concentrations, so a carefully chosen cut leaves many contaminants behind. A round
of dialysis through a semi-permeable membrane then washes the salt back out.

The second is **size**, and it runs backward from intuition. In gel filtration the
column is packed with porous beads. Large proteins are too big to enter the pores,
so they skip them and ride the short path between beads — and **elute first**.
Small proteins wander into the pores, take the long way around, and come off last.
It is the part students most often get backward.

The third is **charge**. An ion-exchange column carries fixed charges on its beads,
and opposite charges stick. The rule follows from the protein's pI: below its pI a
protein is net positive and binds a negative (cation-exchange) resin; above its pI
it is net negative and binds a positive (anion-exchange) resin. Raise the salt or
shift the pH and the protein lets go.

The fourth, and most powerful, is **binding affinity**. Decorate the beads with
something only your protein recognizes — a substrate analog, a cofactor, an
antibody, a metal that grips a His-tag — and your target binds while everything
else flows through. Then displace it with a flood of free ligand. So the choice is
simple: if you know a binding partner, run affinity; if you know the size, gel
filtration; if you know the pI, ion exchange; if you know nothing yet, salt it out
and start narrowing. HPLC speeds any of these up — fine beads under high pressure,
paid for with expensive plumbing.

## Reading the band: identity, sequence, mass

A pure band is not yet an answer. **SDS-PAGE** got you there by erasing every
difference except mass: the detergent SDS coats the chain at roughly one molecule
per two residues, giving every protein the same charge-to-mass ratio, so they sort
by size alone and the small ones run fastest. **Isoelectric focusing** sorts the
other way, letting proteins drift through a pH gradient until each stalls at the pH
where its net charge is zero — its pI. Run focusing first, then SDS-PAGE at a right
angle, and you get a **2D gel**: every protein becomes a spot placed by both pI and
mass. Compare a healthy proteome to a tumor's, and a spot that shifts or brightens
is a candidate biomarker.

When a protein has no enzymatic activity to assay — a hormone receptor, say — you
reach for **antibodies**. An antibody-coated bead pulls its target out of a mixture
(**immunoprecipitation**). Transfer an SDS-PAGE gel to a membrane, probe it with
antibodies, and light up one band by chemiluminescence (the **western blot**),
confirming both identity and size. Coat a plate instead and you have an **ELISA**,
the workhorse of the clinic, with signal proportional to concentration. The same
antibody-sandwich logic, in a strip-based lateral-flow format, is the engine inside
the pregnancy test and the COVID rapid test.

Identity is not sequence. To read the order of amino acids, **Edman degradation**
clips off the N-terminal residue, identifies it, and repeats. But each cycle is
only about 98% efficient, so after fifty rounds you are down near a third of your
signal, and the method stalls past roughly fifty residues. The modern answer is
**mass spectrometry**. MALDI-TOF flicks ionized peptides down a flight tube where
the heavier ones arrive later, weighing them to a fraction of a dalton. Cut your
protein with trypsin, weigh the pieces, and match the mass list against a database
— a peptide mass fingerprint. Push further with tandem MS, fragmenting one peptide
and reading sequence directly from the spacing between peaks. Frederick Sanger
spent twelve years reading the 51 amino acids of insulin by hand; today a
500-residue protein is sequenced in minutes.

## How we measure it in the lab

Every method here answers one of five questions about your protein: is it present,
how pure is it, did the last step help, how much did you keep, and is it still
intact. Specific activity answers the first four at a glance — the slope you watch
climb, step by step, from a smear to a single band. Mass spectrometry and Edman
degradation answer the question the table cannot: which protein, exactly, read off
one peptide mass or one residue at a time. Find it, enrich it, prove it. That is
protein biochemistry, and now it is a few hours of work instead of twelve years.
